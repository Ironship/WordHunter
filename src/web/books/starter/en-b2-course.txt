Chapter 1: The Listening Copy

The recording arrived on a Thursday morning, three weeks after the screening of The Quiet Mile. Dr Saira Bell sent it through a private archive link to Mae and Iris. The message described it as a listening copy, not permission to publish, forward, or use the sound.

Mae did not open it at once. She served breakfast, checked out a family in Room Six, and called a plumber about the upstairs shower. At eleven, she placed her laptop on the dining-room table.

Iris came from Redwater. Robin arrived from Eastmere with his yellow scarf folded in a bag. Nell walked over from the Northlight office. Leon Price joined them because the tape had belonged to his late brother Simon.

Kit offered to come, but Mae asked for the smallest group connected to the old band. Nell was there as Mae's friend, not as a producer or location coordinator. She left her Northlight phone at home.

The archive file began with thirty seconds of low noise. A man's voice named the date: 18 August, more than thirty years earlier. Then someone counted to four.

A guitar entered. Mae's nineteen-year-old voice followed it.

The sound was clearer than the one-minute practice tape in the green box. Mae sang about a late bus passing an empty square. A traveller saw a light above a blue door and knew that someone had waited. After the first verse, a second guitar joined. Ellis Hart sang a lower line beneath Mae. Simon played a short instrumental passage. June Bell's drums entered softly.

The track lasted four minutes and twelve seconds. At the end, Mae laughed and said, "The middle still falls over."

Ellis replied, "Then I will hold it up."

The recording stopped.

No one at the table spoke. The sounds of Lantern House returned around them: a pipe in the kitchen, a gull outside, someone moving a chair upstairs.

"That is my voice," Mae said at last. "I had forgotten how high I sang."

Robin wiped his glasses. He remembered the tune but had never heard this studio session. Leon recognised Simon's guitar. Iris had heard her mother sing only short lines at home.

Nell asked Mae how she felt. Mae shook her head.

"Every person in that song is young," she said. "Simon is alive. June is still speaking to me. Ellis sounds as if he has never entered a radio studio. I do not know where to put all of them."

They listened again. This time, Robin followed the structure. The first verse matched Mae's handwritten pages. The second verse was not in the pages June had returned. The instrumental middle sounded like Simon's later style. Ellis's lower line changed the final melody.

"Did you write the second verse?" Iris asked.

Mae could not remember. She knew some words felt like hers and others did not. Thirty years of silence had not preserved a perfect answer.

Leon pointed to a small sound before the second verse. Someone in the studio said, "Try Ellis's words." The voice might be Simon's, but it was distant.

Saira's message included the tape-box photographs. The main label read ELLIS - COAST SESSIONS - TRACK 6. On the back, a studio worker had typed HART/PRICE. Mae's name did not appear.

The omission hurt more after hearing her voice at the centre.

"I was not a guest singer," she said. "It was my song when we entered that room. Whatever happened inside it, I was not passing through."

She closed the laptop. They did not play the track a third time.

In the afternoon, Lantern House served tea as usual. Mae moved between tables while her younger voice remained silent inside the computer. The recording had answered one question: it existed. It had opened many others about the space between bringing a song into a room and carrying it out under somebody else's name.

After the last guest went upstairs, Mae opened the file alone. She did not invite Iris or ask Nell to stay. The first listening had belonged to a group reacting to a discovery. This one belonged to the person whose body had made the sound.

She wore headphones and kept the volume low. Without other faces to watch, she noticed details that had escaped her. At the end of the first verse, young Mae took a breath too late and hurried three words. Ellis adjusted his harmony to follow her. June struck the edge of a drum and immediately softened. Simon's guitar string made a small dry buzz.

The imperfections brought the session closer. The track was not a lost masterwork waiting intact for recognition. It was four teenagers in a paid room, trying to hold a piece together before their time ended.

One line surprised her. On her handwritten page, the traveller waited "where the old road meets the rain." On the tape, she sang "where the coast road meets the rain." She could not remember changing it. Ellis might have suggested the clearer place; Simon might have sung the wrong word during practice until it stayed. The alteration was too small for a credit and large enough to disturb her claim of perfect memory.

Mae paused before the second verse. She read Ellis's words in the archive transcript. They moved the traveller away from the blue door and towards a station. Mae liked one image despite herself: a timetable turning soft in a wet hand.

David had liked that line too. She remembered playing him the melody years after Low Tide, without the tape. He asked what happened to the wet timetable. Mae had told him it belonged to someone else's verse and stopped playing.

The memory hurt because David was not present to confirm it. She could hear his question in her mind with the same confidence Ellis heard "you can have it." Love did not make recollection exact.

Mae finished the track and wrote four headings on paper: WHAT I KNOW, WHAT I REMEMBER, WHAT OTHERS SAY, WHAT I FEEL. She placed the coast-road line under all but the first. She did not turn the page into a method for solving the song. It simply stopped different kinds of certainty from hiding under one word.

At midnight, she closed the archive link. The recording remained real when she was not listening. That simple fact was new. For decades, the song had depended on her willingness to remember it. Now an object outside her held one performance, and she had to learn how not to mistake survival for ownership.

Chapter 2: What the Tape Could Not Say

Saira visited Lantern House the following week. She worked at Northcross Sound Archive, where the tape had appeared in a collection donated by a retired studio engineer. The collection contained hundreds of sessions, many with incomplete labels.

She brought no original material. The tape remained in controlled archive storage. Instead, she carried a scan of the studio log and a written account of how the collection had arrived.

The log was a narrow sheet with six tracks listed. The first five belonged to an early Ellis Hart project. Track Six had no title, only HART/PRICE and a note: female guide vocal.

Mae read the phrase twice. "A guide vocal suggests I was showing Ellis how to sing it."

"That is one possible reading," Saira said. "It could also mean a temporary vocal they planned to replace. We do not know who wrote the note or when."

The archive had identified Mae because the engineer's collection included a recent card from Ellis. It referred to "Mae Chen's home-light melody" and asked whether the old session still existed. Ellis had sent the card after correcting his public account at the Morrowbay music night.

Saira had contacted him before cataloguing the tape. Ellis confirmed that Mae was the singer. He also said the session developed a Low Tide song into a track he and Simon later considered recording. He did not claim sole authorship, but his memory of the writing differed from Mae's.

"What does he remember?" Mae asked.

"He should tell you himself if you want that conversation. I do not want my summary to become his voice."

Saira explained what the archive could establish. The physical tape matched the period. The studio voice named a plausible date. The performance clearly involved Mae, Ellis, Simon, and June. The box and log credited only Hart and Price. None of that proved who wrote each part before or during the session.

Mae took her handwritten song pages from the green box. The paper showed the first verse, main melody, and final return. June had kept those pages for decades. Their age supported Mae's account, but the pages had no date.

Robin remembered seeing Mae write at school, yet memory was not a clock. He might be joining different rehearsals into one.

"People will ask for the true story," Iris said. "They will want a clear answer."

"Then they may be disappointed," Saira replied.

The archive wanted to catalogue the track with fuller names. It proposed this description:

Untitled Low Tide session, performed by Mae Chen, Ellis Hart, Simon Price, and June Bell. Songwriting credits unresolved. Previously catalogued as Hart/Price.

Mae liked seeing her name first because her voice began the recording. Leon asked why Simon came third when his name had been on the original box. Saira explained that performance order was not a judgment of importance. She could list the four names alphabetically instead.

They settled on alphabetical order for the public catalogue. The description would state that Mae sang the lead vocal and brought an earlier version of the song to the band. That second point came from several accounts but remained attributed, not presented as a physical fact.

Saira also wanted permission to make the listening copy available in the archive reading room. Mae was not ready. Leon could speak only for Simon's estate interests, not for the living performers. June and Ellis also needed to be involved.

"For now, keep it closed," Mae said. "A catalogue can say it exists. Hearing my young voice is different."

Saira accepted the distinction. Discovery did not automatically create public access.

After she left, Mae placed the scan beside her old pages. The documents disagreed without cancelling one another. One showed what she had written; another showed how a studio had named the result. Neither could record the whole conversation between four young people who thought they had more time.

Saira later sent a fuller provenance note. The retired engineer, Colin Ware, had bought the contents of the Coast studio when it closed. He stored labelled reels in dry cabinets but did not create a complete index. After moving to a smaller home, he donated the collection to Northcross.

Colin remembered Roy Denning visiting sessions but not Low Tide by name. He recognised Ellis's younger voice when the archive played a short approved identification extract. He did not remember Mae, which reporters might interpret as proof that she was unimportant in the room. In reality, Colin had recorded hundreds of singers and had forgotten most of them.

His lack of memory was not evidence against hers.

The archive checked the tape brand, reel leader, studio marks, and recording method against the period. Those details supported authenticity. They did not prove that the box stayed unchanged every year in Colin's cabinet. No written transfer list existed from the studio closure.

Leon worried that another reel might have been removed. Ellis wondered whether Roy took a finished version. Saira recorded both possibilities as unanswered, not likely facts.

Mae visited the archive store with Iris. Through a glass panel, she saw shelves of plain boxes. Her voice occupied only one narrow reel among thousands. The sight reduced and enlarged the discovery at once.

Saira showed them how the archive stored the digital copy separately from public catalogue information. Mae could restrict listening without pretending the object did not exist. She could also change her access decision later.

"What happens if I die before deciding?" Mae asked.

The question made Iris flinch. Saira explained that Mae could record present wishes and identify who should be consulted, but the future would still involve laws, other contributors, and archive responsibilities. She did not offer a promise beyond her authority.

Mae wrote a short instruction with her adviser. Iris would be her contact for personal voice access. The note did not make Iris owner of every creative question or give her control over June and Ellis's recorded performances.

On the train home, Iris said she hated receiving responsibility through a question about death. Mae apologised for asking it in front of Saira without warning.

The archive process had a way of turning family futures into fields and forms. They stopped for dinner before returning to Morrowbay and spoke about Iris's next theatre contract instead.

Chapter 3: Ellis at the Blue Door

Ellis asked to visit Mae in person. He did not want the first conversation to happen through lawyers, managers, or an archive email. Mae agreed on the condition that his assistant did not come inside and no one recorded them.

He arrived with Mara, his wife, on a quiet Monday. Lantern House had only two guests, both out for the day. Iris and Leon joined Mae. Nell waited in the kitchen at Mae's request. She could be called if the conversation became too difficult, but she would not sit as an audience.

Ellis looked older than he had at the music night. His white hair was shorter, and he moved carefully around Robin's chair. Mara Hart carried a paper folder but left it closed.

Mae made tea. For several minutes, they spoke about the weather and the train. Then Ellis looked towards the music cupboard.

"I heard the track yesterday," he said. "Saira sent me the same listening copy."

"And?"

"You were right. It began as your song. I knew that before, but the tape leaves no room to soften it."

Mae did not thank him. "What part do you think became yours?"

Ellis remembered writing the second verse in the studio waiting room. Simon created the instrumental middle. June changed the rhythm after the first take. Mae changed the final melody in response to Ellis's lower harmony.

He believed the four of them had turned her song into a shared band piece that day. Mae remembered bringing a nearly complete song and accepting suggestions for one recording. She did not remember agreeing to shared ownership.

"You said we could have it," Ellis said. "After the last take."

"Have what? The tape? The song? Permission to play it next week?"

"I thought you meant the song."

"I was nineteen and angry. The band had already decided you would sing it instead of me."

Ellis remembered an argument but placed it days later. He said Mae left before they made a final recording. Mae said the decision to replace her had made her leave.

The difference was not a small date. It changed the meaning of every choice around it.

Leon asked what Simon had believed. Ellis opened Mara's folder and took out a copy of a letter. Simon had written to him twelve years after Low Tide ended. One line said, We never did right by Mae over Home Light, but perhaps silence is kinder now.

Leon had never seen the letter. He read it slowly. It did not say what they had done wrong. It could refer to the missing credit, replacing Mae's voice, ending the band, or all of them.

"Why did you keep this?" he asked.

"Because Simon died before I answered it," Ellis said.

Mara Hart spoke for the first time. "Ellis has not recorded or released the song. The later song with four similar notes is a separate issue, and he corrected that credit. We came because this tape changes what may be possible now."

Mae heard the careful public language. "Possible for whom?"

Ellis wanted the full session preserved. He also wanted to record a new version with Mae if she ever chose. He did not want a dispute to become the only public form of the song.

Mae almost laughed. "The song had no public form because your name was on the box and mine was not. Now that my voice has returned, you want us to sing together."

Ellis accepted the anger without defending his invitation. He said it could remain only an idea.

When the meeting ended, no agreement had been reached. They had heard each other's memories directly, which was both better and harder than receiving them through Saira.

Nell came in after Ellis left. Mae stood by the blue door, watching him walk towards Market Square.

"He is not lying," she said. "That would be easier. He remembers a version in which he was careless, not cruel. I remember being pushed out. Both versions let each of us sleep at night."

Leon had not left with Ellis. He stood in the sitting room holding the copy of Simon's letter. Mae invited him into the kitchen, where the private disagreement could continue away from the front windows.

"Simon knew something was wrong," Leon said. "That line proves it."

Mae read the line again. "It proves he used the words 'not right.' It does not tell us which part he meant."

Leon wanted his brother's regret to carry weight. Mae understood; she wanted her own early pages to carry weight too. Yet both of them risked making a dead man's short sentence perform work he had not completed while alive.

"He could have written to me," Mae said.

"Perhaps he was ashamed."

"Perhaps. Shame explains silence after the person finally speaks. Before that, it looks exactly like silence."

Leon folded the letter along an old line. He did not defend Simon again. He asked whether Mae wanted a copy. She did, but only after Ellis confirmed that the original could be copied. The letter had passed between the brothers; finding her name inside did not make the paper hers.

That evening, Ellis and Mara took the train home. Ellis told his wife that Mae's anger felt larger in Lantern House than it had at nineteen because he could see the life built around it.

Mara asked whether he still believed the song had become shared. He said yes, musically. He no longer believed that creative contribution had given the others permission to remove Mae's name or voice.

"Those are separate questions," Mara said.

"I spent thirty years making them one because it gave me a defence."

He wrote the first draft of his letter to Mae on the train, then deleted it. An immediate apology risked becoming another attempt to control the end of their meeting. He waited for the archive process instead.

At Lantern House, Nell stayed while Mae checked the evening keys. She did not ask whether Ellis deserved forgiveness. Mae had not offered it, and the question would turn her next feeling into a public measure.

"When Beatrice's notebook came back, we wanted the finding to end the incident," Nell said. "It only ended the search."

Mae understood the comparison. The tape had returned her voice. Its return did not settle who had carried the song away, why no one came back, or what each person owed now.

Before bed, she moved Robin's old chair nearer the piano. Ellis had sat in it during the meeting. The chair did not retain his side of the argument. It became a chair again as soon as Mae placed a guest newspaper on it.

Chapter 4: June's Envelope

June Bell lived in Port Amber, a coastal city several hours north. She had stopped playing drums in her twenties and worked for many years as a school librarian. After returning the missing song pages through Robin, she had kept her distance.

Robin wrote to tell her about the archive tape. He did not include the sound file. June replied by post to Mae, not to the archive.

The envelope contained a twelve-page letter, two photographs, and photocopies from June's old diary. She apologised for writing so much after years of silence.

Her diary entry for 18 August was brief:

Coast room with M, E, S. Home song finally holds together. E wrote new train verse. S cut the middle in half. M furious after. She said take the stupid thing and walked to station. I stayed. Coward.

The words supported parts of both memories. Ellis had written a verse. Simon had changed the middle. Mae had said "take the stupid thing," not "you can have it." June remembered the station, suggesting the argument happened on the recording day.

Yet a private diary was not a perfect transcript. June had written it that night or perhaps days later; she could not now remember. The single letter M might mean Mae, and the date matched the tape, but the phrase "home song" was only June's description.

In her letter, June explained the argument as she remembered it. The studio engineer liked Mae's voice, but a manager connected to Ellis said the band needed a male lead to interest record companies. Ellis did not make the original suggestion. He also did not refuse it. Simon supported him. June stayed quiet because she feared losing the session.

Mae had left. The remaining three tried the song with Ellis singing, but none of those takes appeared in the archive collection. Low Tide ended within weeks.

June kept Mae's handwritten pages because she found them under a drum case. She told herself she was protecting them. In truth, she did not contact Mae because the pages reminded her of her silence.

Mae read the letter alone first. Then she invited Iris, Robin, and Nell. Leon received a separate copy of the lines about Simon with June's permission.

Iris was angry on her mother's behalf. "They chose the person who looked easier to sell. That is the story."

"It is part of the story," Mae said. "I also told them to take it."

"After they pushed you out."

"Yes. But if I make my angry sentence mean nothing, I am also changing the day."

Mae did not believe that a nineteen-year-old's angry words transferred a song for all time. She did believe they affected what the others thought they could do. The difference mattered to her, even if a headline would not hold it.

June offered to come to Morrowbay. She did not expect forgiveness and would accept no meeting if Mae preferred. Mae wrote back that she wanted to see her, but not yet. First, she wanted the archive to examine the diary copies and tape dates without taking the original diary from June.

Saira compared paper, ink photographs, and known events only as far as the archive could. She found no reason to doubt the diary's age, while making clear that its meaning remained June's account.

The public catalogue gained another line: A contemporary diary attributed changes to all four band members and recorded a dispute over the intended lead singer.

The sentence was accurate and lifeless. It could not show June sitting behind her drums while Mae walked out, or Mae reading the words three decades later at the table where actors once practised a fictional argument.

Robin felt responsible for bringing June's letter into the story. He had taught all four musicians and believed he should have noticed the shift towards Ellis before the studio day. Mae rejected the idea that a teacher could supervise choices made after school forever.

"You gave us a room and locked the door at six," she said. "You were not Roy's manager."

Robin still remembered praising Ellis for speaking to event organisers. He had called it leadership without asking whether decisions returned to the group. That language may have reinforced a pattern, though no single compliment caused the later exclusion.

Mae allowed Asha to record Robin as background research if the project proceeded. She did not want him inserted as the wise adult who had always known her talent. He had valued her music and missed important dynamics, both ordinary truths.

June's letter included an apology to Robin for using him as a safe middle person. She had addressed the returned song pages to him because sending them directly to Mae would require facing whether Mae wanted contact.

Mae wrote her reply over three nights. The first version listed every moment June had failed her. The second rushed towards forgiveness. She sent neither.

The final letter said:

I believe that you remember staying because you wanted the session. I am angry that protecting the song mattered more to you than protecting me. I also know we were young and the choice did not look as large from inside that day. I want to see you, but I do not promise what we will become afterwards.

June answered with travel dates, not another apology. That restraint helped Mae trust the meeting more.

Saira asked whether the archive could retain copies of the correspondence. Both women said no. The letters informed their current decisions but were not historical band records simply because they mentioned the tape.

Asha later knew that Mae and June had exchanged letters, but not the exact private wording. She could describe the contact only if both women chose to discuss it in interviews.

The boundary frustrated Mara, who saw the letters as a bridge between past and present. Asha defended the absence. A documentary did not become honest by collecting every object that would improve its structure.

Mae stored June's letter in her own flat, not the green box at Lantern House. The distinction mattered. Low Tide history and a new private relationship had touched, but they did not need the same cupboard.

Chapter 5: A Catalogue Goes Live

Northcross Sound Archive published its updated catalogue in June. The entry did not include audio. It named all four performers, described the uncertain songwriting, and noted that access was restricted while rights and personal permissions were discussed.

Music researchers regularly searched the archive site for Ellis Hart's name. Within a day, a small music blog found the entry. Its first headline read:

LOST ELLIS HART TRACK REVEALS UNKNOWN FEMALE SINGER

After readers pointed out that Mae was named, the blog changed "unknown female singer" to "Morrowbay songwriter." It did not contact Mae before publishing.

A larger entertainment site copied the story. Its headline became:

DID RADIO STAR ELLIS HART STEAL HIS FIRST HIT?

The track had never been a hit or even released. The site confused it with Ellis's later song that used four similar notes. By the end of the day, two different pieces of music had become one simple scandal.

Lantern House received calls from reporters. One booked a room online under his own name but wrote in the request box that he hoped to interview Mae at breakfast. Mae cancelled the booking under the clear condition that the house was not available as a reporting location and refunded the payment.

Another reporter waited in Market Square. He did not enter the property, but he called questions when Mae went to the flower shop. Tom brought her through the shop's back office until the man left.

Mae hated being hidden in her own street. She also disliked the public picture of Ellis as a famous thief and herself as a nameless victim. Ellis had benefited from a system and from his own silence. That did not turn every uncertain memory into proof of deliberate theft.

The inaccurate headline spread faster than the archive correction. Saira added a public note: the session was unreleased, and the archive entry did not establish legal or sole creative ownership. Ellis's representatives released a statement acknowledging Mae's lead vocal and original melody while describing later contributions as collaborative.

Iris thought the statement protected Ellis too carefully.

"He still cannot say, 'Mae wrote the song,'" she said.

"Because he believes he wrote part of it," Mae replied.

"He can believe that and still say your name first."

Mae issued no immediate statement. She wrote three versions and disliked all of them. One sounded angry enough to satisfy strangers. One sounded forgiving when she was not. The third sounded like an archive label.

Nell stayed away from Northlight's publicity staff. Offering professional help without Mae asking could turn friendship into management. Instead, she covered a breakfast shift so Mae could spend a day at Iris's flat in Redwater.

Farah organised food without attaching a supportive message that reporters could photograph. Jamie checked the blue door after someone pushed a microphone through the letter opening. Kit made a plain inner cover so mail could still enter without leaving a direct gap into the hall.

After three days, Mae published four sentences on the Lantern House website:

I sang and brought the original song to Low Tide. Other members made contributions whose exact extent we remember differently. The surviving recording was never released, and it is not an Ellis Hart hit. I am working privately with the people and archive involved. Lantern House guests are not part of this matter and should be left in peace.

The statement did not end the story. It gave Mae a voice inside it without forcing her to settle every fact at once.

The business consequences lasted longer than the headlines' first wave. Three genuine guests cancelled because they feared cameras outside. Two others booked precisely because they wanted to see where Low Tide had played. Mae declined one reservation whose message described the stay as a "song investigation weekend."

Rosa created a separate record of disrupted bookings without preserving reporters' unnecessary personal details. Lantern House needed to understand its losses, but it did not need a collection of strangers' phone numbers after the matter ended.

A music fan sent flowers every day for a week. The first bunch came with a respectful card. The fourth asked Mae to sign a record. She asked Tom's shop to stop accepting further orders in her name. Tom refunded the unsent flowers to the buyer rather than turning the display into local publicity.

Farah faced a different request at Harbour Kitchen. A customer wanted a "Stolen Song" cake for an online video. Farah refused the wording and did not tell Mae until later. She was running a bakery, not a defence team, but she could decide which joke her work helped spread.

Jamie found two people photographing Lantern House from the pavement. They were in a public place and left when asked not to block the door. He resisted the urge to argue. A public street did not become private because the attention was unpleasant.

Mae reopened online bookings with a clear notice that guest information would never be discussed with media and that booking did not provide access to private interviews. The notice sounded colder than her usual welcome, but it protected the welcome from being used as an opening.

Ellis's radio programme devoted thirty seconds to correcting the false claim that the track had been released. He named Mae and the other performers. He did not play audio. Some listeners accused him of avoiding the real issue; others assumed the correction settled it.

Mae heard the clip once. Ellis's professional voice was calm, exact, and familiar to thousands. Her own four-sentence statement looked small beside it. That difference was part of the power between them. He knew how to occupy public sound without appearing to demand space.

Iris wanted Mae to record a video. Mae refused. Copying Ellis's form would not create equal power; it would make her perform a skill she had not chosen to build.

Instead, she asked Saira to make the archive catalogue easy to find beneath searches for the false headline. The factual record could stand without Mae turning herself into a daily public speaker.

Within two weeks, most reporters moved to another story. The inner door cover Kit had made stayed in place. It improved the winter draught, an ordinary benefit left by an extraordinary intrusion.

Chapter 6: The Story Others Wanted

The headlines created offers. A morning television programme wanted Mae and Ellis to appear together. A newspaper offered payment for June's diary pages. Three podcast producers proposed series about "stolen songs." One had already designed cover art showing a broken guitar.

Mae refused all of them.

The least aggressive approach came from Asha Reed, an independent audio editor who had worked with Northlight on film sound. She did not pitch a title. She wrote that any project should begin only if Mae, Ellis, June, and Leon believed a shared account could add something beyond the archive record.

Mara Voss also saw a possible Northlight documentary. The company had never made a full audio series, but it wanted to expand beyond film. Mara asked Nell whether she might help explore it.

Nell's immediate answer was no. She knew Mae too closely and had been present at private conversations. Turning those memories into production material would repeat the problem Daniel faced when he heard Beatrice's garden story.

Mara accepted the refusal but asked whether Northlight could approach Mae independently through Iris. Nell said Mae chose her own contact route; Northlight did not need Nell's permission. She would, however, stay out of internal planning if the approach happened.

At home, Nell told Mae about the conversation before a formal offer arrived. Mae was not angry. She had already read Asha's letter and was considering whether a careful recording could prevent false versions from becoming permanent.

"If I say nothing, the theft story stays," Mae said. "If I speak, people will cut one angry line and use that instead."

"A longer project cannot guarantee fair listening," Nell replied. "It can only make room for more than one line."

Mae asked whether Nell would trust Asha. Nell knew her work but not her well. Asha had edited interviews for The Quiet Mile without turning Beatrice's path day into a story of complete repair. That was evidence of judgment, not a promise.

Northlight sent a written exploration offer. It proposed two paid research weeks with no commitment to publish. Asha would lead editorial work. Each participant could decide which personal materials to show. No recording would be released without a later, separate production agreement. The archive would remain independent.

Mae asked Saira whether working with Northlight would harm the archive process. Saira said the archive would not endorse a documentary, but it could provide the same factual access to any serious researcher under its rules.

Ellis agreed to the research stage. June had not yet answered. Leon wanted Simon represented but did not want a living person to speak as if they knew every thought of the dead.

Iris supported the idea only if Mae had her own adviser. Mae reminded her that support could become control, echoing Beatrice and Elin without knowing their private conversation.

"I am not asking you to protect me from every bad sentence," Mae said. "I am asking you to tell me when I sound unlike myself."

Iris accepted the narrower role.

Nell remained outside the production team. Yet Mae asked her to attend the first meeting as a friend. Nell agreed on one condition of her own: anything she heard there would not enter Northlight records through her.

The story others wanted already had a villain, victim, secret tape, and public confrontation. The story Mae might choose to tell had four young musicians, unequal power, uncertain memory, a damaging silence, and a song that none of them had carried alone in quite the same way.

Mae took Northlight's offer to David's grave on the anniversary of his death. She did not believe he could answer from the stone. She went because their marriage had been the place where the old song remained known but unrecorded.

David had encouraged her to contact Ellis once his radio career grew. Mae refused, and he respected the refusal. Years later, during an argument unrelated to music, he accused her of using Low Tide as proof that any public ambition would betray her. He apologised, but the sentence stayed.

Mae had excluded this from the project boundaries because it belonged to a marriage with no simple witness. A documentary might use David as the supportive husband who believed her or the person who challenged her retreat. He had been both and much more.

At the grave, Mae read Asha's letter again. A longer account could keep David mostly outside while acknowledging that silence had not meant the song was forgotten. It could also bring reporters back to Lantern House and turn June's apology into material.

She realised that she was not deciding between privacy and publicity. The catalogue was already public, the clip did not yet exist, and false stories had begun. Her choice concerned what kind of record she would help create, not whether any record existed.

Back at Lantern House, Mae asked Rosa what the staff needed if a production began. Rosa wanted one contact person for schedule changes and no filming during breakfast. She also wanted to know whether staff would be expected to keep June's visits secret.

Mae said no staff member should be asked to lie. Personal guests would not be announced, but if public attention made privacy impossible, the visit location would change.

Iris asked the same practical question about herself. Would every disagreement with her mother become possible content? Mae said no, then recognised that she could not promise what Iris might choose to say. They would need separate boundaries.

The offer slowly became less like a grand decision and more like a set of relationships that required room. Mae agreed to the research stage because it did not force publication. She did not agree because Northlight was trusted forever or because speaking was morally braver than remaining private.

She wrote to Asha: I am willing to explore a series that keeps uncertainty visible and does not make Lantern House the scene of a crime. That sentence became the starting point of the first meeting.

Chapter 7: Research Without a Title

The first research meeting took place in a hired room at Sea Glass Theatre, not at Lantern House. Mae did not want her home and business to become the project's natural set.

Asha Reed came with one notebook and no recording equipment. She was in her early forties, with a calm voice that did not turn soft when people disagreed. Mara attended for Northlight. Mae, Iris, Leon, and Nell sat across from them.

Asha began by stating what the meeting was not. It was not an interview, not an attempt to secure dramatic promises, and not proof that a series would be made. She wanted to identify the questions each person believed mattered and the harms a project could create.

Mae's first question was how a young woman's original work disappeared from the label. Leon wanted to know what Simon had contributed and why he later expressed regret. Iris wanted the gendered decision about the lead singer stated plainly. Mara wanted the difference between evidence and memory to remain audible rather than being smoothed away by a narrator.

Nell did not add a question. She was not one of the musicians or an intended producer.

Asha asked what should remain outside. Mae named Iris's childhood and her late husband's private response to the band years. Leon excluded details of Simon's final illness and family life. They agreed that Lantern House guests, staff records, and addresses had no place in the research.

The group also discussed the song itself. A documentary about authorship would naturally want to play it. The archive listening copy did not give Northlight that right. Any use required separate agreement from relevant parties and the archive source.

"Can a series work if the audience never hears the track?" Mara asked.

"Perhaps," Asha said. "Silence might show what is unresolved. But we should not call that an artistic choice if it is simply the limit people set."

June joined by telephone for the last half hour. She had decided to come to Morrowbay in July. She would meet Mae privately before speaking to any production. Asha did not ask her to record a remote interview first.

Ellis chose not to attend this meeting because Mae wanted the first questions formed without his public profile filling the room. He sent one written concern: the project must distinguish the unreleased Low Tide session from his later credited song.

At the end, Asha read her notes aloud. Anyone could correct a misunderstanding. Iris changed "Mae lost control of the song" to "Mae was excluded from decisions after the session." The first sounded passive and complete; the second named an action and a period.

Northlight would spend the next two weeks building a private timeline from shared documents. Asha would interview no one yet. She would mark contradictions rather than choosing the most dramatic version.

Outside the theatre, Mara asked Nell once more whether she was certain about staying away from the team.

"Yes," Nell said. "Mae should never have to wonder whether dinner with me is research."

It was the right answer, but it carried a loss. Nell had learned enough about production to want to help shape a difficult story. Choosing not to use that skill was also a form of responsibility.

After the meeting, Asha asked Nell to stay for ten minutes as a potential source, not a producer. Nell had received the blue postcard, watched the first green box open, and seen the song return during the power cut. Those events might be necessary to explain how the archive story began.

Nell felt the conflict immediately. She had excluded herself from making the documentary, but appearing inside it could still shape Mae's story through her memories.

"Ask Mae first whether she wants my part included," she said. "Then send me the exact period you want to discuss."

Mae allowed the limited conversation. She did not want Nell describing private reactions after the first listening or Ellis meeting. The origin path from postcard to music night was already shared history among them and had appeared in public event accounts.

Asha interviewed Nell for forty minutes without a production agreement because it was background research only and would not be used as audio. Nell described the rain-softened postcard, Leon's absence under the clock, and the dinner by candlelight when Robin first played the tune.

Her memory of the power cut differed from Mae's. Nell remembered Leon naming the song before Mae sat at the piano. Mae remembered recognising Robin's first notes and choosing the music herself. Robin could not place the order.

Asha marked the sequence uncertain. For a documentary, the dramatic version would have Leon break the silence and Mae reclaim it. Real memory offered no clean cue.

Nell also admitted that Lantern House's first job offer and Mae's song had become connected in her mind because they happened during the same weeks. She sometimes treated Mae's trust in her as part of the song's return. That meaning belonged to Nell's life, not necessarily Mae's.

The conversation reinforced her choice to remain outside the team. A producer could easily turn personal connection into narrative logic and forget that the link existed only for one witness.

Asha wrote a short source note. It used Nell's account to locate events but did not promise her a role. If the final series needed the postcard story, a narrator could establish it from multiple records.

Nell left the theatre with mixed relief. Her knowledge had been useful without making her useful everywhere. The boundary was not silence. It was a defined doorway through which only part of her memory had passed.

Chapter 8: The First Timeline

Asha built the timeline in layers. The top line held dates supported by physical records. The next held diary entries and letters. Below that, separate colours showed each person's present memory. Gaps remained white.

The earliest evidence was Mae's handwritten page, which probably came from her final school year but had no date. Robin remembered hearing the tune in the school music room. June's diary placed the Coast studio session on 18 August. The tape voice confirmed the same date.

After the session, certainty weakened. June's diary mentioned Mae leaving for the station. A newspaper listed Low Tide at a summer hall show five days later, but no member could remember whether Mae played. A blurred photograph showed three or perhaps four people on stage.

The band ended before September. Ellis and Simon continued working together. A studio log from October listed Home Light under Hart/Price, but the related tape was missing. No evidence showed that version being released.

Twelve years later, Simon wrote the regretful letter. Much later, Ellis used four notes resembling Mae's melody in another song. He had recently corrected that credit. Then the Coast tape emerged.

Asha sent the private timeline to each participant separately. She did not allow one person's comments to overwrite another's. Mae marked the August show as "I think I was not there." Ellis marked it "Mae played first half, left before second." June wrote "cannot remember." Leon had no direct memory.

The conflicting entries stayed side by side.

Iris found the uncertainty frustrating. "Somebody was on the stage. There must be an answer."

"There was an answer that night," Mae said. "That does not mean we can reach it now."

One new fact came from Tom at the flower shop. His late father had photographed summer events and kept negatives. Tom remembered a Low Tide envelope in a cupboard. He offered to look, but Asha did not take the family archive away. She gave him the date and a way to contact her if he found relevant images.

Tom discovered three negatives a week later. A photographic specialist made careful scans. The stage image clearly showed Ellis, Simon, and June. The fourth position held an empty microphone. Mae had not performed when that picture was taken.

Ellis amended his memory. He might have confused the summer hall with an earlier show. He did not try to preserve his version against the image.

The empty microphone was powerful. Mara imagined it as the documentary cover. Asha refused to choose artwork during research. The image proved only that Mae was absent at one captured moment, not why she left or what the empty place meant.

Tom allowed the project to study the scan but not publish it yet. His father's other negatives contained private local events and remained outside the research.

The timeline became more accurate without becoming complete. Every new object narrowed one part and complicated another. The tape had brought Mae's voice back. The photograph showed her absence. Between those two records lay the argument no machine had captured.

Tom's negatives created their own problem of sequence. The three frames showed the band during one song, but the envelope did not identify which. The empty microphone might have been Mae's, a spare, or used by another performer earlier that evening.

Asha located the summer hall stage plan in a local newspaper archive. Low Tide normally placed Mae on the left, where the empty stand appeared. Robin recognised Simon's jacket and June's drum decoration. The image strongly supported Mae's absence but could not prove she had never played another part of the show.

Ellis's memory of her performing the first half remained possible. Mae's belief that she was not there remained possible too. The timeline changed from ABSENT FROM SHOW to ABSENT IN THREE SURVIVING IMAGES.

Mara worried that such wording would exhaust listeners. Asha argued that the project could state the practical conclusion more naturally: no surviving photograph showed Mae on stage that night, while memories differed about whether she appeared earlier.

Tom also found the photographer's pocket diary. On the date, his father wrote LOW TIDE 3 ONLY - GIRL GONE? The note supported the idea that the band had appeared as three. The question mark showed he did not know why.

Mae disliked being reduced to "girl," but the word revealed the photographer's perspective. The archive quote would identify it as his wording, not repeat it in narration without context.

Tom permitted one low-resolution research scan. Publication would require a later agreement and credit to his father's collection. He did not want the negative leaving the flower-shop safe.

Nell remembered Kit's blue boat display based on the Low Tide record cover. For years, the town had preserved an image of four band members while the last-show photograph held three. Neither picture told a full story, but together they showed how public memory could freeze one version and lose another.

Asha placed both images on the private timeline. The documentary was audio, so listeners might never see them. Still, visual evidence shaped which questions the editor asked.

The empty microphone remained tempting. Everyone could project exclusion, regret, or absence onto it. Asha wrote a warning beside the scan: Powerful image. Limited fact.

That note guided more than the photograph. Every strong object in the story needed its factual edge written nearby before metaphor carried it away.

Chapter 9: Conditions for a Story

At the end of the research period, Asha believed a four-part audio series was possible. She did not say the material demanded to be made. Recordings and letters could exist without becoming content. The choice remained with the living people whose voices would carry most of the story.

Northlight offered separate participation agreements to Mae, Ellis, June, and Leon. Each person could obtain independent advice. Payment was for time and agreed material access, not for giving a particular version or emotional response.

Mae read the draft with Iris and her chosen adviser. One section allowed Northlight to edit interviews for length and clarity. That was necessary for any finished series, but it also meant Mae could speak for two hours and hear ten minutes in public.

Asha proposed an additional process. Participants could hear passages where their voice had been edited to create a new sequence or placed beside a direct contradiction. They could correct factual mistakes and raise serious context concerns. They would not control every editorial choice or remove an uncomfortable statement merely because they later disliked it.

Mae found the distinction hard but fair. A documentary could not be four separate personal statements with no editor. At the same time, editorial independence should not become a polite phrase for surprising people with the shape of their own words.

Ellis's representative asked for approval over the final series. Northlight refused. Ellis himself withdrew that request after speaking to Asha. He wanted a clear correction route, not control over Mae's account.

June asked whether she could stop after her first interview. Yes. Material already recorded under the agreement might still be used, subject to the stated process, but no one would force a second session. She chose to begin with one interview and decide later.

Leon faced a different problem. He could provide Simon's letter and speak about finding the green box, but he could not give consent on behalf of Simon's private thoughts. The production would describe what Simon wrote and did, not invent an inner confession around him.

The song recording remained outside the agreement. Northlight might later seek permission for short extracts. The documentary could be planned without assuming access.

Mae added one personal condition. No interview would take place at Lantern House while guests were present. The dining room had held the old band, her business, and her daily life. She did not want microphones turning every familiar object into evidence.

Sea Glass Theatre offered a rehearsal room. Northlight paid the normal hire rate. The room had plain walls, comfortable chairs, and no connection to Mae's teenage home.

Before signing, Mae asked Nell to read the practical schedule, not the editorial terms. Nell checked dates against Lantern House bookings and Iris's work. She did not advise Mae on rights or outcomes.

"You are being very careful not to become useful," Mae said.

"I am trying to be useful only where you asked."

Mae signed. Ellis and Leon signed the same week. June waited until she reached Morrowbay and met Mae. The story had conditions now, but still no recorded voice.

Northlight's finance manager then asked whether the research payment should be lower for Leon because he would provide fewer interview hours. Mara rejected the assumption. Leon's time included reviewing family material, consulting the estate adviser, and carrying the emotional burden of representing a person who could not speak. Hours in front of a microphone were not the only work the project consumed.

Mae's adviser noticed that the draft described the archive track as the "central work." Mae asked for "central recording" instead. Calling it the work risked treating one collaborative session as the complete song and pushing her earlier composition outside the frame.

Asha accepted the change. The distinction also helped Ellis, whose second verse belonged to the recorded version rather than Mae's first solo song. Precise language did not always favour one side.

The group spent longer on withdrawal than anyone expected. If a participant left after recording, Northlight might have built episodes around their voice. Automatic removal could make the series impossible and distort what others had said. Automatic retention could trap someone in a project whose context had changed.

They agreed that Northlight would explain what material had been recorded and how far editing had gone before deciding with the participant what could reasonably remain. Serious safety or privacy concerns would carry more weight than ordinary regret. No rule could predict every future conflict, so the process named responsibility rather than promising a simple result.

Iris disliked the uncertainty. Mae preferred it to a false guarantee that any person could erase every copy at any moment.

"You are agreeing to trust Asha," Iris said.

"Partly," Mae replied. "I am also agreeing to a process for when trust becomes difficult."

The final papers filled thirty pages, but Mae's decision rested on three practical truths. Research could end without publication. The old audio remained separately controlled. Her interview would not happen at home.

She wrote those points on a card and placed it inside the green box. Not because the full agreement did not matter, but because she wanted the purpose of her yes visible beside the materials that had led to it.

When June eventually signed, she added her own card: One interview first. No diary original. Private day with Mae before recording.

The cards were not legal replacements. They were the human-sized promises each woman expected the larger system to remember.

Chapter 10: Mae's Chair

Asha placed two identical chairs in the Sea Glass rehearsal room. One was for Mae; the other was for herself. The microphone stood between them but slightly to the side, so it did not feel like a third face.

Iris waited in the theatre cafe. Mae wanted her nearby but not listening. Nell stayed at Northlight. The interview belonged to Asha's team now.

They began with Lantern House, not Low Tide. Mae spoke about buying the guesthouse with her late husband, David, raising Iris in the rooms above the kitchen, and learning that hospitality required limits as much as welcome.

Only then did Asha ask about music. Mae described the school practice room where she wrote the first tune. She remembered rain against a high window and a bus she could see from the desk. The traveller and blue door were invented, although they later seemed to predict Lantern House.

"Did you think of yourself as a songwriter?" Asha asked.

"I thought I had written a song. That felt different. A songwriter writes another one."

Low Tide began because Ellis wanted a band for the summer hall. Simon had a guitar and knew how to make every tune louder. June owned drums but nowhere to play them. Mae had the music-room key because Robin trusted her to lock up.

At first, nobody had a leader. Then people outside the group began speaking mostly to Ellis. He was confident, handsome, and able to explain the band in one sentence. Mae let him because she disliked talking to adults who called the music "promising."

The pattern seemed harmless until decisions arrived through him.

Mae described the Coast session. She had entered believing they would record her song with her voice. A manager called Roy Denning came for the last hour. He had no formal contract with the band, but Ellis admired him. Roy said a male lead would give the track a clearer market.

"I remember Ellis looking at the floor," Mae said. "For years, I remembered him agreeing. The tape in my head made the silence sound like yes. Perhaps silence was what happened. It still served the same purpose."

Asha did not ask Mae to forgive or accuse him. She asked what Mae did next.

Mae threw down the lyric pages. June's diary said she told them to take the stupid thing. Mae remembered saying worse. She walked to the station, found she had missed the train, and sat outside for almost an hour. David, then only a friend, collected her in his old car.

She never asked for the tape. Ellis did not call. June sent one short message that Mae ignored. Within a month, Mae moved to a training course in another town. Music became something she had once done.

"Why did you stay silent when Ellis became known?" Asha asked.

Mae rejected the word silent at first. She had told David, Robin, and later Iris. She had not addressed the public because there had been no public release of her song. Ellis's later four-note phrase bothered her, but four notes seemed too small to fight over and too personal to hear by chance.

"I built a life that did not need him to admit anything," she said. "That was not weakness. But I also made the song so small that Iris never heard the whole of it. That part was fear."

After two hours, Asha ended before Mae became exhausted. She saved the most detailed authorship questions for another day.

Mae sat alone for five minutes after the microphone was off. Then she carried her own chair back to the wall, although the theatre staff had said she could leave it.

Chapter 11: Ellis Without a Stage

Ellis chose to record at the archive in Northcross. He knew the building but not the interview room. His wife, Mara, waited outside, just as Iris had for Mae.

Asha used the same kind of chairs and the same opening question about life outside the band. Ellis spoke about radio work, music programmes, and years of interviewing artists more famous than he was. He had built a career by helping other people explain themselves.

"That makes this seat uncomfortable," he said. "I know what an editor can do with a pause."

"So do I," Asha replied. "We will not pretend pauses have one fixed meaning."

Ellis remembered Low Tide as his first experience of being listened to. At school, teachers called him charming when they meant unfocused. On stage, charm became useful. Roy Denning was the first adult to suggest it could become work.

He described Mae as the most musical member. She heard when a song had too many notes and removed them without making it empty. Ellis brought energy and words. Simon built arrangements. June held everyone together until she could not.

His account of the session matched June's on the second verse. He wrote it in the waiting room after Mae said the first verse needed another place. He remembered showing her the lines. She changed "last train" back to "late bus" because the rhythm was better.

Mae did not remember that exchange. Her handwritten pages contained only the first verse, so physical evidence could not settle it.

Ellis admitted wanting the lead vocal when Roy suggested it. He believed the band would make two versions, one with Mae and one with him. When Mae became angry, he thought she was rejecting the band rather than the vocal decision.

"That belief was convenient," he said. "It meant I did not have to run after her."

Asha asked about Mae's words when she left.

"I heard, 'You can have the stupid thing.' June heard 'take.' I built a whole permission around the difference between two short verbs. I cannot defend that now."

Why, then, did the box say Hart/Price? Ellis said Roy prepared a later session for him and Simon. They intended to record a new lead, but Ellis could not make the song feel right. He did not write the label and did not correct it. He was nineteen, ambitious, and pleased to see his name.

"Did you believe the song was yours?" Asha asked.

"I believed enough of it was mine to keep going. I also knew enough was Mae's to avoid releasing it. Those beliefs should not fit together, but they did."

Years later, he used four notes in a different chorus. He told himself melodies naturally repeated. Simon challenged him after hearing the song on radio. Ellis did not change the published credit then. Success made correction feel larger and therefore easier to postpone.

He kept Simon's letter because it named the wrong without requiring action. When Mae's song returned at the Morrowbay music night, he could no longer pretend the past had disappeared.

At the end of the interview, Ellis did not request a joint recording. He had understood Mae's reaction at Lantern House.

"If the original tape is all the public ever hears, that may be right," he said. "It contains the person I was before I learned to speak smoothly about my mistakes."

Asha marked the sentence for possible use but did not treat it as a perfect ending. Smooth self-criticism could become another form of charm.

After Ellis left, Asha recorded a private editorial note. She had found him persuasive even when he described his own failure. His skill as an interviewer gave shape to uncertainty; Mae's less polished pauses could sound weaker by comparison.

The series would need to resist confusing fluency with truth. It could not solve that by cutting Ellis until he sounded guilty or polishing Mae until she sounded equally practiced. The difference in public skill was itself part of their unequal positions.

Mara Hart asked for her own background conversation. She did not want to defend her husband on tape, but she had watched him decide when and how to acknowledge the past.

She explained that Ellis first told her about Mae after the four-note correction, not when they married. He described Low Tide as a school band that fell apart. Only later did he admit that the unreleased song began with Mae and that Simon had challenged him about the later melody.

"I asked why he had not contacted her," Mara said. "He answered that too much time had passed. That is a sentence people use when they mean action has become embarrassing."

She also resisted the idea that Ellis had spent decades actively hiding a tape. He did not possess it and probably believed it lost. His wrong lay in benefiting from the old label, using related notes later, and not seeking Mae when he understood enough to do so.

Asha asked Mara whether she wanted this recorded formally. Mara declined. Becoming a participant would draw their marriage into a story she had joined years after the event. She allowed Asha to use the conversation only to identify questions for Ellis, not as an unnamed source supporting claims.

In his follow-up, Ellis acknowledged the late disclosure to his wife. He had controlled the story inside his own home as carefully as in public. That pattern mattered more than a dramatic theory that he had hidden a physical tape.

Asha did not use Mara's sharper sentence about embarrassment. It remained hers. Ellis's own answer was less elegant and more revealing: "I waited until apology felt impossible, then used impossibility as the reason to wait."

That line also sounded polished. Asha placed it beside a long breath and his admission that Mae owed him no response. She did not add music that told listeners whether to admire the insight.

Chapter 12: Simon's Second Box

Leon returned to the storage room where he had found the green box years earlier. Most of Simon's belongings had been sorted, but a locked metal case remained behind old speakers. Leon had assumed it contained cables.

The small key from the first envelope did not fit. A storage worker helped him identify the case number on the original inventory. The key had been recorded in a sealed property packet held by Simon's estate adviser.

Leon opened the case with the adviser present. It contained cables, two microphones, and three small reels. One reel had HOME - AFTER written across its paper cover. Another said JUNE DRUM TEST. The third had no label.

He did not play them on an old machine. He contacted Saira and arranged for the archive to inspect the reels without taking ownership. Mae, June, and Ellis received notice before any sound was copied.

The HOME - AFTER reel contained fragments from the Coast session. Most were failed starts and instrument tests. Near the end, the recorder continued after the music stopped.

Voices moved around the studio. Simon complained about a cable. June asked what time the last train left. Roy Denning's voice entered from farther away. He praised Mae's melody and said Ellis could "front it properly" once the middle was tightened.

Mae replied, "It is not a coat. He cannot just put it on."

Roy laughed. Ellis said something the microphone did not catch. Then came a door sound, several overlapping voices, and Mae's clear sentence:

"Fine. Take the stupid thing. See if it still knows the way home."

The outer door closed. June said Mae's name. Simon said, "Let her cool down." Roy began talking about the next take.

The reel ended before any next take.

Hearing the sentence did not resolve its meaning. It confirmed June's verb and Mae's anger. It also made clear that Roy had proposed replacing her before she left. Ellis's response remained buried under movement.

Mae listened at the archive with Iris. She had believed the sentence might make her sound childish. Instead, her young voice sounded controlled until the final words.

"I was giving them the song and cursing it at the same time," she said. "I do not know whether I meant either part by morning."

Saira reminded everyone that a recorded outburst was evidence of words, not a complete agreement or permanent intention. The archive description would identify context without deciding ownership.

Leon struggled with Simon's "let her cool down." To him, it sounded dismissive. Simon had allowed work to continue rather than following Mae. The brother Leon missed and the young man on the reel had to occupy the same memory.

Asha requested research access to the new fragments. Northlight would use nothing in the documentary until separate audio permission was settled. For now, she added the exact words to the timeline and removed Ellis's remembered "you can have it."

Ellis listened alone. Afterwards, he wrote to Mae:

I remembered a softer sentence because it gave me a softer role. The reel has corrected that. It has not told me everything I failed to say under the noise.

Mae kept the letter but did not answer immediately. A good sentence could acknowledge a truth and still arrive thirty years late.

Chapter 13: The Sound Under the Sound

Amara introduced Asha to a sound-restoration specialist called Tomas Wynn. His work could reduce tape noise and make quiet speech clearer, but every adjustment involved judgment. Removing a frequency might also remove part of a voice.

Tomas prepared three versions of the unclear section where Ellis spoke. The first was almost untouched. The second reduced the door and cable sounds. The third used stronger processing that made a few syllables seem audible.

In the strongest version, some listeners heard Ellis say, "We can both do it." Others heard, "We can redo it." Tomas refused to write either phrase into a transcript.

"The process has not recovered certainty," he said. "It has created a cleaner place for our minds to guess."

Asha decided the documentary should not isolate those syllables as a clue. It could explain that Ellis spoke and the words were lost under other sound. Playing the heavily processed version would invite audiences to hear whichever story they already preferred.

Iris disagreed at first. She heard "redo it" and believed Ellis had accepted replacing Mae. Leon heard "both do it," which supported Ellis's account of two versions. Mae could hear either after someone suggested it.

"Then the sound is not evidence for one sentence," Mae said. "It is a mirror."

The original track contained other subtleties. June's drums entered only after Mae called her name. Simon stopped playing during one line and began again with a new pattern. Ellis's harmony sometimes led the melody rather than following it.

Asha wanted listeners to hear how collaboration appeared in sound, not only in claims. Yet describing musical details risked turning the documentary into an expert lesson rather than a human story. She asked each musician what they heard emotionally.

June heard herself waiting for permission. Ellis heard his confidence growing with each take. Mae heard the band listening closely to her before they stopped listening to what she wanted.

All three responses could sit over the same notes.

Tomas also found a mechanical click before the second verse. It might indicate that the tape had been paused, which meant part of the conversation or writing process was missing. The four-minute track was not an untouched window. Someone at the studio had already chosen when recording began and stopped.

The idea unsettled Iris. She had treated the tape as the most honest witness because it could not forget. In fact, it contained only what a microphone happened to keep.

Asha added a line to her working outline: Recordings preserve sound, not the whole room.

Nell saw the sentence when Mae showed her an approved project update. It reminded her of film frames at Mile House. Amara's camera could prove where a table stood while leaving the owner outside the picture. Evidence was powerful partly because its edges were easy to overlook.

Northlight chose the lightly restored audio for internal work. The original remained available for comparison. No one would clean the young voices until they sounded more certain than they had been.

Tomas invited the participants to his studio in pairs so they could hear what restoration changed. The room contained old speakers, modern screens, and a workbench scattered with cables. He began with a copy of an ordinary spoken sentence, not the disputed reel.

He removed a low hum. The voice became clearer but thinner. He removed a chair scrape and accidentally softened the speaker's first consonant. He restored the consonant, and part of the scrape returned.

"There is no version with only truth and no noise," he said. "The voice and room occupy some of the same sound."

Mae and June heard the demonstration together. June preferred the almost untouched Coast track because the hiss felt like the room she remembered. Mae preferred the light restoration; she had no loyalty to a machine's age.

For Simon's reel, Tomas could make Roy's sentence clearer without changing Mae's response. However, the stronger clarity made Roy sound closer to the microphone and therefore more dominant than he had physically been. Sound perspective carried its own meaning.

They chose a middle version and planned to tell listeners that it had been restored. Asha refused the common phrase "cleaned up," which implied noise was dirt and the remaining speech pure.

Tomas also identified two small edits made on the physical tape decades earlier. One splice joined the first and second takes. The archive track presented as one performance was already a studio construction. Mae's opening verse and Simon's later break might not have been played in the same uninterrupted take.

This discovery complicated the phrase "the session recording" without changing the contributors. Saira amended the catalogue from "performance" to "assembled studio track."

Ellis remembered Roy asking the engineer to combine the best parts. June did not. Mae had assumed they were hearing one take because her vocal seemed continuous.

The splice proved again that sound could preserve an event while shaping it. An unknown engineer had made the first editorial decision long before Asha arrived.

Mae felt less betrayed by the splice than she expected. Music had always involved choosing takes. What mattered was naming the choice rather than presenting the track as an untouched witness.

When they left, June said the hiss still sounded better. Mae said June only liked it because she had spent thirty years becoming a librarian. Their disagreement was ordinary enough to make both of them laugh.

Chapter 14: June Comes South

June arrived on a warm July afternoon. Mae met her at Morrowbay station without Northlight, Iris, Robin, or Nell. They had not seen each other since they were twenty.

June carried one small case and wore a denim jacket despite the heat. For a moment, they stood on opposite sides of the ticket gate, looking for the people they remembered inside older faces.

"Your hair is short," June said.

"Yours is grey."

"It has been grey for years. You missed the difficult middle."

Mae laughed, surprising both of them.

They walked to Lantern House by the same route Nell had taken on her first day. At the Market Square clock, June stopped.

"Robin said someone once asked you to meet here about the tape."

"Leon did not come. It was a poor beginning."

"I wrote to Robin because I was afraid to come too."

Mae had prepared Room Three. June offered to pay, but Mae wanted her as a personal guest. They agreed that the stay would not enter business records as a public booking. June's presence would remain private while she decided about the documentary.

In the dining room, Mae opened the green box. June recognised the song pages and yellow scarf. She touched neither until Mae invited her.

"I kept these pages because they proved you had written it," June said. "Then I used keeping them as an excuse for not returning them."

Mae asked why June had not followed her from the studio.

"I wanted Roy to hear my drums. I thought if I left, the session would continue without both of us. I told myself one person should stay to protect the song. Really, I wanted to stay."

The answer hurt because it was not noble. Mae trusted it more for that reason.

June remembered Ellis arguing with Roy after Mae left, although the reel did not capture it. Ellis said the group could not simply replace her. Roy replied that bands replaced singers all the time. Simon wanted to finish the instrumental part while the studio was paid for.

"Why did you write only that I was furious?" Mae asked.

"Because the diary was about me. Your anger became the weather around my choice."

June cried then. Mae did not tell her everything was forgiven. She sat beside her until the moment passed.

They ate dinner in the kitchen after guests had gone upstairs. Mae told June about David, Iris, and buying Lantern House. June spoke about her school library, former partner, and the students who believed librarians never had bands.

Their missing thirty years could not be repaired in one evening. They did not try. At ten, June carried her case upstairs. Mae locked the blue door and placed the green box back in the cupboard.

The next morning, June signed the research agreement. She chose to record only after spending another day with Mae away from microphones.

Chapter 15: The Drummer's Time

June's interview began with rhythm. She tapped four beats on the arm of her chair and explained that Low Tide often rushed. Ellis moved ahead when he became excited. Simon followed him. Mae slowed a line to make room for words. June's task was to keep all three inside one piece of time.

"That sounds like power," Asha said.

"It felt like running behind people with a rope."

June had not considered herself a writer. She changed rhythm, shortened breaks, and sometimes decided where a chorus returned. No one discussed whether those choices counted as songwriting. Credit seemed to belong to the person who brought words and chords, unless a manager wrote something else on a box.

On the archive track, June created the soft drum entrance after Mae asked her not to make the late-bus verse sound like a march. The later rhythm under Ellis's verse came from Simon tapping his guitar case. June copied it and made it steadier.

She did not want a songwriting credit simply because the dispute had made every contribution visible. She did want the public record to say she shaped the arrangement.

"If we call every sound writing, Mae's original disappears in a crowd," she said. "If we call none of it writing, Ellis's verse disappears. The word credit is carrying several different jobs."

Asha asked what June believed the credits should be now.

June proposed: Song by Mae Chen, with second verse by Ellis Hart; arranged and performed by Low Tide. Simon's estate might argue that the instrumental middle was composition rather than arrangement. Ellis might say his harmony changed the final melody. Mae had not yet accepted any wording.

June described the argument after the session. Roy's suggestion did not shock her at the time. Music programmes showed male bands, with women usually presented as singers rather than writers or instrumentalists. June had accepted that pattern as ordinary while being harmed by it herself.

"I was afraid they would replace Mae," she said. "I did not understand that by staying silent, I was helping them do it."

After Mae left, the group attempted one take with Ellis singing. He stopped before the second verse and said the key was wrong. Simon proposed changing it. June refused to play. That was when the session ended.

No tape of the failed take had appeared. June's memory was the only current source. Ellis remembered trying but not June's refusal. The documentary would present the difference rather than choosing the more dramatic version.

Asha asked why June kept the diary.

"Because I never throw away paper," June said. "That is the librarian's answer. The other answer is that I wanted one place where I had admitted being a coward."

At the end, June tapped the four beats again. This time she stopped after three.

"That is what the band felt like after Mae left," she said. "People think a missing beat is silence. It pulls on everything around it."

Asha knew the line could become a trailer quote. She also knew using it too beautifully might turn June's regret into promotion. She marked it with a question rather than a star.

June asked to hear the interview the next day. She worried that her line about being a coward would become a final judgment. In the full recording, it followed stories of fear, ambition, and the failed take. Outside that sequence, it sounded like a confession designed for listeners.

Asha showed her the transcript but did not promise the line would stay or go. Its meaning would depend on placement. June could add context now if the word no longer expressed what she intended.

June kept it. "I was a coward in that moment. I was also nineteen. The second fact explains the first without cancelling it."

At Lantern House, Mae and June began the harder work that no documentary schedule contained. June wanted to apologise each time the studio day arose. Mae became tired of receiving the same apology in slightly improved language.

"I do not want our friendship to be you standing at my door forever," Mae said.

"I do not know whether I am allowed inside."

"Come inside and help with the cups. I will tell you when I am angry."

They washed breakfast dishes together. June put bowls on the wrong shelf and claimed the kitchen had too many rules. Mae said a librarian should respect classification.

Later, June showed Mae unrelated diary entries from the same summer. Most concerned clothes, school friends, and a person she had kissed behind the summer hall. The ordinary pages changed the weight of the famous entry. June had not been writing history; she had been nineteen and trying to understand each day before the next.

Mae did not read beyond the pages June offered. The diary's evidential value did not make the whole book public between friends.

They decided the documentary could quote the studio entry in June's voice. The written page would not appear in promotional images. If a visual version was later made, only the agreed lines could be shown.

On June's final morning in Morrowbay, she left her denim jacket in Room Three. Mae found it after the train departed and posted it back through the normal tracked service. She did not keep it as another object from the band's return.

June sent a picture when it arrived: the jacket hanging beside her library coat. The message contained no apology. Mae was pleased.

Chapter 16: Four Parts of One Song

With June in Morrowbay, Mae agreed to a private musical session at Sea Glass Theatre. It was not a performance or documentary interview. Asha could observe but not record. Ellis came with his guitar. Leon brought Simon's instrument from storage but kept it in its case.

Robin sat at the theatre piano. Kit had tuned a loose part in the bench that morning, then left. Iris and Nell waited in the back row.

Mae played the song as she remembered writing it alone. One guitar carried the main melody. She sang the first verse, left an instrumental space, and returned to the final lines. Without the band, the piece lasted less than three minutes.

Ellis then sang his second verse without accompaniment. Its words led naturally back to Mae's late traveller, but the rhyme and movement sounded like his later work.

"That part is yours," Mae said. "I do not remember writing any of it."

It was the first creative point they had settled together.

Simon could not explain his middle. Leon opened the guitar case and found an old set list tucked under the cloth. One line read HOME SONG - SIMON BREAK 8. It supported the memory that Simon created an eight-bar section, though it did not show the notes.

June tapped the two rhythms from the archive track. Robin played the final melody from Mae's handwritten page, then added Ellis's lower harmony. The harmony pulled one note down. On the tape, Mae followed that pull during the last repeat.

"I changed the note because you were under it," Mae told Ellis. "That does not mean you wrote my melody."

"No. But it means the recorded version contains the space between us."

The phrase made Mae pause. She had spent weeks protecting the original song from being swallowed by collaboration. Now she could hear that the archive track was neither her untouched composition nor a Hart/Price work. It was her song altered by four people, then wrongly carried forward under two names.

They tried the full arrangement. Leon did not play Simon's guitar; he was not a musician and did not want to stand in for his brother. Robin played the instrumental line from memory, making clear that it was an approximation.

At the end, no one clapped. Ellis asked whether Mae would consider recording that arrangement with accurate credits. Mae said the documentary had to come first. She did not want a new, polished track to replace the difficult evidence of the old one.

June agreed. A perfect reunion version would suggest that time had repaired the band. It had not. Simon was absent. Roy Denning had died years earlier. Mae and June were beginning a friendship again, not continuing one without a break.

Asha wrote no notes until she left the room. In the theatre cafe, she recorded what she remembered about the session, including the fact that her account might miss details because no microphone had been allowed.

The documentary outline changed. Episode One would follow the return of Mae's voice. Episode Two would examine the studio day and unequal decision. Episode Three would explore memory, contribution, and the missing names. Episode Four would ask what recognition could mean now without pretending the past could be replayed correctly.

For the first time, the project had a shape. It still did not have a title.

Chapter 17: Names Beneath the Track

Saira asked the four sides to propose catalogue credits separately. She did not want the first confident wording to become the centre around which everyone else negotiated.

Mae wrote: Original song and first verse by Mae Chen. Second verse by Ellis Hart. Recorded arrangement by Low Tide.

Ellis wrote: Song by Mae Chen and Ellis Hart, based on Chen's original melody and first verse. Arrangement by Low Tide.

June's wording matched Mae's, with an added note naming Simon's instrumental section. Leon proposed: Music by Mae Chen and Simon Price; words by Mae Chen and Ellis Hart; arrangement by Low Tide.

The differences were small on paper and large in meaning. Ellis believed his lower harmony had helped create the recorded melody. Leon believed Simon's eight-bar section was composition, not merely arrangement. Mae feared that placing three names beside "music" would recreate the old disappearance in a fairer-looking form.

Asha invited them to discuss the actual components rather than defend complete credit lines. They met in the archive listening room, where the tape could be played but not copied.

Mae's handwritten page established the main melody before the Coast session. Ellis accepted that. His second verse was his, though Mae may have changed two words. Simon created the instrumental break. June shaped both drum patterns. The final repeated melody changed when Mae responded to Ellis's harmony.

"Can one note have two writers?" Leon asked.

"It can have a history," Saira said. "The catalogue can describe more than a label line."

They considered two kinds of credit: a concise display line and a fuller note. The display could identify the original song and recorded arrangement. The note could specify the second verse, break, rhythm, and final variation without pretending every contribution fitted the same category.

Ellis offered to place Mae alone on the main song line. He would accept his verse in the note. Mae suspected that public pressure had made generosity easier, but the reason did not make the offer worthless.

Leon wanted Simon visible without expanding his brother's claim beyond evidence. He agreed that "instrumental section by Simon Price" was clearer than shared music credit.

June rejected a named writing share. "Put my drums in the performance note. Do not turn the fact that nobody valued arrangement into a reason to call all arrangement composition now."

The provisional catalogue became:

Original song by Mae Chen; second verse by Ellis Hart; instrumental section by Simon Price. Recorded arrangement developed and performed by Mae Chen, June Bell, Ellis Hart, and Simon Price.

The final melodic variation remained described in the note as emerging from Chen's lead and Hart's harmony. No one had to decide which voice owned the space between two notes.

The wording did not settle financial rights or every possible future use. It described the archive track as the participants could currently support. Saira marked it provisional because new evidence or agreement might change it.

Mae read her name at the beginning. For the first time, the credit followed the song into the room instead of beginning after she left.

The archive did not change only the visible catalogue line. Its internal search terms, box description, and linked names also needed correction. If Mae appeared in the public title but remained absent from the underlying record, future researchers might lose her again when systems changed.

Saira showed the proposed record to each participant. It included alternative titles such as Home Light and Mae's Song, while making clear that neither was confirmed. "Untitled home-light song" remained the neutral display title.

Mae disliked seeing "Mae's Song" as an alternative because Ellis had used the phrase casually, not as a title she chose. Yet removing it would make searches for historical references harder. They kept it as an attributed former description: called "Mae's Song" by some band members.

Simon appeared with his full name and dates. June appeared under the name she had used at nineteen as well as her current name after a past marriage. She chose which form led. Ellis's public stage name linked to the school name on old logs without exposing unrelated identity records.

Metadata seemed far from music until Mae saw how the Hart/Price label had shaped thirty years of assumption. A small field could become the first story every later person inherited.

Leon asked for the original box image to remain visible. Hiding the wrong credit would hide evidence of the omission. Saira agreed, adding a caption that named it as a historical label, not the archive's current conclusion.

Mae considered whether placing her name first repeated another hierarchy. Alphabetical performer names solved that problem on the general record, while the creative note began with the original composition because chronology mattered there.

No format was neutral. Alphabetical order treated different roles alike. Contribution order required judgment. Public fame pulled Ellis forward even when his name came third because readers recognised it first.

The archive could not make every reader encounter the names equally. It could stop using Ellis's fame as the organising principle.

When the corrected entry went live, Mae printed one copy. She did not frame it. She placed it in the green box beside the old tape label, allowing the right and wrong records to remain together.

Iris wanted to photograph the two for the family. Mae allowed a private picture that showed no archive access codes or unrelated letters. The image went into Iris's own album rather than online.

Recognition had arrived through a catalogue form, which felt both inadequate and important. Mae had imagined something louder. In practice, the first correction was a set of careful fields that a future stranger might never notice were once empty.

Chapter 18: The Rough Assembly

Asha's first assembly ran for nearly six hours. It contained no narration and little music. She had placed interview sections, archive descriptions, and periods of room sound in a loose order so the team could hear the available material.

Mae listened alone at Sea Glass Theatre. Asha sat outside, ready to stop if needed but not watching her reactions.

The first hour made Mae uneasy. Her younger voice appeared before her present one. The archive track sounded bright and hopeful. Then present-day Mae said, "The omission hurt more after hearing my voice at the centre." The contrast made the story feel like a fall from innocence.

Mae's youth had not been innocent. Low Tide argued, competed, made jokes, and knew adults might treat members differently. She had also made choices that hurt June and Ellis before the final session. None of those details yet appeared.

The second hour gave Ellis too much space to explain himself smoothly. Asha had included long passages because his account was detailed. Mae's anger arrived in shorter sentences. Without intention, the structure made him sound thoughtful and her sound emotional.

Mae wrote that observation on the response form. She did not ask Asha to cut Ellis's complexity. She asked for room around her own thought process.

The third hour contained June's missing-beat line directly after her admission of cowardice. It was beautiful and effective. Mae hated it.

"June's regret is not a poem for the ending," she told Asha during the break.

Asha agreed that the placement made the line resolve too much. She moved it into the musical discussion, where rhythm was the real subject.

The rough assembly also revealed what the project lacked. Leon had spoken mostly about Simon's objects, not their brotherhood. Iris existed only as a daughter defending Mae. David, whose private life Mae excluded, appeared as a name in the station story and then vanished. That absence was intentional, but listeners might mistake it for lack of importance.

Mae recorded one additional sentence: "David knew the song and believed me. I am not telling the rest of his response because he cannot choose what becomes public now. His absence from this series is a boundary, not an empty place in my life."

Asha placed the sentence once, without sentimental music.

At the end of six hours, Mae was exhausted. The assembly had not misquoted her, yet its sequence created meanings no individual sentence carried alone.

That was the editor's real power. Asha did not hide from it. She kept a decision log showing why major juxtapositions were made and whose concern had changed them. The log would never remove subjectivity, but it stopped choices from pretending to be natural.

The next version would include brief narration to establish dates and make clear where evidence ended. Asha chose a narrator with no connection to the musicians. Mae did not want Nell, Iris, or a famous music voice guiding the audience towards loyalty.

The project finally gained a working title from Ellis's phrase in the theatre: The Space Between Voices. Mae liked it because it did not put theft, silence, or Ellis's fame at the centre.

Ellis hesitated when he heard the title. He had spoken the phrase during a private musical session, not an interview. Asha had written it from memory afterwards. Using it publicly risked turning an unrecorded moment into her property.

She asked him directly. Ellis allowed the title without seeking a special credit. Mae also agreed because the phrase described the recorded variation rather than awarding ownership. June thought it sounded too elegant but offered no better name.

The narrator, Celia North, recorded a test. Her voice was clear and warm. Mara worried that warmth softened the conflict. Asha tried another reader whose sharper delivery made every uncertainty sound like suspicion.

Celia kept the role, but Asha directed her to avoid emotional emphasis on disputed words. Narration would establish source and sequence, not perform a verdict. The participants' own voices could carry feeling.

Music design created another problem. Northlight could not yet use the archive track, so a composer made simple transitions from new sounds: a bus passing, a station clock, a guitar case tap, and the Lantern House door. Mae objected to the door sound. It made her present home seem destined to complete the teenage lyric.

The real brass lamp and blue door were coincidences shaped by years of later life. Turning them into a repeated sound motif made fate where there had been choices.

Asha removed the door. She kept the clock only in the timeline sections and the guitar-case tap in the arrangement episode. Silence separated the most difficult interview passages.

Ellis reviewed his assembly next. He noticed that Asha had included three of his self-critical statements and little of his affection for the band. The edit risked making him a man performing confession rather than someone who had also made good music with friends.

Mae supported adding one memory of pleasure. Accountability did not require deleting every happy practice. Asha used Ellis's account of the four of them missing the last bus after a show and walking home with June's drum between them.

June remembered that night differently: her father collected the drum halfway. The episode kept the broad walk and removed the detail of carrying it all the way.

Leon heard too little of Simon's humour. He offered a story about Simon changing every set-list title into food names. Asha included one example, then returned to the letter. The dead member gained texture without becoming lovable enough to cancel his actions.

The rough assembly improved through these additions. Complexity was not only uncertainty about blame. It was the presence of ordinary warmth beside harm, making the choices harder to dismiss as the work of people who had always been cruel.

Chapter 19: Twenty-Two Seconds

The archive prepared a private online page so participants could hear approved excerpts while reviewing the catalogue. During a routine update, one setting on the twenty-two-second studio argument excerpt changed from restricted to public preview.

The mistake remained live for thirty-eight minutes. A music blogger's automatic search found the Ellis Hart tag and saved the audio before the archive closed access.

By lunchtime, Mae's sentence and Roy's laugh were circulating online:

"Fine. Take the stupid thing. See if it still knows the way home."

The clip ended before Simon's response. It did not include Roy's earlier suggestion to replace Mae as lead. Without that context, some listeners heard Mae giving the song away. Others treated her words as obvious proof that the band stole it after humiliating her.

The same twenty-two seconds supported opposite certainties.

Saira called all participants before the archive issued a statement. She explained the error, what had been accessed, and what the archive was doing. She did not blame an unnamed junior worker. The public setting had passed through a checking process that failed.

Mae was furious. She had refused public audio while deciding how much of her younger voice to share. Now the most painful sentence was the first part strangers heard.

Ellis's team asked platforms to remove copies where possible, but new versions appeared with music and captions. One slowed Mae's voice to make it sound ghostly. Another placed it before a clip from Ellis's later radio show.

June saw comments calling her a witness who had hidden evidence for decades. Leon saw people write that Simon's death protected him from consequences. None of those strangers knew what June's diary or Simon's letter actually said.

Northlight suspended all documentary editing. Asha did not want the project racing to release a corrective episode while attention was high. That would benefit from the same loss of control it claimed to repair.

The archive removed the private review page entirely and offered secure in-person listening until its access process had been examined. It issued a precise correction:

An excerpt from a restricted recording was made publicly accessible in error for thirty-eight minutes. Public circulation was not authorised by the performers or other participants. The excerpt is incomplete and does not establish ownership or intent. We apologise to Mae Chen, June Bell, Ellis Hart, Leon Price, and those represented in the recording.

The apology named the people affected. It could not pull the sound back into the tape box.

At Lantern House, Mae closed online booking for two days because false reservations had begun again. Real guests could call the normal number. Rosa handled enquiries while Mae stayed with Iris.

Nell wanted to drive to Redwater, but Mae asked her not to. Too many people were already arriving around the event. She wanted one evening with her daughter and no plan.

The twenty-two seconds had been preserved for three decades without an audience. Once released, they crossed the world before Mae finished lunch.

At the archive, Saira reconstructed the access failure. A software update had replaced two labels, "visible to reviewers" and "public preview," with similar symbols. One staff member selected the wrong symbol; a second person checked the text but not the audio setting. The broader process assumed that restricted objects would remain restricted unless deliberately published, an assumption the update had broken.

Saira had approved the private review page. Although she had not changed the setting, she felt personally responsible. The archive director offered to lead participant calls, but Saira believed the apology should come from the person who had built trust with them.

Mae listened to her explanation and said, "I do not need you to carry every worker's mistake in your own name. I need the archive to carry it."

That distinction helped Saira remain accountable without turning guilt into the centre of the response.

The archive froze public audio changes until every restricted record had been checked. It did not claim that a new symbol or extra approval made future failure impossible. Its report described the update, double-check weakness, automatic search that found the clip, and thirty-eight-minute exposure.

One board member wanted to avoid publishing the exact duration because it might attract more attention. Saira argued that vagueness would protect the institution at the expense of those already exposed. The number stayed.

The blogger who first saved the clip refused to remove it, saying the archive had made it public. Asha did not contact him with a dramatic appeal. Northlight's request stated that access had been accidental and performers had not authorised reuse. Some platforms removed later copies; others did not.

Mae learned that control after release came in fragments. One copy disappeared while three appeared elsewhere. Each successful removal felt pointless until she remembered that fewer future listeners still mattered.

She chose not to listen to altered versions. Iris kept a private list only where needed for formal requests, then stopped searching. Constant monitoring could turn harm into daily work performed by the harmed person.

Saira visited Lantern House after the immediate crisis. She came through the front door without archive papers and paid for her tea. Mae did not reassure her that everything was fine. They spoke about the damage, then about Ruth Bell's railway novel on the sitting-room shelf.

Trust did not return through a perfect apology. It survived in a reduced, more realistic form: Mae believed Saira would tell her the next difficult fact directly, while no longer believing the archive could keep every boundary simply because it intended to.

Chapter 20: No Simple Side

Public sympathy gathered around Mae, but sympathy quickly became a demand. People wanted her to condemn Ellis in stronger words. When she refused an interview, some accused her of being pressured into silence.

Ellis received threats. One message included his home street. His public team cancelled an event, and he stayed away from Morrowbay. Mae did not excuse his earlier choices, but she issued a direct statement that harassment and threats did not support her.

Some former supporters turned against her for that sentence. They said she was protecting a powerful man. Others praised her for forgiveness she had not offered.

"Every statement becomes a story I did not write," she told Iris.

Iris thought Mae should say plainly that Ellis had benefited from replacing her. Mae had said this in the documentary interview, but Iris wanted it in the immediate public statement.

"If you leave space, his team fills it," Iris argued.

"And if I give strangers the hardest sentence they want, they use it as permission for anything."

Their disagreement lasted through dinner. Iris accused her mother of making herself smaller to keep peace. Mae accused Iris of treating anger as the only honest voice.

They slept in separate rooms. In the morning, neither withdrew the main point.

June published her own short statement. She confirmed that Roy proposed replacing Mae and that the remaining band did not protect her. She also said the leaked clip was incomplete and should not be used to invent certainty around every contribution.

Ellis released no new defence. His earlier interview remained private. He wrote to Mae that he would follow her lead on immediate public response. Mae replied that he should make his own ethical choices rather than asking her to manage his silence.

At Northlight, Mara faced pressure from a distributor who suddenly wanted the documentary quickly. The distributor suggested a two-part special while the subject was current. Mara refused. Northlight might lose the offer, but speeding up would reward the leak.

Asha spent the pause organising material without editing. She documented which public claims were false: the track was not a hit; Mae had not signed a release; June's diary had not been sold; Simon's reel did not contain a clear confession from Ellis.

Nell covered two more Lantern House shifts. Reporters had mostly left Market Square, though one still called each morning. Nell answered as staff: the guesthouse had no comment and bookings were for accommodation only.

Mae returned on Sunday. She stood in the dining room where her young voice had first been heard by the small group. Nothing in the room had changed, but she felt as if strangers had entered it.

Robin came for tea. He did not tell her to ignore public noise. He said a teacher sometimes heard a whole class repeat the wrong note because the loudest singer began first.

"You cannot shout thirty years louder than them," he said. "You can decide which room deserves your voice next."

Mae did not yet know whether that room was the documentary.

At Iris's flat, the argument continued in a quieter form. Mae admitted that Ellis had benefited from the replacement decision even though the song was never released. He gained Roy's attention, studio confidence, and a story in which leadership belonged to him.

Iris admitted that she wanted a sentence strong enough to end public doubt. She had spent years managing theatre rooms where clear calls prevented confusion. The internet looked like another room in need of a firm voice.

"It is not a theatre," Mae said. "No one has to stop talking when you call the next scene."

They wrote possible statements together and watched each one fail in a different direction. "Ellis took my song" ignored the genuine second verse and the unclear angry permission. "The band made a mistake" made a pattern of exclusion sound accidental. "We all remember differently" placed unequal choices on a flat surface.

Mae eventually chose no new statement beyond rejecting harassment. The documentary, if continued, would hold the fuller account. Immediate public silence could be a chosen limit without repeating her old withdrawal.

Outside, a neighbour had recognised Mae from an online picture. She left a note under Iris's door offering support. The gesture was kind and unsettling because it showed that Redwater was not fully outside the story.

Mae thanked her without discussing details. Then she returned to Morrowbay on an afternoon train, not the morning service reporters expected.

Farah met her at the station. She did not hide Mae through a back route. They walked down the main hill together, stopping at Harbour Kitchen for tea. A few people looked; no one approached.

At Lantern House, Rosa had handled the bookings well. The house did not need Mae's immediate return to survive, which allowed her to enter as a person rather than a manager rescuing work.

Robin's advice about the loudest singer helped, but Mae did not want to spend life choosing only quiet rooms. The documentary might become a room with enough structure for several voices. It might also amplify conflict again.

She asked Asha for the raw-interview listening before deciding. Hearing herself without editorial shape would not reveal a pure truth, but it would show what she had actually placed in Northlight's hands.

That night, Mae slept in her own flat behind the kitchen. She could hear a guest walk to the bathroom and the boiler begin. Familiar sounds reclaimed the building more effectively than any public statement.

Chapter 21: The Pause

Northlight offered every participant the choice to end, continue, or delay the project. Recorded interviews would remain secured under the agreements while the decision was made. Nothing would be released simply because money had been spent.

June wanted to continue. The leak had shown how quickly her silence could be rewritten by others. Leon was uncertain. He worried that any series would bring more attention to Simon's failures without allowing his brother to respond.

Ellis said he would accept Mae's decision, which irritated her again. His participation was his responsibility. She did not want to become the moral owner of the entire project because she had been wronged first.

Asha met each person separately. With Mae, she did not argue that the series could correct the clip. Correction was one possible benefit, not a debt Mae owed the public.

Mae asked to hear her full raw interview. Asha provided it in the Sea Glass room. Mae listened to every hesitation, repeated sentence, chair sound, and request for water.

The raw recording did not feel like her either. It was three hours of one day under unusual attention. The edited version was shaped; the raw version was shapeless. Neither could claim to be the whole person.

She chose to continue under two changes. First, the leak and archive failure would be part of the series, not hidden as embarrassing production history. Second, the leaked sentence would never be used alone in promotion, even though it was now widely available.

Asha agreed. Mara accepted a slower schedule with no release date.

Leon continued after hearing the proposed treatment of Simon. The narrator would not speculate about what he "must have felt." His letter, set list, recorded words, and others' memories would remain distinct.

Ellis made his own choice to continue. He added a second interview specifically about public power. He acknowledged that he could hire advisers, issue statements, and wait behind a protected address while Mae's business became a target.

June asked to record a shorter follow-up about the difference between preserving paper and controlling it. She had kept the diary, but the leak proved that possession alone could not decide context once an institution held a copy.

Saira agreed to be interviewed about the archive error. She would describe the system failure without exposing the personal details of individual staff. Accountability did not require offering one worker to the same public appetite for blame.

The project resumed in September. The pause had cost Northlight money and made the series less commercially timely. It had also given the participants room to choose after the conditions changed.

Mae returned to the microphone with less trust in every institution around it. Asha did not ask her to restore that trust before speaking. The new series would have to carry the damage in its own making.

The distributor withdrew its early offer after Northlight rejected the faster schedule. Mara did not pretend the loss was welcome. The money would have covered most editing and promotion costs. Northlight now had to reduce its launch plan and postpone another small audio project.

At a company meeting, one manager asked whether the participants should contribute by allowing more promotional material. Asha objected. They were not responsible for replacing money lost because Northlight chose an ethical delay. Mara supported her and found savings elsewhere.

The project gave up a planned filmed trailer, a public launch at Northcross, and paid advertisements using the archive track. It kept fact review, sound restoration, participant payment, and secure listening. The budget revealed priorities more clearly than the proposal had.

Mae heard about the cuts through her adviser. She offered to waive part of her payment. Mara refused. Accepting would turn Mae's desire for care into a subsidy for the production company.

Ellis offered private funding. That presented a different problem: the person with the largest public power would become the project's financial support. Northlight declined, and Ellis accepted the reason.

June suggested a fundraiser, then withdrew the idea herself. Public donations might create a sense that listeners owned access before participants had completed review.

Mara used Northlight's reserve and negotiated a simpler distribution agreement with an independent audio platform. The platform would host all four episodes without demanding a sensational subtitle. It received no rights to create short derivative clips beyond agreed promotion.

The pause also changed the team's emotional rhythm. Before the leak, Asha sent updates every Friday. Afterwards, some participants wanted immediate notice of every edit involving the leaked sentence, while others needed fewer messages.

They chose individual contact plans. Mae and Iris received one shared weekly summary. June received paper transcripts because she disliked the secure portal. Ellis used his representative for scheduling but received editorial concerns directly. Leon wanted a phone call before any new Simon material entered.

Equal treatment did not require identical communication. It required the same respect for informed participation through different practical routes.

When recording resumed, Asha left the first five minutes of Mae's session unused. They spoke about breakfast and the train until Mae's voice settled. The microphone did not need to capture every return to the room.

Chapter 22: Mother and Daughter

Iris had not signed a participant agreement. She appeared only in Mae's account and as a family adviser. After their argument, Asha asked whether Iris wanted a formal interview about inheriting a story she had not lived.

Iris refused at first. She did not want to become "the angry daughter" who said what Mae was too polite to say. A week later, she changed her mind on the condition that her interview include disagreement with Mae rather than presenting the family as one voice.

Mae supported the condition, though she feared hearing their private conflict in public.

In the Sea Glass room, Iris described growing up beside the locked music cupboard. She knew her mother had played in a band, but the story came as a funny picture of old clothes and a record with two songs. She did not know there was a lost composition at the centre.

When the song returned, Iris felt protective and excluded at once. Strangers could now hear a young version of Mae that her own daughter had never met.

"I wanted to fight the people who took it," Iris said. "Then Mum refused to use the same simple words, and I thought she was refusing my support."

Asha asked what Iris understood later.

"That I was trying to give her the anger I needed. It was not exactly hers."

Mae listened to the passage during review. She asked Asha to include her own response: Iris's anger had sometimes carried Mae when she was tired, but support became heavy when it allowed only one direction.

They recorded one conversation together. It was the only joint interview in the series. Asha stayed mostly silent.

Iris asked why Mae had never sung the full song to her. Mae said David knew it, but after his death the tune became tied to two losses: the band and her husband. She kept it in a cupboard because private grief felt safer than unfinished authorship.

"You taught me to ask for my name on theatre work," Iris said. "You did not ask for yours."

"I taught you the part I had learned too late."

The sentence brought them closer without ending their disagreement. Iris still believed Ellis's public responsibility should be stated more strongly. Mae still resisted making him the only reason her name disappeared. Roy's influence, Simon's choice, June's silence, industry expectations, and Mae's own withdrawal all mattered in unequal ways.

After recording, they ate at Harbour Kitchen. Farah had become head baker and rarely worked the counter, but she came out to greet them. She offered no opinion about the dispute. She gave each woman the same apple pastry.

Iris laughed at the careful equality. The ordinary gesture made it possible to talk about her next theatre job instead.

Their relationship was larger than the song, which was exactly why the documentary could include only part of it.

Iris's interview changed how she worked at the theatre. During her next production, a young designer complained that the director presented her idea as the team's general solution. Iris first wanted to compare the situation to Mae's song, then stopped. The designer's work deserved attention without becoming an example inside someone else's famous story.

She helped the team correct the design record and name the original proposal. Afterwards, she told Mae only the broad lesson, not the designer's details.

"I keep seeing you in every missing name," Iris said.

"Then look twice," Mae replied. "Sometimes it will be me. Sometimes it will be a different person who needs a different answer."

Their joint interview underwent a careful review. Asha had shortened a long pause after David's name. Without the full pause, Mae's next sentence sounded calm. With it, the moment became visibly painful and might invite listeners to imagine details she had excluded.

Mae asked to keep some of the silence but not all. Asha used four seconds. There was no objective length that represented grief honestly. The choice balanced audibility and privacy.

Iris challenged another edit. Her words "Mum refused" appeared before Mae's explanation, making Mae sound defensive. In the original conversation, Iris had first described her own need for anger. Asha restored that context.

Mae, in turn, allowed a sentence where Iris said her mother could use complexity to avoid direct blame. Mae disliked it but recognised it as a fair description of their disagreement. The series did not require Iris to end by accepting Mae's view.

After review, mother and daughter agreed not to listen together again until release. Repeatedly examining their conversation made ordinary speech feel risky. They needed to recover the ability to disagree without considering how an editor might cut it.

They spent a weekend in Bell Island, where no production work was planned. Iris left her phone in the hotel room for one walk. Mae bought a yellow scarf that reminded her of Robin and then chose a green one instead.

On the ferry home, they discussed whether Iris wanted children, a subject far more private and uncertain than the song. Neither mentioned the documentary. The boundary around that conversation was spoken once and needed no microphone to make it real.

Chapter 23: Nell Outside the Room

Nell's decision to stay outside the production became harder after the leak. People in Morrowbay knew Northlight employed her and assumed she was making the series. A local paper described her as Mae's producer, even after Northlight corrected it.

At a location meeting for an unrelated short film, an owner asked whether Nell secretly recorded private conversations. He had read about the archive leak and confused the institutions.

Nell explained her actual role and Northlight's separate documentary team. She did not promise that organisations never made mistakes. She described the records and boundaries relevant to his location only.

The owner accepted the explanation, but the question stayed with her. Trust travelled across work just as suspicion did. The archive's error changed how someone viewed a film location coordinator he had never met.

Gabriel suggested that Nell take a formal separation statement from Northlight: she had no editorial access, handled no documentary recordings, and would not use information learned through friendship. Mara added it to the internal project file and public staff response.

Mae worried that her story was harming Nell's career. Nell refused that frame. The leak was not Mae's action, and questions were part of working responsibly around public events.

At home, Kit saw another effect. Nell avoided mentioning Mae at all, even in private, because she feared crossing an invisible production line. Their friendship began to feel like a subject she had to store in the locked second room.

"Not every thought becomes research because Northlight exists," he said.

Nell knew this, but the line between friend and worker had become emotionally sharp. She spoke to Mae directly. They agreed on a simple distinction. Mae would mark anything she shared specifically for production review, though Nell still had no editorial role. Their ordinary conversations would remain ordinary unless Mae clearly chose otherwise later.

This did not create a legal wall around friendship. It restored trust in intention.

One Wednesday, Mae came to Nell and Kit's flat for dinner. She spoke about a difficult guest, June's terrible tea, Iris's new job, and the way the blue kitchen panels had looked in The Quiet Mile. She did not mention the latest edit.

Nell stopped waiting for the song to enter every pause. Kit served bread. The Market Square clock struck eight.

When Mae left, she said, "Thank you for not asking how the documentary is going."

Nell replied, "Thank you for telling me about the terrible tea."

The exchange was light, but it protected something the series could not record: a relationship that continued when no story was being gathered.

Nell's unrelated Northlight work grew during the same months. She and Gabriel scouted locations for a quiet contemporary drama set in a printing shop. The work should have felt simple after The Quiet Mile, but every owner now searched Northlight's name and found the archive story.

One printer admired the series proposal and offered family letters as possible set material. Nell declined to view them without a story need and clear discussion. Public praise could open doors too widely, just as excitement had at Mile House.

Another owner refused Northlight entirely because he believed the company had leaked the tape. Nell clarified that Northcross Sound Archive made the error but did not pressure him to reconsider. He was allowed to decide that the connection felt too close.

Mara worried the documentary was damaging the film side of the business. Asha reminded her that producing factual work created responsibilities across the company brand. They could not ask participants to bear public risk while Northlight expected its other work to remain untouched.

Gabriel saw Nell becoming too cautious in response. She turned down a useful photograph of the printing shop because a family member appeared in the distant background, even though the owner and person had both agreed to the scout image.

"Care is not the same as avoiding every record," he said. "If you no longer trust a clear yes, you stop people choosing with us."

Nell reviewed the permission and accepted the photograph for internal use. The lesson from Mae was not that all sharing became harm. It was that context, power, and later use mattered.

At home, Kit faced a related choice. A theatre magazine wanted pictures of his workshop and blue clock bird. He agreed to photographs of work areas after removing customer names and private drawings. He did not reject the article because cameras could be misused.

Nell helped him check the caption. It called the bird "the prop that saved opening night," although Lydia's invisible performance had saved the preview before the bird returned. Kit asked for the line to become "the prop that travelled to a laundry and returned for opening night."

Accuracy made a less heroic headline and a better account.

Their dinner conversations slowly stopped circling the documentary. Mae's story continued nearby without becoming the weather of every relationship around it.

Chapter 24: Building the Second Version

Asha dismantled the first assembly. She kept many interview passages but changed the centre of each episode.

Episode One no longer opened with the archive track. It began with Lantern House breakfast sounds recorded after guests and staff had agreed to a non-identifying room recording. Mae described a life built after the band. Only then did the young voice enter.

Episode Two placed Roy's audible suggestion before Mae's departure line. The order matched the reel and stopped the leaked sentence from floating without cause. Ellis's lost response remained explicitly unclear.

Episode Three explored the credits through musical parts. It included Mae's original solo version recreated at Sea Glass, but only after she approved that use. The new performance was labelled a present-day demonstration, not evidence of exactly how the first song sounded.

Episode Four included the archive leak, public reactions, and the decision to pause. Saira described institutional failure. Mae explained why sympathy could become another demand. Ellis discussed unequal protection. June spoke about context leaving her hands.

Asha commissioned an external fact reviewer, Dr Leona March, who had no connection to Northlight or the archive. Leona checked each statement against its stated source. She did not decide whose memory was morally right. She marked places where narration turned probability into certainty.

One narration line said Roy "decided to replace Mae." The reel proved he proposed Ellis as lead, not that he held formal power to decide. Asha changed it to "Roy urged the band to replace Mae as lead, and the others did not reject the proposal before she left."

Another line said Mae wrote the song alone. The original melody and first verse were hers; the archive version included later work. The new wording kept those stages separate.

Leon challenged a line calling Simon a co-writer because it did not identify which section. The narration now named his instrumental break.

Iris wanted the broader pattern around women in music stated. Asha added context from public historical sources without claiming every band behaved alike. Mae's experience fit a pattern; the pattern did not replace the details of her experience.

The second version lasted four hours and forty minutes. It was still long, but every episode had a clear movement. Asha resisted making the leaked clip the dramatic end of Episode Three. Public failure belonged in the story, not as a device to force the next play button.

Mae listened across four separate mornings. This time, she recognised the life around her younger voice. Ellis heard places where his explanation remained uncomfortable. June disliked hearing her own beautiful missing-beat line, but accepted its new musical context. Leon wished Simon could answer and knew no edit could repair that absence.

The Space Between Voices was becoming fairer. Fair did not mean that everyone liked an equal amount of it. It meant the structure did not secretly promise certainty, balance blame by counting minutes, or hide the power involved in deciding what listeners heard next.

Leona's fact review produced eighty-seven notes. Some corrected dates and names. Others challenged the confidence of small connecting words: therefore, although, still, and finally. Those words could turn separate facts into a causal story.

One draft said Mae left, therefore the band ended. Low Tide ended soon after, but no document proved her departure was the only cause. Ellis and Simon also disagreed with Roy, June refused another session, and school ended. The narration became: Mae left the session; within weeks, Low Tide had ended.

Another line said Simon later wrote because he still felt guilty. His letter showed regret but did not reveal why he chose that year. "Still" disappeared.

Mara worried that the revised narration sounded cautious to the point of weakness. Asha read the episode aloud. Most qualifications appeared only where needed; participant voices remained direct. Precision did not require every sentence to carry a warning label.

They also commissioned a sensitivity read focused on gender and music history. The reader noted that Roy risked becoming the only sexist force, allowing the younger men to appear as passive victims of an older system. Asha strengthened the section where Ellis and Simon chose not to reject his proposal.

At the same time, June's silence could not be explained only by gender solidarity. She wanted the session and feared losing her chance. Her interview already said so.

Mae asked whether the series judged her angry departure too heavily. If a male songwriter left after a replacement suggestion, listeners might call him principled. The narration did not label her reaction, but repeated discussion of "permission" risked placing responsibility on her exact words rather than the decision that caused them.

Asha reduced one legal-sounding section and let the reel sequence carry the context. The project was a narrative about people, not a hearing deciding whether an angry sentence met a formal standard.

Ellis accepted the change even though it weakened one possible defence. He retained space to explain what he believed at nineteen without requiring the series to treat that belief as reasonable.

The second version went through sound review, factual review, context review, and participant response. Each layer improved something and introduced another perspective. Asha had to decide when further review stopped clarifying and began making the work afraid of any position.

She set a final date for factual corrections. Emotional responses after that would be heard but would not automatically reopen the edit. Everyone agreed reluctantly. A documentary that remained permanently revisable would never become a public record at all.

Chapter 25: Letting the Song Be Heard

The documentary could be completed without the archive track, but its absence had begun to feel false. Listeners would hear everyone discuss a performance that remained behind a locked door while an unauthorised argument clip was already public.

Saira proposed allowing carefully chosen extracts under a new agreement. The archive held the physical source, while the performers and relevant interests had different claims around the recorded work. No single person could simply open it for Northlight.

Mae's starting position was that the full track should not appear in the series. She wanted listeners to hear enough of her lead voice and original melody to understand what had been omitted. She did not want the documentary to become the first commercial release of a session whose meaning remained unsettled.

Ellis agreed. June wanted a longer section so her drums and Simon's break were not reduced to names in narration. Leon supported her; a short first-verse clip might make the arrangement sound like Mae alone with background players.

Asha built a use map. Episode One would include thirty seconds from the opening, centred on Mae. Episode Two would use the transition into Ellis's second verse. Episode Three would play Simon's instrumental break and June's rhythm. Episode Four would return to the final variation where Mae and Ellis's voices changed each other.

The total across the series would be two minutes and ten seconds, with no extract longer than forty seconds. The leaked argument would appear for only the necessary sentence and preceding context from Roy.

Mae listened to the proposed cuts. Separated across episodes, they allowed the song to develop as the history developed. Yet she worried that listeners could join the pieces into most of the track.

Tomas explained that the edits overlapped slightly for context, but important sections remained absent. More importantly, Northlight would not present the clips as fragments listeners were invited to reconstruct.

June asked for the count-in. Four young voices and drumstick taps could be heard before the guitar. That small beginning showed a band in a room, not a polished Ellis Hart recording.

They added six seconds of the count-in and removed six seconds elsewhere.

The credit would be spoken the first time the track appeared and written in every episode description. Mae's name came first as original songwriter and lead singer. Ellis, Simon, and June's contributions followed in the fuller note.

No one described this as a final legal settlement. It was permission for defined documentary use under agreed credit. Future release, performance, or recording would require its own decisions.

Mae signed last. When she placed her name beneath the use map, she felt less as if she were granting access than correcting the direction in which the song travelled. It would enter public hearing with her name attached before the first note.

The use agreement also covered what happened after the series ended. Northlight could keep the final programme and necessary production records, but it could not place isolated music clips in a general stock library. Future trailers for unrelated work could not use Mae's count-in as an attractive historical sound.

The audio platform asked for permission to normalise the volume across episodes. Tomas tested the process. Making the old track as loud as present interviews removed the sense that listeners were leaning towards something fragile. They kept the session slightly quieter while ensuring it remained clear.

Mae approved no dramatic sound under her departure. Asha had considered using the instrumental break there, linking Simon's music to the moment he failed to follow her. The connection was editorially powerful and unfairly certain. The break stayed in the musical episode.

June requested that the first drum entrance be audible on ordinary phone speakers. Tomas adjusted the surrounding guitar slightly, not the drums themselves. Simon's eight bars received a similar check through small speakers and headphones.

Each technical change returned them to questions of representation. A mix could make one contribution seem central simply by raising its level. The original studio balance was also a choice made by an unknown engineer, not a neutral truth.

They decided not to rebuild a perfectly equal mix. The documentary would use the archived balance with light restoration. The narration would name contributions that the old mix pushed back.

Saira added the documentary use agreement to the archive's restricted rights file, not the public catalogue. A future researcher would see that clips had been licensed but not receive participant contact details.

Mae asked for one physical copy of the final use map. She placed it beside the provisional credit and old Hart/Price box photograph. The green box now held a trail of changed decisions: omission, discovery, correction, and defined access.

"It is becoming an office," Iris said.

"Then the office should have better folders," Mae replied.

Kit made slim dividers from acid-free card supplied by Saira, but he did not touch the original pages. His contribution was practical and named only in the family inventory, not the song credits.

Mae laughed at how careful everyone had become around a box she once left in a cupboard for years. Care had arrived late. It could not repair time, but it could stop the correction from becoming another careless movement.

Chapter 26: Leon's Eight Bars

Leon listened repeatedly to Simon's instrumental break. It lasted only sixteen seconds on the archive track, eight bars between verses. For Leon, it carried the weight of representing a brother who could no longer explain himself.

He feared two opposite errors. If he claimed too much, he would repeat the old Hart/Price label by crowding Mae out. If he claimed too little, Simon's regret would erase the music he had genuinely made.

Asha recorded a second interview with him at the archive. Leon brought no family photographs. He spoke about growing up six years younger than Simon, waiting outside band practices, and believing his brother knew every song in the world.

Simon could be generous with instruments and ungenerous with attention. He taught Leon three guitar chords, then mocked him for playing slowly. He paid for Leon's first train trip alone but forgot to meet him at the other end.

"Death encourages people to edit a person into one stable character," Leon said. "Simon was not stable. None of us are."

The second metal-case reel marked JUNE DRUM TEST contained Simon trying versions of the instrumental line while June changed rhythm underneath. Mae and Ellis could be heard talking in another room. The fragment predated the final track by an hour, according to the studio voice.

It supported Simon's role clearly. It also showed June shaping the length: she stopped him after eight bars and said, "Back to Mae now." The phrase returned the arrangement to the original song.

June laughed when she heard it. She had forgotten saying anything so direct.

The documentary added ten seconds from this reel. Leon no longer needed narration to call Simon a co-writer of the whole track. Listeners could hear the break forming and June setting its edge.

Leon approved the wording "instrumental section by Simon Price, developed with June Bell." June accepted, though she still preferred her contribution within arrangement rather than composition. The fuller note explained both.

After the interview, Leon asked Saira whether Simon's reels could remain at the archive after the project. He was tired of storage boxes but did not want to donate material simply because it had become difficult to hold. Saira offered a renewable deposit: the archive could preserve the reels while ownership stayed with the estate.

Leon chose that option for five years. The green box remained with Mae. Simon's guitar remained with Leon. Not every object connected to the song had to gather in one institution.

For the first time, Leon heard the eight bars without treating them as a defence. They were simply good: a young guitarist finding a line, losing it, and finding a shorter version when the drummer told him where to stop.

Leon invited Mae to see Simon's guitar before he returned it to storage. She had avoided the instrument because it carried memories of Simon showing off during practice. Now it lay in an open case on Leon's dining table, older wood marked by years of use after Low Tide.

Mae recognised a scratch near the bridge. Simon made it when he tried to play with a coin after losing his pick. She remembered laughing until he threatened to remove her from the band. At the time, the threat was a joke. Later events changed its sound without proving he had meant more.

Leon asked whether the guitar should appear in documentary photographs. The series was audio, but Northlight planned a small web page. Mae thought the object might pull attention towards Simon's hands in the instrumental break. Leon wanted him represented as a musician, not only a silent man in an argument.

They offered Asha one approved image of the guitar in its case. No family room or storage label appeared. The caption named Simon's eight-bar section and the fact that Leon retained the instrument.

Mae did not hold it for the photograph. A reunion image of the original songwriter with the dead collaborator's guitar would create intimacy she did not feel.

Leon admitted that he had resented Mae's return at first. Simon had been locally remembered as the talented member who died too young. Correcting the credit introduced a story in which he helped exclude someone. Leon feared losing the kinder public version of his brother.

"You did lose part of it," Mae said. "So did I. I remembered Simon as agreeing with Roy immediately. The reel shows him talking about a cable while Roy speaks. That is not better, but it is different."

They discussed Simon's later letter. Leon once viewed it as a near apology on Mae's behalf. Mae saw it as an admission made safely to Ellis instead of her. Both readings remained.

Before she left, Leon played the eight bars on Simon's guitar. He knew only the rough shape from the reel and missed two notes. Mae sang the beginning of her next line over it. For a few seconds, the parts joined without claiming to recreate the studio.

They stopped before the moment became ceremonial.

Leon kept the guitar at home after all. The reels could rest at the archive, but this object belonged inside his imperfect memory of a brother who was funny, careless, gifted, and absent.

Chapter 27: The Invitation to Sing

Ellis planned a final season of live radio events before leaving his regular programme. The last event would take place in Northcross, with music and conversations from his career.

His producer wanted to address the Low Tide story. Ignoring it would seem evasive after months of headlines. Performing the song with Mae could appear generous and memorable. Ellis knew enough by then to distrust how perfect that image looked.

He asked Mae privately whether she wanted any involvement. He offered several choices: no mention of the song; a short factual statement; an archive representative discussing the discovery; or a shared performance if she actively wanted one.

Mae considered attending without singing. Iris feared the audience would read her presence as forgiveness. June feared her absence would make the event look like a duet story between the two visible singers. Leon did not want Simon represented by a photograph on a screen.

Mae asked Ellis what he wanted apart from public expectations.

"I want to sing it correctly once," he said. "By correctly, I mean with your name and voice first. But wanting that may still place my need for repair on your stage."

Mae appreciated the answer and refused the duet.

She proposed that Ellis speak about the credit correction without playing the track. The documentary would later allow people to hear their voices in context. His farewell event did not need to solve the story early.

Ellis accepted. During the broadcast, he described Mae as the original songwriter and lead singer. He named Ellis's verse, Simon's break, and June's arrangement. He said the old studio label had been wrong and that his own later silence had allowed it to remain wrong.

He did not call himself a thief or ask the audience to forgive him. He did not announce a reunion.

Mae listened from Lantern House with June and Iris. When Ellis moved to another song, Mae felt an unexpected sadness. Part of her had wanted to hear the old melody travel through a large room under her name.

"You could have said yes," June told her.

"I know. No was still my answer."

Choice did not remove the feeling of what was refused.

After the event, Ellis's producer sent Mae the full recording. The public clip used only his factual statement. Headlines called it an apology, though he had not used that word. Some praised Mae for making him accountable; she had not set his script.

Mae wrote to Ellis that he had spoken clearly. It was the first kind line she had sent him since the tape emerged. He replied with thanks and nothing more.

Their relationship was not restored friendship. It was becoming a form of honest distance in which neither person needed the other to perform an ending.

June watched a recording of Ellis's statement the next day. She appreciated that he named her arrangement, but she disliked his phrase "we failed Mae." The plural spread responsibility evenly across people who had held different power.

Mae saw the problem and another side. "We" prevented Ellis from presenting himself as the only important actor in the wrong. Roy, Simon, June, and the wider expectation around a male lead mattered. The phrase was incomplete rather than false.

Leon thought Simon's name should have been spoken directly in the admission. Ellis had named the instrumental contribution but not his later silence. The farewell event was Ellis's account, not a complete public hearing. Leon chose not to issue a competing statement.

The reactions showed why Mae had refused the stage. A four-minute segment could not carry every person's preferred balance. If she had sung, the image of reconciliation would have crowded out the words before and after it.

Still, the missed performance stayed with her. She dreamed of standing beside Ellis under bright lights, beginning the song alone, then hearing June and Simon enter. In the dream, Simon was young while everyone else was old. Mae woke angry at herself for wanting an impossible picture.

She told June, who understood. June had imagined playing drums at the event even though she supported Mae's no.

"Refusing a reunion does not mean we have no wish for one," June said.

They decided to hold the private Sea Glass session in memory as enough. It had not been recorded, so no one could replay it until it became a cleaner past.

Ellis sent the farewell set list to the archive with his spoken correction printed in full. Saira catalogued it as part of his career collection, linked to the Low Tide entry. The correction would remain visible beyond the broadcast clip.

Mae did not send her kind letter to the archive. It was a private step between them, not evidence that the story ended well.

The following Wednesday, Ellis visited Lantern House for tea without Mara, assistants, or folders. He paid at the counter like any day visitor. They spoke about Robin's piano and the poor sound at the old summer hall. Neither suggested music.

When he left, Mae realised she had spent twenty minutes with him without seeing the teenager at the studio door. Honest distance could sometimes become an ordinary table between two people.

Chapter 28: What the Series Left Out

The final edit removed almost as much strong material as it kept. Asha cut Ellis's story about Mae winning a school music prize because Robin could not confirm the year and the detail made her talent look like a simple path towards professional success.

She removed June's description of Roy's clothes. It was funny but turned a person who represented real power into an easy comic villain. Roy could not answer, yet the reel captured enough of his actions to describe them directly.

Mae asked to remove one angry sentence about Simon after Leon explained its effect. The sentence expressed her true feeling during the interview, but she had based it on a letter whose wider context she did not know. Asha would have kept it as clearly marked emotion; Mae chose not to make it public.

Leon did not ask her to pretend Simon acted well. He approved the section containing "let her cool down" and discussed how it changed his view of his brother.

Iris lost a passage about her father because Mae's boundary around David made it impossible to include enough context. The remaining fragment would have made him only the good man who believed Mae, flattening a marriage into support for the song.

The series also left out most of Nell's role. She appeared once in narration as the staff member who received Leon's blue postcard years earlier and connected the first green box. Her current friendship and employment did not need to become a thread.

Nell was relieved and slightly hurt. She had lived near the story's return for years. The feeling showed her why subjects sometimes fought for space even when a cleaner narrative benefited from less.

Asha gave every participant a final context review. They marked factual concerns, not preferences about pace or music. Mae challenged a transition that placed her statement "I built a life that did not need him" immediately before Ellis's successful radio years. The contrast implied she was defining herself against his fame. Asha moved the sentence into the Lantern House section where it belonged.

Ellis objected to the narrator saying he "accepted" Roy's proposal. He had not clearly spoken on the reel. Asha changed it to "did not stop the proposal before Mae left," then used Ellis's own admission that the convenient belief allowed him not to follow.

The final episode ended not with forgiveness, a performance, or the corrected catalogue. It ended with Mae opening Lantern House at six thirty while the young recording's count-in played softly. Her present voice said, "Recognition is not returning me to nineteen. It is letting what I made enter the life I actually have."

Asha worried the line was too complete. Mae had spoken it after weeks of thought, not in a spontaneous emotional moment. Completeness was not automatically false.

They kept it, followed by ten seconds of ordinary kitchen sound rather than music.

Chapter 29: Release Morning

Northlight released the four episodes on four consecutive Mondays. It published no surprise clips. The trailer used present-day voices, the archive count-in, and the stopped station clock sound from Orison, recorded separately and identified as part of the documentary design.

The promotional image showed four empty chairs around a microphone. It did not use Tom's photograph of Mae's empty stage position or June's diary. The title carried no famous name above the others.

On the first Monday, Mae served breakfast. She did not organise a listening event or watch download numbers. At ten, Farah arrived with bread, and Nell covered the desk while Mae walked along the harbour.

The first episode established Mae's life before playing thirty seconds of the song. Reviews noticed the slow approach. One called it frustrating; another praised the refusal to begin with scandal.

The second episode produced the strongest reaction. Roy's suggestion and Mae's departure could now be heard in sequence. Some listeners still demanded a simple verdict. Others argued over whether Ellis's missing words mattered. The series declined to fill the sound under the door.

Episode Three drew attention to June and Simon. Leon received messages from guitar players interested in the eight bars. June's former students wrote that they had never known their librarian played drums. She enjoyed those messages more than public sympathy.

The final episode about the leak made Northcross Sound Archive uncomfortable. Saira had insisted on including the failure, and the archive director publicly supported her account. The institution published its completed review and changes without claiming that new process could undo the release.

Ellis's audience divided. Some believed the series treated him too gently because he remained articulate and successful. Others thought it punished a teenager for an unclear collaboration. Ellis did not respond to every interpretation. He linked to the full series rather than selecting clips that made him look best.

Mae received hundreds of messages. Rosa screened those sent through Lantern House and passed on only those about the music, not guests or threats. One writer said Mae's refusal to use the word stolen had helped her think about her own old band. Another accused Mae of protecting institutions.

No response could tell Mae whether releasing the series had been right in every sense. Public recognition arrived mixed with strangers' confidence, commercial attention, and renewed pain.

Northlight paid participants under the agreement regardless of audience numbers. The series found a steady listenership rather than becoming a brief worldwide hit. Asha was pleased. Mara had hoped for larger numbers but admitted that a sensational title would have won the wrong argument.

On the fourth Monday, Mae listened to the final kitchen sounds alone in the real kitchen. The documentary version seemed quieter. A real delivery van stopped outside, the coffee machine hissed, and Room Five rang for an extra towel.

Mae closed the player and answered the bell. Release had not turned life into an ending.

Northlight monitored broad audience response but did not collect intimate listener stories for advertising without permission. The platform reported completion rates: many people finished Episode One, fewer stayed through the detailed credit discussion, and more returned for the leak episode.

Mara found the numbers discouraging. The section the team considered most nuanced had the largest drop. Asha argued that value was not measured only by keeping every listener. The credit episode existed for those willing to follow the complexity.

The platform proposed shorter companion clips to bring people back. Northlight made four factual previews under the agreed promotion plan. None used Mae's departure sentence. One featured June explaining arrangement, another Leon discussing Simon's eight bars, and another Saira on recordings preserving only part of a room.

The fourth used Mae saying, "I thought I had written a song. That felt different from being a songwriter." Listeners responded warmly, but some turned it into an inspirational quote about confidence. Mae accepted that public words travelled into meanings she had not intended, as long as Northlight had not stripped the relevant context.

Schools and music groups asked Asha to speak about the series. She accepted a few events but did not bring Mae's private materials. June attended one discussion at her former library and focused on keeping records rather than retelling the studio argument.

Ellis invited Asha onto his radio programme. She declined until the full release had settled. When she later appeared, she discussed editing power and allowed no song clips beyond the agreement. Ellis asked difficult questions without making himself the centre.

Mae gave one long written interview to Ruth Bell for a small literary journal. Ruth knew Lantern House but did not assume access. Mae chose writing because she could consider the exact words before publication. She spoke mainly about beginning to compose again, a fact not yet public.

The journal's headline was MAE CHEN AFTER THE LOST SONG. Mae asked for AFTER THE FOUND SONG instead. The editor accepted. "Lost" made absence the song's identity; "found" allowed movement beyond it.

At the end of the release month, the false theft headline still appeared in some searches. The documentary had not replaced every simple account. It had created a fuller one people could reach.

Mae stopped checking. A public record did not require her daily attention to remain available.

Chapter 30: The Whole Track

Six months after the series, the participants agreed that the full Coast session could be heard in the Northcross archive listening room. It would not be available for download. Visitors would receive the full catalogue note before listening, including the provisional nature of the credits.

Mae had once feared that restricted access would make the song disappear again. Now she valued a place where people could hear it deliberately rather than meeting a clip in a feed.

The archive held a small opening evening. Seats were limited, and participants chose their own guests. No livestream was made. Saira introduced the recording in three minutes, then turned off the room lights.

The count-in began. Mae sat beside Iris and June. Ellis sat across the aisle with his wife. Leon held Simon's old set list in his pocket. Robin listened with his eyes closed.

For four minutes and twelve seconds, no present voice explained the past. The young band played. The middle still seemed about to fall over, then held. Mae's final melody bent towards Ellis's harmony. June's drums made space. Simon's eight bars returned them to the verse.

At the end, young Mae laughed and said, "The middle still falls over."

Ellis answered, "Then I will hold it up."

In the documentary, those words might have sounded like a warning about power. In the room, after hearing the whole track, they also sounded like two musicians solving a piece together. Both meanings existed because the future had changed the past without altering the tape.

The audience did not clap immediately. When they did, Mae felt they were applauding the recording, not granting a verdict.

Afterwards, a young songwriter asked Mae whether she planned to release the track. Mae said not now. The archive hearing was enough for this stage.

Another visitor asked Ellis if he regretted the lower harmony. He said no. He regretted what followed, not the musical choice. Mae agreed. Removing every trace of his contribution would not restore her original song; it would create another false object.

June stood beside the archive display. Her diary remained in Port Amber. Only agreed scans of three pages appeared, with unrelated lines covered. She told Saira that she might deposit it one day, but no decision had been made.

Leon renewed the reel deposit. He was surprised to feel relief rather than loss when he left the building without the metal case.

On the train home, Mae and Iris did not discuss credits. They shared a packet of biscuits and complained about the cold carriage. The full song had finally played under Mae's name. It did not need to occupy every mile afterwards.

The archive opening generated new access requests. One university researcher wanted to compare the track with Ellis's later melody. A local school wanted to bring a music group. A commercial producer asked to include the whole song in a programme about forgotten recordings.

The listening-room agreement allowed individual research visits but not group playback or reuse. Saira explained the difference to each requester. The school could study the public documentary and visit an archive exhibition without hearing the restricted full track as a class. The producer would need a separate discussion with all relevant parties.

Mae said no to the commercial programme. Its outline grouped her song with recordings "rescued from obscurity," as if discovery by a larger audience gave the work value. She had chosen the archive room for attentive access, not as a waiting stage before wider release.

The university researcher received a listening appointment after agreeing to the room conditions. Her later paper described melodic similarities but did not decide intention from four notes. Mae read the relevant section before allowing a short quote from her interview. She disagreed with one interpretation and still permitted it because the factual use was clear.

June attended the school exhibition in Port Amber instead. She brought copies of her public diary page and spoke about how ordinary record-keeping can become important. She did not present herself as a model of perfect preservation. The pages had survived because she kept everything, including shopping lists and bad poems.

Ellis asked the archive for his own supervised listening appointment months after the opening. He wanted to hear the full track without an audience. Saira offered the same room and conditions as for other participants.

Mae happened to be at the archive that day for a catalogue review. They met in the corridor. Ellis invited her to listen with him, then immediately said she need not.

Mae chose not to enter. She had heard the track with him at Sea Glass and with the archive audience. Ellis needed one encounter that did not depend on her reaction.

While he listened, Mae and Saira reviewed a spelling correction in June's former name. The work was small and necessary. Through the wall, no music could be heard.

Ellis emerged after five minutes. He said only that the young band sounded better than he remembered. Mae replied that they also sounded less finished. Both statements were true.

They took separate trains home. The archive kept the track available under limits that no longer required all four living people to gather each time. Recognition had become a durable practice, not a single emotional evening.

Chapter 31: A Second Song

Winter made Lantern House quiet. Mae closed two rooms for painting and served breakfast to only four guests most mornings. June visited for a week without any documentary work attached.

One evening, Robin played the old song on the dining-room piano. Mae stopped him after the first verse.

"I know how that one ends," she said. "Play something else."

Robin played three simple chords. Mae found a fourth. June tapped a spoon against her cup, then laughed when Mae gave her a warning look.

The next morning, Mae wrote four lines on the back of an old shopping list. They were not about a late traveller, a band, or a missing name. They described yellow flowers left on an empty breakfast table after a guest departed early.

She nearly threw the paper away. Instead, she put it in the desk drawer beside kind guest notes.

Over several weeks, the lines became a song. Mae did not announce it. She changed words while waiting for coffee and found a melody while carrying towels. June heard one part over the phone. Robin suggested a chord that Mae rejected. Iris heard the chorus and said nothing until Mae finished.

"Is it yours?" Iris asked.

"At the moment."

Mae had learned that collaboration did not begin only when a studio tape rolled. June's rhythmic suggestion and Robin's rejected chord still affected how she listened. She wrote their helpful responses in her notebook without deciding they owned the song.

She called it After Breakfast.

At Farah's birthday dinner, Mae sang it for Nell, Kit, Farah, Jamie, Iris, June, Robin, Rosa, and Tom. There was no stage. Farah's round table had moved to a larger flat above Harbour Kitchen, but the four old spoons remained in its drawer.

Mae's voice was lower than at nineteen. The song needed no high notes. It ended with a person carrying a yellow flower into the street because leaving it behind felt wasteful.

No one recorded the first performance. When it ended, June asked permission before tapping a possible rhythm. Mae listened and said the second half suited it, not the first.

"Write that down," June said. "I am not keeping another diary for you."

Mae wrote the date, title, first-performance names, and June's suggested rhythm in a new music book. She was not creating evidence for a future dispute. She was giving ordinary care to work she intended to keep making.

Iris cried quietly, then denied it. Farah brought cake. Jamie asked whether the song needed a turning wooden flower for live shows. Kit told him no object needed a motor simply because he could build one.

Nell watched Mae close the music book and leave it on the table. It was not locked in a green box.

The next morning, Mae entered After Breakfast in a simple song register under her own name. She listed the date, lyric version, melody draft, and June's later rhythmic suggestion. She did not do this because she expected another dispute. The act acknowledged that creative work deserved ordinary records before a crisis made them urgent.

June wrote a short note agreeing that her rhythm was an arrangement contribution, not a claim to the song. Mae kept it with the music page. Their clarity did not make the friendship cold. It allowed them to experiment without leaving every meaning for later memory.

Robin asked whether his rejected chord deserved a negative credit. Mae wrote "Robin suggested one terrible chord" in the private rehearsal notes. He claimed this was an attack on his professional reputation.

Humour entered where fear had once been. They could name contributions without treating every name as a battle.

Iris arranged a clean recording of After Breakfast at Sea Glass Theatre. Mae chose a local engineer and kept Northlight outside the project. June played the agreed rhythm. Robin used a different chord. The young guitarist followed Mae's demo.

Before recording, they wrote the display credit and fuller performance note. Mae's name stood alone as songwriter. All performers and arrangement contributions were visible. Everyone read the line before the microphones turned on.

The session still produced uncertainty. June changed one fill on the final take. The guitarist suggested holding the last note. Mae accepted both. They recorded those choices in the session log without reopening the whole concept of authorship.

The finished track went to a small local music platform under Mae's own account. She allowed Lantern House to link to it but did not use guest email addresses for promotion. People who came for rooms had not agreed to become a music audience.

Ellis bought the track. He sent no public endorsement until Mae asked whether he would share it. She decided she did not need his larger audience to make the release real.

After Breakfast found a few hundred listeners. Farah played it before Harbour Kitchen opened. Jamie put it in a Bayline van playlist until his colleagues complained about hearing the same song. Kit listened in the workshop while making a frame for Mae's new music book.

The new recording did not sound important in the way the archive tape did. That was its freedom. No one needed it to prove what had happened decades earlier. It was present work entering the world with care and then becoming music in ordinary rooms.

Chapter 32: Light Above the Door

One year after the archive opening, Lantern House held a small evening for Mae's new songs. It was not a Low Tide reunion or a documentary event. Tickets were limited to the dining room, and the income covered the musicians and room closure.

Mae had written three more pieces after After Breakfast. June played a small drum set. Robin joined on piano. A young guitarist from Morrowbay played the parts Mae asked for and received a clear performer credit. Ellis attended as a guest and did not go on stage.

The old home-light song was not on the programme. Mae no longer needed every musical evening to return to it.

Nell and Kit sat beside Farah and Jamie. Farah now managed the full early kitchen team at Harbour Kitchen. Jamie had completed his supervisor course but still repaired vans two days each week. Iris managed the event with a light hand, allowing her mother to change the order once without rewriting the whole schedule.

Mae began with After Breakfast. Yellow flowers stood on each table. When she reached the final line, someone outside opened the blue front door, and the brass lamp moved in the wind.

For Nell, the sight carried every earlier version of the house: her first wet arrival, actors at breakfast, a film camera, reporters in the square, June's suitcase, and the green box on the dining table. None of those versions had become the only story of the place.

During the break, Ellis spoke to Mae near the piano. He had brought no proposal. He told her the new songs sounded like work she had been doing for years rather than a late return.

"Perhaps I was," she said. "Running this house taught me rhythm."

They stood in comfortable silence for a moment. Then Ellis went back to his seat.

The final song was about a town clock that people used for meetings even though it ran two minutes slow. Mae had written it after watching strangers wait beneath it. June's rhythm entered on the second verse. Robin missed one chord and found his way back.

After the last note, the audience clapped. Mae thanked each musician by name. She also thanked the people serving tea, including Rosa and Nell. Recognition had become a present action rather than only a correction of the past.

Beatrice Rowe had travelled from Westmere with Elin. She knew Mae through Nell and the documentary, but they had met only twice. Beatrice brought a yellow flower from her garden and gave it to Mae after the music.

"Your breakfast song made me think of my kitchen table," she said. "Not the film table. Mine."

Mae understood the distinction. A song could enter another person's memory without claiming the memory as its source.

Anya and Lucas came from Orison Bridge. The fictional ticket still sat below their stopped station clock. Anya told June that the second song's rhythm sounded like a train without copying one. June accepted this as a better compliment than being told she kept perfect time.

Ruth Bell sat near the garden door with a new notebook. She wrote nothing during the songs. Mae had asked the audience to keep the evening unrecorded, and Ruth decided that included her usual habit of catching phrases. She could remember the feeling without carrying exact lines into her next novel.

At the back, Saira and Asha listened as private guests. The archive and documentary had helped the old recording travel with context. Neither institution had produced Mae's new work. Asha felt the unfamiliar relief of hearing a song she did not need to edit.

The small crowd also contained people who knew none of the long history. Two regular Lantern House guests bought tickets because they liked local music. A young couple came after hearing After Breakfast online. For them, Mae was not first a recovered voice. She was the woman singing four current songs in a warm room.

Ellis left before the close group moved to the kitchen. He thanked June and Robin, shook Kit's hand, and said goodnight to Mae at the front desk. No reporter waited outside, and no photograph marked the departure.

Mae placed Beatrice's yellow flower in a narrow glass beside the new music book. By morning it would stand on a breakfast table. Its journey into the room had no label, agreement, or dispute, only a gift freely given and used for the purpose Mae understood.

Later, when the guests had gone, the close cast gathered in the kitchen. The green box remained locked in the music cupboard, preserved but no longer lonely. Mae's new music book lay open beside the cups.

Nell stepped outside with Kit. The harbour was dark, and the Market Square clock had just struck eleven, probably two minutes late.

Above them, the brass lantern lit the blue door. Years before, Mae had written about a traveller seeing a light and knowing someone had waited. Nell had once thought becoming part of that light meant staying in one place.

Now she understood it differently. The light did not hold anyone still. It gave departures and returns a visible point in the night.

Kit took her hand. Inside, Farah laughed at something Jamie had said. June tested a soft rhythm on the table. Mae's lower voice joined Iris's.

Nell closed the blue door against the cold but left the lamp burning.
