Chapter 1: The Company Wakes

The first theatre breakfast began badly. Sami came down at half past six, although his room sheet said seven thirty. Benji wanted coffee, but the large coffee pot broke again. Lydia could not find the milk she had requested.

Nell moved between the kitchen and dining room with three small pots. She remembered her first busy breakfast at Lantern House. That morning had felt like a storm. This one felt like a storm with actors in it.

"I put your milk on the top shelf," Mae told Lydia. "It has your name on it."

Lydia returned from the guest fridge with the bottle. "I looked at the door, not the shelf. I am sorry."

At seven, Iris arrived with a pile of new schedules. She had slept in Nell's old attic room. Her hair was still wet, and one shoe was open.

"The theatre needs everyone by eight," she said. "Casper has moved the first meeting."

Sami looked at his phone. "The plan says nine."

"That was yesterday's plan."

"I liked yesterday's plan."

Iris put the new pages beside each cup.

SEA GLASS THEATRE - TUESDAY
8:00 Company meeting
8:30 Stage walk
9:15 First reading
11:00 Clock test
1:00 Lunch

Dev, the stage carpenter, read the page while he ate toast. "The clock cannot be tested at eleven. Its moving part is still in Redwater."

"Kit is bringing it on the afternoon train," Nell said.

Iris crossed out the clock test. "Then we will do costumes at eleven and test the clock tomorrow."

The company left Lantern House in small groups. Nell gave each person a simple town map. The theatre was only a ten-minute walk away, but Benji turned left instead of right.

Nell called after him. "The sea should be behind you."

He turned around. "I was checking the other way."

When the hall was quiet, Mae sat down with a cup of tea.

"Seven adults," she said. "They are harder to move than twenty wedding guests."

"Wedding guests know where the wedding is."

At ten, Iris called. Casper had changed the afternoon again. He wanted photographs of the cast at four. The costume boxes had arrived, but one box was missing. Nell checked the delivery papers from the station. Both boxes had been there the night before.

She went to the back room. The first box stood beside Dev's empty clock bag. The second was behind the spare breakfast chairs, where Benji had put it during the room changes.

Nell called Iris. "I found it. No one needs to send another box."

"Bring it at eleven, please."

Nell carried the box through the wet streets. At the theatre door, a new sign had appeared.

THE CLOCKMAKER'S DAUGHTER
COMPANY REHEARSALS
NO PUBLIC ENTRY

Nell had thought Lantern House was busy. Behind that door, another kind of day was beginning.

The company returned in the evening at different times. Dev came first because he wanted to check the long clock bag. Sami and Owen arrived together, still arguing about one line in the play. Lydia stopped outside to finish a phone call.

Benji was last. He had walked to the wrong end of the harbour after practice. His paper map was wet, and one corner had torn away.

"I followed the theatre sign," he said.

"The sign points towards the theatre from the station," Nell replied. "It does not point home from every street."

She gave him a dry map and marked Lantern House with a blue circle. Then she marked his new room, the theatre, station, and late shop. Benji put the map inside his green case.

At dinner, the company filled two long tables. Mae served vegetable pie. Casper sat at one end and read notes while he ate. Iris took the pages away when his fork stopped moving.

"The working day is over," she said.

"I only need to change tomorrow's first scene."

"Then change it after you have eaten."

Lydia told Mae about the empty stage. She had worked in large theatres, but she liked the low roof at Sea Glass because the audience would feel close.

"Close audiences can hear everything," Sami said. "Even a hungry stomach."

Mae gave him another piece of pie.

After dinner, Dev opened the back-room door and found one of the clock pieces leaning against his bag. It had been moved when Nell searched for the costume box.

"Nothing is broken," he said, "but these parts must stay flat."

Nell cleared a space on a low shelf. She and Dev put the wooden pieces there with cloth between them. She added a card to the door.

CLOCK PARTS - DO NOT MOVE
Ask Dev or Nell before using this shelf.

The card did not make the back room larger, but it made the shared space clearer.

When the actors went upstairs, Owen returned to the desk. He had left his script in the dining room. Nell helped him look under the tables.

They found it on Robin's old chair by the piano. A pencil marked every page. Owen had written Lydia's lines as well as his own.

"You already know her part," Nell said.

"I know the words. That is not the same as being ready."

He put the script under his arm and went to Room Four. Weeks later, Nell would remember that sentence.

Chapter 2: The Empty Stage

Iris met Nell inside the theatre. She wore a headset and carried a pencil behind one ear.

"Leave the costume box in the workroom," she said. "Then I will show you the building."

The Sea Glass Theatre was smaller than the summer hall. It had two hundred red seats, a narrow stage, and a low roof. From the audience, the stage looked flat. From the side, Nell saw ropes, lights, doors, and dark spaces.

"Do not walk onto the stage without telling me," Iris said. "People may be moving scenery."

She took Nell through a blue door. Behind it, Farah was checking coats on a long rail. She had agreed to help the costume maker for six weeks.

"This one has no buttons," Farah said, holding up a brown coat.

"It had buttons in Redwater," Iris replied.

They found three buttons at the bottom of the costume box. Farah put them in a small labelled bag.

Nell followed Iris to the stage. The actors sat in a circle with papers. Casper Wynn, the director, stood in the middle. He was tall and spoke very softly, so everyone became quiet to hear him.

The play told the story of Mara, a young woman who repaired clocks in her father's shop. Her father wanted her to sell the shop, but she wanted to finish a clock her mother had designed. Most of the story happened during one long winter night.

"The clock is not just furniture," Casper said. "It carries the family's lost time."

Dev looked at Nell. "It also carries four heavy wheels."

Lydia played Mara. Sami played her brother, and Owen was their neighbour. Owen was also Lydia's understudy. If Lydia could not perform, he would play Mara with a few small changes.

During lunch, Nell sat with the company in the theatre kitchen. It was colder than the kitchen at Lantern House. Sami tried to warm his soup in an old machine, but the machine stopped after ten seconds.

"Everything in this theatre needs repair," he said.

"Kit will be happy," Nell replied.

In the afternoon, she helped Iris check local travel. A bus would take the company to a school for one afternoon show in October. The bus company had written the wrong time on its letter.

"The school day ends at three fifteen," Nell said. "A bus at three will not work."

Iris called and changed it to four. She wrote the new time on every copy of the plan.

At four, a photographer arrived. The actors stood on the empty stage in their everyday clothes. The real costumes were not ready.

"Look as if you have just heard a clock stop," Casper told them.

Nell watched from the side. No clock stood on the stage, but Lydia's face changed. She looked into the empty air as if something important had ended.

For the first time, Nell understood how a room with no walls could become another place.

Before leaving, Iris showed her the company office. It was hardly an office at all. A narrow desk stood between boxes of old programmes. Four keys hung on hooks, and a white board covered most of one wall.

The board listed every person who should be inside the theatre that day. A green mark meant present. A black circle meant expected later. Iris changed Kit's circle to FRIDAY because his train part would not arrive until then.

"This list is about the building, not about watching people," she explained. "If we close at night, I need to know that no one is still working alone in a back room."

Nell noticed Benji's name was missing. He had joined late, and the printed list had not changed. Iris added him before he returned from lunch.

Then the theatre phone rang. A man said he was Lydia's friend and wanted her practice times. Nell knew Lydia was staying at Lantern House, but she did not give the caller any details. She took his name and number for Iris.

Lydia did not know the man. The number did not accept a return call. Iris told the company not to share daily movement plans outside the agreed team.

"The public show times are on the poster," she said. "Practice times are working information."

Lydia thanked Nell for not trying to be helpful to a stranger. The moment stayed in Nell's mind. At Lantern House, she often knew when guests slept, travelled, or ate. Knowing something did not make it hers to pass on.

At five, Nell helped close the office. She returned each key to its hook and checked the back-room list with Iris. Dev was still working, so the theatre did not close around him.

Outside, families walked towards the harbour. No one could see the half-built clock, the missing coat buttons, or the names on the white board. The theatre looked asleep, although work continued behind one high window.

Nell carried the costume box label back to Lantern House. She put it with the delivery paper instead of throwing it away. The two numbers matched. If a box disappeared later, the small piece of card would help show where it had begun.

Chapter 3: The Last Part

Kit's train arrived at three forty-five. Nell waited by the station clock. She had not seen him for twelve days.

He came through the gate with a flat wooden case. The blue glass bead hung from the handle.

"You brought it back," Nell said.

"I said I would."

They wanted to stop and talk, but Iris needed the clock part before five. They carried the case down the hill together.

Kit told Nell about the Redwater theatre. He had finished six doors and a curved wall. The work was difficult, but the team had asked him to stay for another project in spring.

"Did you say yes?" Nell asked.

"Not yet. The dates are not clear."

At Sea Glass Theatre, Dev opened the case. Inside was a wooden circle with small metal wheels behind it. Kit had made it match the old clock face.

"Turn this handle," Dev said.

The hands moved from six to midnight. At midnight, a tiny door opened above the clock face. A wooden bird came out.

Farah clapped. "That bird needs a coat."

"It is a bird," Kit said.

"It is also on stage."

Casper came over. He watched the clock move twice. Then he asked if the bird could stay out for three more seconds.

Dev looked at Kit. Kit looked at the wheels.

"Yes," Kit said, "but not today. I need a different spring."

Casper wanted it by Friday. Kit planned to return to Redwater on Sunday night, so he agreed to change it at his Morrowbay workshop.

At six, Nell and Kit finally left the theatre. Rain had stopped, and the harbour was bright under the evening sun.

"I thought you were home for the weekend," Nell said.

"I am. Rosa has already planned Sunday lunch for ten people."

"Farah is making dinner tonight."

"Of course she is."

They walked to Nell's flat. Farah had put paper birds around the kitchen again. One hung above Kit's chair.

"That is cruel," Kit said.

"It is theatre," Farah replied.

Mae, Rosa, and Tom joined them. Jamie appeared on Farah's laptop for a short video call. His garage would close in November, and he was looking for work. Kit told him that a Morrowbay repair company needed a van mechanic.

"Send me the name," Jamie said. "I will look at it."

Later, when everyone had gone, Nell walked Kit to Rosa's house. They stopped in Market Square under the clock.

"Two weeks felt long," Kit said.

"It was twelve days."

"That felt long too."

He kissed her below the clock. This time, no sea bird interrupted them.

Chapter 4: Changes in Pencil

By the end of the first week, Nell had three different work plans. Lantern House used a black book. The theatre used printed pages, email, and a board by the stage door.

Iris wrote changes in pencil because the plans changed every day.

On Thursday morning, the board said that Lydia and Sami would practise Scene Four at ten. At nine thirty, Casper moved Scene Four to the afternoon. Sami had already changed into heavy boots for it.

"I could have stayed in bed," he said.

"You had breakfast at seven," Iris replied. "You were not in bed."

Nell's main job was to keep the house and theatre information together. When Casper planned an early practice, Mae needed to know how many breakfasts to prepare. When a guest moved rooms, Iris needed the new room number.

Nell made one clear daily note.

FRIDAY CHANGES
Lydia and Sami: breakfast at 6:45
Dev: collect new spring at 8:30
Benji: move to Harbour Flat after rehearsal
Company meal: theatre kitchen, 6:00
Late return to Lantern House: about 9:30

She put one copy in the Lantern House kitchen and one by the theatre door. She sent a picture of it to Iris.

At noon, Casper saw the note. "The company meal is at seven, not six."

"The cafe will deliver at six," Nell said. "That is the time in your order."

Casper found the order on his phone. He had written six.

"Then we will eat at six," he said. "The note is right."

Iris gave Nell a quick smile. She had argued with Casper twice that morning.

After lunch, Benji moved to the room near the theatre. Nell helped him carry his green case. His new landlady, Mrs Lowe, gave him two keys and a page of house information.

"The blue key is for the street door," she said. "The silver one is for your room. The kitchen is shared. Please put your name on food."

Benji looked at the quiet room. "I will miss breakfast."

"Lantern House is two streets away," Nell said. "You can pay for breakfast when there is space."

On the way back, she stopped at Kit's workshop. He was changing the clock spring. The wooden bird lay on the table beside him.

"Does it stay out longer now?" she asked.

"Three seconds longer. Casper may ask for four tomorrow."

Nell sat on a stool and watched the little wheels turn. She had spent all week changing times on paper. Kit was changing time inside a wooden clock.

"The theatre is strange," she said.

"Yes. That is why I like it."

On Saturday morning, Nell checked all three copies of the plan. The theatre board said the company would finish at five. The Lantern House kitchen copy said six. Iris's phone picture showed five thirty.

The difference mattered because Mae had ordered dinner for six fifteen. If the actors came back at five, they would wait. If they came at six, the food might become cold.

Nell called Iris before breakfast ended.

"Which finish time is real?"

Iris looked at Casper's latest notes. He had added a short meeting at five. The company would leave around five thirty.

Nell changed the kitchen page and theatre board. Then she called the food shop and confirmed delivery at six. All three plans finally agreed.

At noon, Casper ended practice early. Lydia had remembered every scene, and the clock part was not there to test. The company left at four forty-five.

Sami reached Lantern House and asked for dinner at once.

"The food comes at six," Nell said. "That is still the plan."

"But we are early."

"The cook is not early."

Mae made tea and put bread on the table. Sami waited with everyone else. The food arrived at six, hot and complete.

Later, Iris apologised for the different pages. "I changed one copy and thought I had changed the plan."

"A plan is not changed until the people who use it know," Nell said.

Iris raised an eyebrow. "That sounds like Mum."

"I work with her."

On Sunday, Nell had no theatre work. She and Kit walked along the beach before his evening train. The wooden bird spring was finished, and the clock pieces were safe at Lantern House.

They found a quiet place among the stones. Kit drew the clock wheels in Nell's small notebook. He explained how one wheel moved another, but Nell understood only half of it.

"You do not have to understand every part," he said. "You only need to know which part depends on the next one."

Nell looked at the drawing. It was like the theatre plans. Breakfast affected practice. Practice affected dinner. A train delay changed a costume test. Every small time touched another one.

At the station, Kit gave her the pencil. "For changes."

Nell kept it in her theatre bag until it became too short to use.

Chapter 5: A Voice in the Hall

During the second week, Lydia began to speak more quietly. At first, everyone thought she was saving her voice. Then she stopped in the middle of a scene and asked Iris for a break.

"My throat hurts," she said. "I cannot make the high notes."

The play included one short song. Lydia had to sing it alone beside the clock.

Iris moved the afternoon work to scenes without Lydia. Lydia contacted her own clinician and followed the personal guidance she received. She returned to Lantern House and rested in Room Three.

Mae put a note outside the room so cleaners would not knock. She left a jug of water on a table in the hall.

Owen sat in the dining room with Lydia's script. As understudy, he knew most of her lines, but he had never played the full part on stage.

"Casper wants me to practise tomorrow," he told Nell. "He says it is only a careful plan. It does not feel careful to me."

"It feels real now," Nell said.

Owen nodded. He had joined the company as a neighbour in the play. He did not expect to become the main character.

After dinner, he practised lines with Sami in the sitting room. They spoke softly because Lydia was upstairs.

"You cannot sell the shop," Owen said as Mara.

"It is not only your choice," Sami replied as her brother.

Robin's old chair stood beside the piano. Owen sat in it while he read the next page. Mae listened from the hall.

"He understands the line," she told Nell.

"Which line?"

"The one about a house holding time. He looked at the room before he said it."

The next morning, Lydia felt no better. She could speak, but not for a full day. Casper asked Owen to take her place in practice until Monday.

Owen put on Lydia's brown coat. Farah moved two buttons and shortened the sleeves with temporary stitches. The coat looked different on him, but it still belonged to Mara.

On stage, Owen missed one entrance. He turned the wrong way after the song. He forgot where to put the clock key. Each time, Iris stopped, marked the page, and began again.

At lunch, Owen looked tired.

"I am making every mistake," he said.

"Not the same mistake," Farah replied. "That would be less useful."

Owen laughed. In the afternoon, he found the right door without looking down. The scene continued all the way to the clock striking midnight.

Chapter 6: The Brown Coat

Farah took the brown coat home on Friday. The temporary stitches were not strong enough for a full show. She needed to change the lining without cutting the original cloth.

Nell found her at the round table after work. Thread, pins, and pieces of paper covered it.

"Do not put tea near the coat," Farah said.

Nell moved her red cup to the kitchen counter. "How long will this take?"

"Two hours if it goes well. All night if it becomes angry."

The coat had to fit Lydia and Owen. Farah made small hidden ties inside it. She could change the shape quickly before a show.

Kit called from Redwater while Farah worked. Nell put the phone on the empty end of the table.

"I can see only a brown wall," he said.

"That is the coat."

"Where is Nell?"

Nell moved into the picture. Kit was eating dinner in his rented room. Behind him, the blue glass bead hung from a lamp.

"The theatre asked me to stay until December," he said. "The wall panels are late."

He had planned to finish in November. Another month was not long, but Nell had been counting the weeks.

"Will you still come home every second weekend?" she asked.

"Yes. I may miss the first weekend in October."

That was the weekend of the theatre's first public preview. Kit had built the clock part, but he might not see it move for an audience.

"The show runs until November," Nell said. "There will be other nights."

Farah did not look up from the coat. She knew Nell was disappointed.

After the call, Nell helped cut paper labels for the hidden ties. Farah marked one L and one O.

"Distance is dull," Farah said. "It is not a great enemy. It is just trains, tired calls, and different plans."

"That does not make it easy."

"No."

At eleven, the coat was ready. Farah hung it from the kitchen door. The sleeves looked normal, and the hidden ties could not be seen.

The next day, Owen tried it at the theatre. Then Lydia came in for a short visit and tried it too. It fitted both of them.

"How did you do that?" Iris asked.

Farah smiled. "I did not ask the coat to choose."

Chapter 7: Water on the Floor

Heavy rain fell for three days. On Monday morning, Iris opened the theatre and heard water dripping.

The back practice room had a wet floor. Water had come through a damaged part of the roof. Two boxes of paper scenery were near the wall.

Iris called Dev and the theatre manager. They moved the dry boxes first. The manager closed the room and contacted the building team.

At eight, the company arrived. Their main stage was not available because lighting work was happening there. The wet practice room could not be used.

"We need another space until Thursday," Iris said.

Casper walked around the theatre kitchen. It was too small. The summer hall had an art sale. The school hall was busy.

Nell thought of the empty dining room at Lantern House. Breakfast ended at nine thirty. The tables could move to the sides.

"You could read and practise small scenes there," she said. "Not the clock scene. The floor is not a stage."

Mae agreed to one day first. The company carried scripts and two chairs back to Beacon Lane. Nell put a notice at the dining-room door.

DINING ROOM CLOSED 10:30-4:00 TODAY
Guest tea is available in the sitting room.

The actors began with quiet scenes. At first, they spoke as if they were still at breakfast. Casper asked them to imagine the walls of the clock shop.

Owen stood beside the piano. Sami entered through the garden door. Lydia watched from Robin's old chair. She was speaking normally again, but she had not returned to full practice.

At noon, a guest came downstairs with a book. Ruth Bell had returned to finish her railway story. She watched for ten minutes from the hall.

"Your neighbour enters too quickly," she told Casper.

Owen looked surprised. "Why?"

"He has come through snow. He would close the door before he spoke."

Casper asked Owen to try it. Owen entered, turned to an invisible door, and pushed it closed against an imagined wind. The scene suddenly felt cold.

"She is right," Casper said.

Ruth went back upstairs without another word.

The practice continued until four. No guest complained. Mae served afternoon tea in the sitting room and explained the unusual noise.

At the end, the actors put every table back in its place. Sami found a paper clock hand under Table Six. Benji had dropped it during a sound test.

"Theatre leaves strange crumbs," Mae said.

Chapter 8: One Room, Two Uses

The practice room roof needed more work than expected. The theatre manager said it would stay closed for a week.

Lantern House could not give up the dining room every day. Guests wanted to sit there after breakfast, and Mae needed time to prepare tables for the next morning.

Nell and Iris looked at the company plan. They divided the work by space. Large scenes would use the theatre stage in the afternoon. Quiet readings could happen in the Lantern House sitting room before lunch. Costume work would move to Farah's flat for two evenings.

Casper did not like moving between places.

"The company loses attention every time we walk down the street," he said.

"The company loses the whole morning if it waits for one perfect room," Iris replied.

They agreed to try the plan for three days.

On Tuesday, Lydia returned for half a day. She read at a table while Owen continued to practise her movement on stage. They shared notes instead of competing.

"You turn towards Sami before this line," Lydia told him. "It makes the argument warmer."

"Casper asked me to face the clock," Owen said.

"Then keep his choice. I only wanted you to know mine."

At Lantern House, Nell moved guest tea to the garden because the weather had cleared. Ruth Bell sat under the tree with her book. She did not mind the actors in the sitting room.

At one, a delivery arrived for the theatre. The driver saw the company name on the front desk and left two large lamps in the hall.

Nell checked the address before he left. The lamps belonged at Sea Glass Theatre, not Lantern House.

"I can take them there later," he said.

"They cannot stay in the hall," Nell replied. "The path must remain clear."

The driver returned the lamps to his van and delivered them to the correct door.

By Thursday, the new plan felt normal. Actors crossed Beacon Lane with scripts under their coats. Guests saw brown costume sleeves in Farah's flat window. Mae learned the first scene because she heard it so often.

When the theatre room reopened on Friday, everyone was pleased. They were also surprised to miss the warm dining room.

Ruth left a new note at the desk.

The neighbour now closes the door. My sisters have reached the railway station. We all made progress this week.

Ruth checked out on Saturday. Her case was heavier because it held the completed pages. She asked Nell to call a taxi for the eleven o'clock train.

At ten twenty, the taxi office said the car would be fifteen minutes late. The station was only a short walk away, but Ruth could not carry the case down the hill alone.

Nell was still on duty. Dev had come for breakfast and offered to pull the case. Ruth accepted after he promised not to open it and read the ending.

They left at ten thirty. The taxi arrived at Lantern House five minutes later and found no one. Nell cancelled it, but the office charged a small late fee under its clear booking terms.

Ruth came back at noon. The train had been cancelled, and the next one was at two. Dev had returned to the theatre, so she had brought the case up the hill herself in short stages.

"My sisters reached the station more easily than I did," she said.

Mae offered tea and kept the case behind the desk. Ruth paid the taxi fee because she had chosen to walk before the agreed pickup time. She did not argue about it.

While she waited, she watched the theatre company cross the street. Lydia and Owen were discussing the brown coat. Sami carried two paper lamps. Benji walked in the correct direction for once.

"A story looks very untidy before it is finished," Ruth said. "People expect writers to sit alone, but my book contains taxi drivers, noisy children, your garden, and an actor closing an invisible door."

"Will readers know?" Nell asked.

"No. They will only feel that the world around the sisters is full."

At one thirty, Ruth took a new taxi. This time, she waited in the hall until the driver rang the bell. Nell watched the car turn towards the station.

Three months later, a parcel arrived for Lantern House. Inside was Ruth's printed book, The Station at Winter End. On the first page, she had thanked "a house that was never truly quiet."

Mae placed it in the sitting room. Guests read it in the same chair where Ruth had watched the first practice.

Chapter 9: The Producer's Visit

Mara Voss, the producer, came from Northcross to watch a full practice. She paid for the production and managed its tour. Iris cleaned the planning table before she arrived.

Mara wore a red suit and carried a small silver notebook. She watched without smiling. When the practice ended, she asked everyone to stay.

"Advance ticket sales are lower than expected," she said. "The first Saturday is nearly full, but the weekday evenings are quiet."

The company needed enough ticket money to complete the twelve planned shows. Mara did not want to cancel anything, but she had to control costs.

Casper said the audience would come after the first reviews. Mara said reviews could also be late or poor.

"We will not make the show cheaper on stage," she said. "However, we must stop costs that do not help the audience."

She cancelled a second photographer and one planned company dinner. She asked Iris to check every travel order again. Then she looked at the Lantern House bill.

"Why are we paying for seven breakfasts on Mondays when the company travels home on Sunday night?"

Nell showed her the room record. "Only three people travel home. Four stay in Morrowbay. The bill is for four breakfasts, not seven."

Mara read the total again. She had looked at the number of rooms, not the number of breakfasts.

"You are right," she said. "The price includes four."

Mae had taught Nell to keep clear records. Now those records protected a cost that was real.

At lunch, Mara sat beside Nell in the theatre kitchen.

"Iris says you solve local problems," she said.

"I carry many boxes."

"That is often the same thing in theatre. Have you worked on a tour before?"

"No. I started at Lantern House in March."

Mara seemed surprised. "You pay attention. That is harder to find than experience."

After lunch, the company performed the first half again. The wooden bird came out of the clock and stayed for three seconds longer. Mara wrote something in her silver notebook.

Before she left, she gave Iris a new budget and a firm ticket target. If sales did not improve by opening week, the last two weekday shows might go.

The company watched her taxi leave.

"She did not say the show was good," Sami said.

"She stayed for the second half," Iris replied. "That is how Mara says it."

Chapter 10: A Face on the Poster

The first posters appeared around Morrowbay. Lydia's face was in the centre. Sami and Owen stood behind her. Above them, the wooden clock showed one minute before midnight.

THE CLOCKMAKER'S DAUGHTER
A new play at Sea Glass Theatre
Previews from 2 October
Opening night 6 October

Owen stopped outside the bakery to look at a poster. In the picture, he was the neighbour, not Mara. If Lydia could not perform, the poster would still show her face.

"That is normal," Iris told him. "The photograph was taken for the planned cast."

"I know. It only feels strange after wearing the coat all week."

Lydia was stronger and planned to return fully on Monday. Owen was glad, but part of him would miss the larger role.

On Saturday, Lydia and Owen practised together. They took turns as Mara while Casper watched. Their versions were different. Lydia moved quickly and showed her anger. Owen was quieter and made the character seem more afraid.

"Neither choice is wrong," Casper said. "The story changes its colour, but it remains the same story."

Farah adjusted the brown coat between each turn. The hidden ties worked, although one came loose after a fast movement.

Nell watched from an empty seat. Kit was working that weekend, so she sent him a picture of the clock. She did not photograph the actors during practice.

At the break, Owen sat beside her.

"I used to think an understudy waited for another person to fail," he said. "Now I see that the waiting is work."

"Will you be sad when Lydia returns?"

"A little. I will also be very glad. Both feelings can sit together."

After practice, they found a poster with a printing mistake. The theatre address said Sea Glass Street, but the theatre was on Harbour Street. Two hundred copies had already gone to cafes and shops.

Iris called the printer. New posters would take four days. Nell suggested a clear address label for the posters already in town.

Farah designed a small blue strip:

SEA GLASS THEATRE, 8 HARBOUR STREET

The company spent an hour putting strips over the wrong line. Even Casper helped. By evening, every poster in the town centre had the correct address.

The faces on the poster stayed perfect. The important small line below them had needed everyone.

On Sunday morning, Nell and Farah walked through town to check the address strips. Rain had lifted one corner from the poster outside the station. Farah replaced it with a new strip and pressed clear tape over the ends.

The cafe poster was hidden behind a menu board. Its owner moved it closer to the window. At the library, a children's event card covered Lydia's face, but the theatre dates and address were still easy to see. Nell left it there. The library did not exist only for the play.

Near Market Square, they found an old poster without a correction. It was inside a locked travel-shop window. The shop was closed until Tuesday.

Nell wrote the correct address on a small card and pushed it through the letter opening with a polite note. On Tuesday, the owner added the strip.

They stopped at Tom's flower shop. The blue boat was no longer in the window, but Tom had kept the Low Tide record on a shelf. He put the theatre poster beside it.

"Mae's old band and Iris's new play," he said. "This window has become a family wall."

Farah looked at the poster. "Mae will hate that sentence."

"Then do not tell her."

At lunch, they met Owen outside Harbour Kitchen. Two teenagers had asked him for Lydia's name because they recognised the poster. He had told them the show dates and nothing about where the cast stayed.

"It is strange to be known as the person behind another person," he said.

Farah gave him a warm roll. "The poster is one picture from one day. You have had many days since then."

Owen kept the paper bag folded in his script. Later, when he played Mara, the bag was still there between two pages.

That evening, a visitor arrived at Lantern House using the wrong address from the old poster. He was looking for the theatre, not a room. Nell showed him the correct street on the town map. He reached the show before the doors closed.

The printing mistake was small, but it had travelled. The blue strips slowly caught up with it.

Chapter 11: Monday at the Station

Lydia went home for the weekend and planned to return on the nine-ten train. At nine twenty, she sent Iris a message. Her train had stopped outside Eastmere because of a signal problem.

The company needed Lydia for a costume test at eleven. The costume maker was visiting for only one day.

Nell checked the travel update. Lydia's train would not reach Morrowbay before noon. Another train left Eastmere at nine forty-five by a different route, but Lydia was already on the stopped train.

"We cannot move her from one train to another," Nell said. "We need to move the test."

The costume maker could test Sami and Owen first. Lydia would come after lunch. Iris changed the plan and called the driver who was meeting Lydia.

At eleven thirty, the train began moving. It reached Morrowbay at twelve twenty. Nell waited at the station with a sandwich and the new schedule.

Lydia came through the gate carrying her brown travel bag.

"I have been sitting beside a field for three hours," she said. "I know every cow."

She ate the sandwich as they walked to the theatre. The costume test began at one. No time was lost because everyone had changed order.

That evening, the signal problem continued. Several guests called Lantern House because their trains were late. One couple would miss their first night.

Mae checked the booking terms. Their room had been held and could not be sold at the last minute, but she offered to move breakfast to the second morning and keep their bags after checkout.

Another guest decided to come by coach. Nell gave him the coach station address and told him the late bus time from the public timetable. She did not promise that the bus would wait.

Kit was also travelling from Redwater. His train ended at Northcross, so he had to stay there overnight. He called Nell from a small hotel room.

"I can come tomorrow afternoon," he said. "I am sorry."

"You did not stop the trains."

"I know, but I wanted to see you tonight."

Nell looked at the empty chair across from her at Farah's table. "I wanted that too."

They ate dinner together by video. Kit had a cold cheese sandwich. Farah had made vegetable pie for Nell.

"Your dinner is much better," he said.

"Yes. This is not one of those times when both choices are equal."

Chapter 12: The Clock Stops

The first full practice with scenery began on Wednesday. Dev rolled the clock to the centre of the stage. Benji checked the sound. Farah prepared the brown coat.

For the first hour, everything worked. Then the clock stopped at eleven fifty-eight. The handle would not turn.

Dev opened the back. One small wheel had moved out of place. He put it back, but it moved again during the next scene.

"The floor is not level," Kit said. He had arrived from Northcross that afternoon. "The clock leans when it rolls to this mark."

Dev placed a thin piece of wood under one wheel. The clock stood straight, but Casper did not want the piece visible.

"The audience will look at the actors," Iris said.

"At midnight, everyone looks at the clock," Casper replied.

Kit and Dev took the clock to the workshop after practice. They could not leave it broken with the first preview only ten days away.

Nell brought them food at eight. The clock stood in the middle of Kit's workshop. Its back was open, and metal parts lay on a white cloth.

"Can you fix it?" she asked.

"Yes," Kit said. "We need the front wheels to move separately. Then the clock can stay straight on an uneven floor."

They worked until nearly midnight. Nell sat by the small heater and updated the company room list. One actor would leave early on Friday. Mara Voss was coming back on Saturday. The paper work did not stop when the wooden clock did.

At half past nine, Rosa came to the workshop with a flask of tea. She had seen the lights while driving home. Kit told her that he and Dev would finish soon.

"You said that at seven," Rosa replied.

She looked at Nell's room list. "At least one person here is doing work that ends."

Nell had discovered another problem. Mara Voss had asked for Room Seven on Saturday, but a couple had already booked it for their anniversary. Room Nine was free, although it did not have the sea view Mara usually requested.

Nell wrote to Mara before the next day. She explained that Room Seven was unavailable and offered Room Nine at the normal price. She did not move the older booking because a producer seemed important.

Mara replied at six in the morning.

Room Nine is fine. I need a desk more than a view. Thank you for checking.

When Nell showed the message to Mae, Mae nodded. "A person with a larger bill does not own someone else's room."

At the theatre, Dev and Kit rolled the repaired clock across three parts of the stage. One part was almost level. Near the back wall, the clock leaned left. The new wheel movement kept its face straight in both places.

Casper asked them to mark the best path with tape. Iris asked if the tape could come off without marking the floor. Dev tested a small piece near the side first.

The clock moved through the whole scene that afternoon. At midnight, the bird door opened. The company clapped, but Dev raised one hand.

"It has worked once," he said. "That is not the same as always."

They tested it again during the break and at the end of the day. Nell wrote each result in the prop record. Five complete tests, no stops.

Kit left for Redwater before dinner. Nell walked him to the station with his empty wooden case.

"The case is lighter now," she said.

"My week is not."

He slept during most of the train journey. Nell knew because his message from Redwater said, MISSED MY STOP, CAME BACK ONE STATION, HOME NOW. She kept the message, not because it was funny, but because it showed how much his work was costing in tired hours.

At eleven forty, Kit turned the handle. The hands reached midnight. The little door opened, and the bird appeared.

Dev rolled the clock across the workshop's old floor. It stayed straight.

"Again," Kit said.

They tested it five more times. It worked each time.

Nell walked home with Kit. He was staying at Rosa's house for two nights before returning to Redwater.

"You fixed time," she said.

"Only wooden time. Real time is still a problem."

He meant the short days they had together. Nell took his hand.

"Then we will not spend tonight talking about tomorrow's train," she said.

They stopped at Farah's door. The kitchen light was on, although Farah had gone to bed. A plate of cake waited for them under a bowl.

Chapter 13: An Offer for Jamie

Jamie came to Morrowbay for a job interview at Bayline Repairs. The company looked after vans for local shops and theatres. Kit had given him the manager's name.

Jamie wore his only suit. It was too warm for the day, and he hated the tie.

"Do I look like I can repair a van?" he asked Nell.

"You look like you can sell one."

"That is not useful."

The interview was on an industrial street outside the town centre. Jamie took the bus. Nell wanted to go with him, but he said no.

"I can find a garage alone," he said.

While Jamie was away, Nell worked at the theatre. A rented van had arrived with lighting equipment. The driver gave her the delivery list. One box number was missing, and another number appeared twice.

Nell asked the driver to wait while Benji checked. They found all the correct boxes. The mistake was on the list, not in the van. Benji wrote the actual numbers before he signed.

"Jamie would like this place," Nell said. "There are always vans."

At two, Jamie sent a message: FINISHED. STILL ALIVE. NEED FOOD.

They met at Harbour Kitchen. Farah gave him a large sandwich and waited for news.

"They asked about the garage closing," Jamie said. "I told them the truth. I also explained the work I did when our manager was away."

"How did they seem?" Nell asked.

"Like people who need a mechanic. I will know on Friday."

He stayed at the flat that night. Peter called during dinner and asked twenty questions. Jamie answered the first six, then gave the phone to Nell.

"Dad, there is no news yet," she said. "We will call when there is."

On Friday morning, Bayline Repairs called. They offered Jamie a job from 1 November. The pay was a little better than his old job, but he would need to move.

Farah's sofa was available for two weeks. After that, he could rent Benji's room because the theatre sound assistant would leave after the show.

Jamie accepted the job. Peter was worried about both his children living in Morrowbay. Their mother, Helen, was pleased that they would be near each other.

"This does not mean you can bring car parts into our kitchen," Farah told Jamie.

"What about one small motor?"

"No."

Chapter 14: The First Preview

The first preview was not the official opening. The audience paid less, and the company could still make changes. It felt official to Nell because every seat was full.

Kit could not leave Redwater. Nell saved the seat beside her for Farah, but Farah had to stay with the costumes. Mae sat on Nell's other side. Robin had returned from Eastmere for one night.

At seven twenty, Iris asked the audience to take their seats. The lights became low. A bell rang.

The play began with Lydia alone in the clock shop. Snow moved across the back wall as white light. The wooden clock stood in shadow.

Nell had watched many practices, but the audience changed everything. People laughed at lines that had never seemed funny. They became silent when Sami opened his father's letter. One person coughed during Lydia's quiet song, then tried not to cough again.

In the second half, Owen entered through the wrong door. He looked surprised but continued. Lydia moved a chair to give him space. The change looked planned.

Then the clock reached midnight. Dev turned the handle from behind the wall. The hands moved. The little door opened.

No bird came out.

Nell held her breath. On stage, Lydia looked at the empty door. The missing bird was part of the story. Mara was supposed to catch it before it fell.

Lydia slowly reached into the clock and brought out an invisible bird. She held her empty hands as if they carried something small.

"She finished it," she said to Sami. "Mum finished it after all."

The audience accepted the invisible bird. Some people even cried.

After the final scene, the actors bowed. Everyone clapped for a long time.

Behind the stage, Dev found the wooden bird inside the clock. Its small door had caught on a piece of cloth. Farah removed the cloth, and they tested it again.

The audience stayed in the front hall for a short talk. Casper, Lydia, and Sami sat on high chairs. Iris collected written comments on plain cards. The company wanted to know where the story was clear and where people became confused.

One person wrote that she did not understand why the mother had hidden the clock design. Another thought the neighbour knew too much too early. A child drew the wooden bird and wrote, IT WAS HIDING.

Casper read every card. He did not agree with all of them. Still, he changed one line in the first scene so the hidden design was mentioned sooner.

Nell helped Iris put the cards into three groups: story, sound, and building. Several people had not heard Sami when he faced the clock. Benji could adjust his microphone, but Casper also asked Sami to turn a little towards the audience.

One building card said the side seats were cold. The theatre manager checked the heating times and found that one area warmed later than the rest. He changed the start time for the next evening.

No card asked for the wooden bird to appear in a different way. The audience had seen Lydia's hands and believed her. Casper decided not to make the problem part of the next show.

"A preview is not a promise that nothing will go wrong," Iris told the company. "It is a chance to see what the audience receives."

At Lantern House, the actors continued talking over late soup. Owen felt that his wrong door had made his next line better. Sami disagreed because it had left him on the wrong side of a chair.

Lydia listened, then said, "We solved it once. We do not have to keep the accident because it surprised us."

Mae was closing the dining-room curtains. "A useful sentence for many parts of life," she said.

Nell wrote it in the empty back page of her notebook. She did not know then how often a film team would later ask to keep a lucky accident.

Casper spoke to Lydia. "You did not look afraid."

"I was very afraid."

"Good. Mara was not."

Mara Voss checked the ticket report. The next two previews were almost full, and opening night had sold every seat. The last weekday shows were still uncertain, but she did not cancel them.

Nell called Kit from the empty theatre. She stood beside the clock while she described the night.

"Did the audience see the missing bird?" he asked.

"They saw one because Lydia did."

Chapter 15: Notes After Midnight

After the preview, Casper held a company meeting. It began at ten forty and continued past midnight.

He had notes for every scene. Some were small. Sami had to move one cup. Owen needed to wait before opening the door. Lydia should hold the invisible bird for one second longer, even when the real bird worked.

Iris watched the clock on the wall. The company needed rest before the next performance.

"Can the lighting notes wait until morning?" she asked.

"The lighting team starts at eight," Casper said.

"Then send them the exact changes tonight. The actors do not need to hear every light number."

Casper did not like being stopped, but he agreed. Benji and the lighting team stayed. The actors went back to Lantern House.

Nell had left soup and bread in the guest kitchen. Lydia ate in silence. Sami talked about the audience. Owen wrote down the door change before he forgot it.

"Did you hear the woman laugh in Scene Three?" Sami asked.

"Everyone heard her," Lydia said. "She was wonderful."

At one, Nell checked the front door and turned off the hall lamps. She was not working the next morning, but she still had to walk home.

Mae came out of her flat in a dressing gown. "Stay in the attic tonight," she said. "It is late and the room is empty."

Nell sent Farah a message and went upstairs. The attic looked different without her things. There was a new white lamp and a picture of a boat. The round window still showed the harbour.

She found Kit's old note in the desk drawer: No more indoor rain. The paint will be dry by Saturday.

Nell had forgotten to take it when she moved. She put it inside her phone case.

Before sleeping, she read the next day's plan. The company would meet at eleven, not eight. Iris had protected their morning.

At breakfast, Mae kept one table free until nine. Lydia came down first. She looked at the empty chair across from her.

"Last night, I held nothing in my hands," she said. "Two hundred people believed it was a bird. This morning, I cannot believe this cup is coffee."

Mae filled the cup. "Some things need less imagination."

Chapter 16: The Missing Bird

The second preview began with the wooden bird working perfectly. It came out at midnight, and Lydia caught it. The third preview also went well.

On opening day, the bird disappeared.

Dev opened the clock at noon. The small space above the face was empty. He had put the bird there after the last show. No one remembered taking it out.

The bird was not valuable, but Kit had made it for the clock. It also appeared in the most important scene.

Iris asked everyone to check their work areas. Farah searched the costume room. Benji looked in sound boxes. Nell checked Lantern House because Dev had taken the clock bag there during the wet week.

Nothing was found.

They made a list of the bird's last known places. After Saturday's show, Dev had removed it from the clock. He had placed it on the work table while he fixed a loose wheel. On Sunday, the theatre laundry company collected cloths and costume shirts from the same room.

"It may have gone into a laundry bag," Farah said. "The bags were open beside the table."

Nell called the laundry office. The Sunday bags had gone to a large centre in Northcross. Most had already been emptied and washed.

"It is a wooden bird with blue wings," she explained. "It is about the size of a hand."

The office worker asked for a photograph. Kit had taken one before the bird was painted. Farah had a later picture on her phone.

They sent both pictures. The centre agreed to check its lost-item shelf, but it could not promise an answer before the evening show.

Dev cut a new bird shape from light wood. He did not have time to make the moving wings. Farah painted it blue. It would work from the audience, although it was simpler.

At five, the laundry centre called. A blue wooden bird had been found inside a clean towel cart. A delivery driver was coming to Morrowbay at six thirty and could bring it.

The driver arrived at seven ten. The show began at seven thirty. Nell met the van at the stage door and checked the bird. One wing had a small mark, but it still moved.

Dev put it inside the clock with nine minutes left.

There was no time for a full test. Dev turned the small handle behind the clock and watched the door open once. The bird came out. He closed it again and rolled the clock into its starting place.

Farah touched a little blue paint over the mark. The colour was still wet, so she placed a small paper cover around the wing. Dev removed it just before the clock went on stage.

Iris had prepared the simple spare bird while they waited for the van. It lay on the work table. She did not put it away until the interval.

"One real bird and one quiet bird," Benji said. "We have more birds than plans."

"We have exactly enough," Iris replied.

In the front hall, guests were arriving in smart clothes. Mae wore a dark green dress that Iris had chosen. Robin had come from Eastmere and sat beside her. Peter and Helen could not travel that evening, but they had sent flowers to Nell at Lantern House.

Nell had never received opening-night flowers. The card said, FOR ALL THE BOXES YOU CARRIED. Dad had added, AND THE RECORDS YOU KEPT. Her mother had drawn a small lamp.

Nell left the flowers in water at the ticket desk. She did not want to carry them through the audience.

Mara Voss crossed the hall with the two visiting theatre managers. She stopped to ask if the bird had returned.

"It is in the clock," Nell said. "A spare is ready."

Mara did not ask for the whole story then. She only said, "Good," and took her seat.

At seven twenty-eight, Dev stood behind the clock wall. Farah held the brown coat ready for Lydia. Iris checked the stage-door clock against her watch.

Nell suddenly understood that every person had a different opening night. For the audience, it began when the lights went down. For the company, it had begun with breakfast, a missing prop, a laundry call, and wet blue paint.

Iris called the audience into the theatre. Nell sat in Kit's empty seat. The curtain did not exist in this production, so she could see the clock waiting in the dark.

Chapter 17: Opening Night

Opening night began exactly on time. Mara Voss sat in the last row with two theatre managers from other towns. A newspaper writer sat near the front.

Nell knew every movement of the first scene. Still, it felt new. Lydia's voice was clear. Sami waited before opening the letter. Owen closed the invisible door against the snow.

At the interval, no one from the company talked about the wooden bird. Dev had tested it once before the show. That had to be enough.

The second half began. The clock moved to the centre of the stage. Lydia and Sami argued about selling the shop. Owen found the mother's design under the floor.

At midnight, the hands turned. The small door opened. The blue bird came out, with both wings moving.

Lydia caught it. For one moment, she looked down at the real mark on its wing. Then she spoke the line.

"She finished it. Mum finished it after all."

The audience was completely quiet.

At the end, everyone stood to clap. Farah pulled Nell through the side door. The whole company gathered on stage after the audience had gone.

Mara Voss came down from the last row. She did not give a long speech.

"The tour theatres want the show," she said. "And tonight's ticket report is strong enough to keep all twelve Morrowbay performances."

The company cheered. Dev lifted the wooden bird above his head.

Casper thanked the actors and crew. Then he thanked Lantern House for becoming a practice room, store room, dining room, and home.

Mae was standing near the stage door. "It was already three of those things," she said.

They returned to Lantern House for food. Farah had made a cake shaped like a clock. Its hands showed midnight. Kit joined by video from Redwater. Nell placed the phone at the end of the table, between Rosa and Jamie.

"I saw the bird on a live call," Kit said. "Benji showed me from the side."

"It has travelled more than you this week," Nell told him.

At one in the morning, Lydia cut the last piece of cake. She gave it to Owen.

"For being ready," she said.

Owen divided it in two and gave half back to her.

Chapter 18: The Morning Review

The Morrowbay News published its review online before breakfast. Sami found it on his phone at seven fifteen.

"Four stars," he called across the dining room.

Everyone wanted to read it at once. The review praised Lydia's warm performance and Sami's difficult family scenes. It called Owen "a quiet centre in a noisy story." It also described Dev's clock as "a character with a wooden face."

The writer had made one mistake. She said the play was set in a mountain village. The story was set in an unnamed northern town. Casper wanted Iris to ask for a correction.

"Does the mistake harm the show?" Iris asked.

"It changes the place."

"The play does not name the place."

Casper read the line again. He decided it was the writer's picture of the story, not a factual error. They did not contact the newspaper.

A second article appeared at noon. It showed Lydia holding the wooden bird. The picture had been taken after the show with permission. The article told the story of the bird's trip to the laundry centre.

Kit's name appeared below the picture as its maker. Farah's name appeared as the painter. The laundry driver was named too.

"The bird has a better review than I do," Sami said.

Ticket sales rose that afternoon. The next Friday sold out. More people booked rooms at Lantern House, but Mae kept one room free for company changes.

Mara Voss came to the desk before leaving town. She paid the current house bill and checked the receipt.

"We move to Bellweather after Morrowbay," she told Nell. "Our tour assistant has taken another job. Would you consider joining us for three weeks?"

Nell thought she had heard the question wrongly.

"On the tour?"

"Yes. Iris will stay here for another production. I need someone who knows this show and keeps calm when a wooden bird travels without a ticket."

Nell asked for time to think. She had a permanent job at Lantern House. She shared a home with Farah. Kit was already away.

Mara gave her a written outline with dates, pay, travel, and rooms. "Talk to Mae. I need an answer next Friday."

Nell put the paper in her bag. It was only a three-week offer, but it made the road beyond Morrowbay feel real.

She did not tell the company at once. Mara had asked her privately, and Nell wanted to speak to Mae before the offer became a public plan. However, Iris saw the folded outline when they cleared the theatre office.

"Mara asked you about Bellweather," Iris said. It was a statement, not a question.

Nell nodded. "Three weeks. I have not decided."

Iris put a stack of ticket reports into a folder. "She asked me first whether Lantern House could manage without you. I told her that was a matter for you and Mum, not me."

"Would you take the job?"

"I have taken that job many times. I like touring, but I also like coming home before a theatre forgets my name."

They walked back along Harbour Street. New rain made the poster colours shine. People stood outside the ticket office buying seats after the strong review.

At Lantern House, Mae was helping a guest find a lost glove. The glove was in the guest's own coat sleeve. Nell waited until the woman went upstairs before mentioning the offer.

Mae did not read it in the busy hall. She placed it inside the booking book at the next free page.

"We will look after dinner," she said. "A new path can wait until the soup is served."

That evening, Nell watched the company at their tables. Lydia was describing her new nephew. Owen was setting the small silver clock. Sami had bought every copy of the Morrowbay News from the late shop, although each paper contained the same review.

These people would leave in November whether Nell went with them or not. Lantern House would lose their voices. The stage would become empty for another production. Staying still would not keep this part of life unchanged.

After dinner, Nell took the outline to her old attic room. No guest was there that night. The round window showed the line of harbour lamps.

The document was clear about pay and rooms, but it could not tell her how she would feel in Bellweather. It listed duties but not late meals, wrong station exits, or missing objects. Nell now knew that the unwritten part of work was often the largest part.

She turned the page over and made two short lists. One was not a list of reasons to go. It held the things she needed to confirm: her Lantern House dates, rent while away, travel home, and who would hold emergency contacts. The other listed the people she needed to speak to before answering.

At the bottom, she wrote one honest line.

I want to see what happens after the show leaves Morrowbay.

That sentence did not decide the practical details, but it stopped her from hiding her choice inside them.

Nell closed the window and went downstairs. The company had gone to bed, but Owen's silver clock was still on the dining-room table. Its alarm was set for six thirty. She carried it to the desk and left a note with his room number, rather than opening his door after midnight.

Mae was checking the front lock. "You look less worried," she said.

"I made a list."

"Of course you did."

Nell showed her only the line at the bottom. Mae read it without smiling or looking sad.

"Then tomorrow we will make the rest possible," she said.

They turned off the hall light. Upstairs, seven theatre guests slept before another performance. Beyond the blue door, the road to the station was empty, but Nell could already picture herself walking down it with a case again.

Chapter 19: Mae's Answer

Nell waited until breakfast was over before she showed Mae the tour offer. They sat at the kitchen table with the printed page between them.

Mae read the dates first. The tour would leave Morrowbay on 17 November and return on 9 December. Nell would work in Bellweather and two smaller towns. Her room and train travel would be paid.

"Mara needs an answer by Friday," Nell said.

"What answer do you want to give?"

"I do not know. I want to go, but I do not want to leave you at the busiest part of the theatre stay."

Mae looked at the calendar. The company would leave Lantern House on the same day as Nell. December bookings were quiet. Rosa could help with weekend breakfasts.

"Do not use me as a reason to say no," Mae said. "Lantern House had mornings before you came. It will have three weeks without you."

"My job here is permanent."

"Permanent does not mean you must stand behind the same desk every day. It means we plan honestly when life changes."

Mae wanted Nell to keep her regular job, but the three tour weeks would be unpaid leave from Lantern House. Nell would return to her normal hours on 11 December. They wrote the agreement down so both of them understood it.

"You decided very quickly," Nell said.

"No. I decided when Iris first asked you to help at the theatre. I knew someone would see what you could do."

Nell called Farah next. Farah was in the costume room, so she spoke quietly.

"Three weeks is nothing," Farah said. "I kept your four spoons for more than a month. I can keep your room."

"I still pay rent."

"Then I will keep it very well."

Jamie was moving to Morrowbay at the beginning of November. He would sleep on the sofa, so Farah would not be alone.

Only one talk worried Nell. Kit was away already. If she joined the tour, their trains and weekends might never meet.

That evening, she placed the tour dates beside Kit's Redwater plan. They had one free weekend in the same place before Christmas. The small square on the calendar seemed unfair.

When Kit called, Nell did not begin with the calendar. She told him about Mara's offer and waited.

He smiled. "You should go."

"That was fast."

"I know what you sound like when you want something and are trying to make it a problem."

Nell looked at the dates again. "We will miss each other."

"We already do. We will also be proud of each other."

Chapter 20: A Place for Jamie

Jamie began work at Bayline Repairs on 1 November. He moved into Farah's flat two days earlier with three bags, a tool case, and a lamp shaped like a tyre.

Farah looked at the lamp. "That cannot go in the kitchen."

"It is useful."

"It can be useful beside the sofa."

For two weeks, Jamie slept in the sitting room. Every morning, he folded his bedding and put it behind the sofa. He left for work before Nell, but after Farah.

His new room near the theatre would become free when Benji left with the company. Mrs Lowe showed it to him on Sunday. It was small but had space for his tools in a locked cupboard downstairs.

"No engine parts in the room," she said.

Jamie looked at Nell. "Does everyone in this town know me?"

"Not yet."

Bayline Repairs looked after the theatre van. On Jamie's third day, the van would not start outside Sea Glass Theatre. Benji called the company office, and Bayline sent Jamie with another mechanic.

Nell saw him arrive in a blue work jacket. She wanted to run over, but she stayed behind the theatre door while they worked. Jamie was there as a mechanic, not as her younger brother.

The van needed a new battery. Bayline took it away and brought a replacement van for the evening. The company did not miss its delivery.

After work, Jamie came to the theatre kitchen.

"The old battery had a date on it," he said. "It should have been changed before the tour."

Iris checked the vehicle record. The hire company had said the van was ready for long travel. She sent the repair note to Mara.

"Will the van be safe for the tour?" Nell asked.

"Bayline will test it after the new part arrives," Jamie said. "The company can decide after it gets the report."

Mara chose to keep the replacement van for the first week. The original could join later if it was ready.

Peter and Helen visited that weekend. They brought kitchen plates for Jamie's future room and a warm coat for Nell's trip.

Peter looked around Farah's flat. Three young Carters now sat at the round table: Nell, Jamie, and a childhood picture on the wall that Peter had brought.

"You both left Dunley," he said.

Helen touched his arm. "They did not leave us."

Peter nodded slowly. The next morning, he helped Jamie carry the tyre lamp to Mrs Lowe's house.

Mrs Lowe had cleared the locked cupboard for Jamie's tool case. She gave him a written list of the furniture already in the room. They checked the small desk, bed, chair, and lamp together. One mark on the desk was already there, so she added it to the page before they both signed.

Jamie paid his deposit by bank transfer. He kept the receipt in a folder instead of sending it only as a phone picture. Peter watched this with approval.

"You have become organised," he said.

"Nell told me you would ask."

The new room looked over the back of Sea Glass Theatre. Jamie could see the stage-door light from his window. He could also hear the van when it returned after a late show.

Helen unpacked the kitchen plates even though the shared kitchen already had plates. Mrs Lowe found a separate shelf for them.

At lunch, Peter asked Jamie if the Bayline job was secure. Jamie gave the same honest answer Mae had once given about Lantern House.

"The company has regular work, but no one can promise that nothing will change. I signed a permanent contract. That is enough for now."

Peter looked at Nell, who tried not to smile.

In the afternoon, they visited Kit's workshop. Kit was in Redwater, but Rosa opened it so Peter could see Jamie's turning base under the flower boat. Tom had returned the display after summer. The boat stood on a high shelf, still bright blue.

"You made this on one weekend?" Peter asked.

"Kit made the boat. I only made it turn."

Peter took a picture. He sent it to three people before Jamie could stop him.

When Peter and Helen left on Sunday, they did not ask either child to return to Dunley. Peter gave Jamie a set of clean work gloves. Helen gave Nell another warm scarf for the tour.

At the station, Peter looked towards the sea. "We may come for Christmas," he said. "If Lantern House has a room."

"Book early," Nell replied. "Mae does not move other guests for family."

Chapter 21: The Understudy's Night

Lydia received a message before a Thursday performance. Her sister had gone into labour earlier than expected. Lydia wanted to travel to Eastmere to be with her family.

The last afternoon train left in forty minutes. She would not return before the show.

Owen was eating soup at Lantern House when Iris called him. He put down his spoon and listened.

"Yes," he said. "I am ready."

His hand shook when he ended the call.

Farah met him at the theatre. She changed the hidden ties in the brown coat. The costume team had already prepared his other clothes. Benji checked Owen's microphone.

The theatre put a clear notice at the door.

TONIGHT'S PERFORMANCE
The role of Mara will be played by Owen Marsh.

Some audience members asked to change their tickets. Most stayed. Mara Voss told the ticket desk to answer honestly and without making Owen sound like a weaker choice.

Nell sat at the side of the hall. She had watched Owen practise the part, but he had never performed it for a full audience.

His Mara was quiet at first. The opening lines felt smaller than Lydia's. Then Sami entered with the letter. Owen looked at him as if the whole family story was inside that one page.

The audience leaned forward.

During the song, Owen's voice was lower than Lydia's. The music still worked. At midnight, he caught the wooden bird and held it close to his chest.

When the play ended, the applause was warm and long. Owen bowed with the company. His face showed relief more than joy.

Backstage, he checked his phone. Lydia had sent a picture of a new baby boy wrapped in a yellow blanket.

"Her nephew arrived at nine twelve," Owen said. "She watched the second half on a video call without sound."

Lydia returned on Saturday. She brought Owen a small silver clock from Eastmere.

"For keeping time while I was gone," she said.

Owen placed it by his bed at Lantern House. He returned to his usual role that night. He did not seem smaller after being Mara. He carried the experience with him into the neighbour's quieter scenes.

Chapter 22: Farah's Form

Farah wanted to take a six-month baking course in Northcross. It met on Mondays, her only full day away from Harbour Kitchen. The course focused on bread and pastry for professional kitchens.

She left the application form on the round table for a week.

"The date is getting closer," Nell said.

"I know where the date is."

Farah had worked in kitchens since she was eighteen. She could already make excellent bread. The form asked why she needed more training.

"I do not know how to answer without sounding as if my work is bad," she said.

Nell understood. The tour offer had made her feel the same way. Wanting another path did not mean that the first path had failed.

Farah finally wrote about her plan. She wanted to run the baking side of Harbour Kitchen one day. She knew daily production, but she needed experience with larger orders and new methods. Her manager had agreed to change her Monday hours.

The form also asked for an example of work. Farah chose photographs of the clock cake and Sophie's wedding cake. She asked both people before using the pictures.

Kit helped by video when the website would not accept one large image. Jamie sat beside the computer and said the file was bigger than the whole application.

"Do not touch anything," Farah told him.

They made a smaller copy and kept the original. The application went through at ten fifty on the final evening.

APPLICATION RECEIVED
Reference: NK-2048
Interview date: details to follow

Farah read the message twice.

"Now I have to wait," she said.

"Owen says waiting is work," Nell replied.

Two weeks later, Farah had an interview in Northcross. She took a box of small apple pastries. The interviewers asked about her early kitchen work and future plans.

The course offered her a place from January. Farah accepted. She would travel by morning train each Monday and return in the evening.

"Everyone is leaving on trains," Jamie said at dinner.

"And coming back on them," Farah replied.

Chapter 23: A Difficult Saturday

The second Saturday show sold out. The matinee ended at five, and the evening performance began at seven thirty. The company had little time between them.

During the matinee, one wheel on the clock made a hard sound. Dev checked it after the show. A small metal edge had bent. The clock still moved, but it could damage the wooden floor.

Kit was in Redwater. Dev sent him pictures. Kit called and explained which spare part might fit, but Dev had to choose the repair himself after seeing the real wheel.

At the same time, the theatre cafe's food delivery was late. Actors needed dinner before they changed clothes. Nell called the cafe. Its driver had gone to the summer hall by mistake.

The food would arrive in twenty minutes. Nell bought fruit and bread from the shop across the street so no one had to wait.

At six fifteen, Dev finished the clock. He rolled it across the stage. The wheel was quiet and did not mark the floor.

At six twenty, the hot food arrived. Sami ate too quickly. Iris told him to slow down because his first scene began in more than an hour.

At seven, a family arrived with tickets for the matinee. Their email clearly said two thirty, but they had read only the date.

The evening show was full. The ticket manager found three returned seats in different rows. The parents could sit apart, with their older daughter near Nell at the side.

"We made the mistake," the father said. "Thank you for finding anything."

The show began. Nell kept the daughter's coat under her own seat because there was no space beside the narrow chair.

At midnight in the story, the clock rolled smoothly. The wooden bird appeared. The audience clapped during the scene, which had never happened before.

Afterwards, the family waited in the hall. The daughter said it was the best wrong time they had ever had.

Nell returned her coat. In the pocket was a folded matinee ticket. The printed time was large, but excitement had hidden it from them.

The company went back to Lantern House. Everyone was too tired to celebrate. Mae had left bread, cheese, and a note in the guest kitchen.

EAT WHAT YOU NEED. LEAVE THE REST FOR MORNING. THE HOUSE IS ASLEEP.

They ate quietly and did not wake it.

Chapter 24: Casper and Iris

As the show became successful, Casper began planning changes. He wanted new snow lighting for the tour and a larger table for the clock shop. Iris reminded him that every new object had to fit into the van and into smaller theatres.

"Bellweather has a wider stage," Casper said. "The table will make the room feel real."

"Greyminster has a door that is ten centimetres narrower than this one," Iris replied. "The table will not enter the building."

They argued in the theatre office. Their voices were not loud, but everyone outside could hear the sharp stops between their words.

Nell had measured the tour doors from venue information. She showed them the page. A folding table could fit all three theatres, but it would look different from the heavy shop table Casper imagined.

Dev drew a design with a solid front and folding legs. From the audience, it would seem heavy. In the van, it would lie flat.

Casper liked the front but disliked the legs. Iris liked the size but worried about the time needed to build it.

Mara Voss approved a small cost only if Dev could finish it before the final Morrowbay week. They agreed.

Later, Iris sat at the Lantern House kitchen table. She had missed dinner.

"Casper thinks I stop ideas," she told Mae.

"Do you?"

"Sometimes. Ideas have weight, cost, and train times."

Mae warmed soup for her. "Then you do not stop them. You make them live in the same world as everyone else."

Casper came to the house a few minutes later. He wanted to leave a revised scene note for Lydia. He saw Iris eating alone.

"I pushed too hard about the table," he said. "The folding design is good."

Iris put down her spoon. "I spoke as if the answer was only no. I should have shown you the door measurements first."

They did not become best friends over soup. They still disagreed the next day about the colour of the new table. However, they kept the problem on the table and not between them.

Dev built a small test corner before he began the full table. He painted one side dark brown and the other grey. On stage, the brown looked warm under the shop lights. The grey almost disappeared beside the clock.

Casper chose brown. Iris checked the dry time and van size. Both answers worked.

The folding legs arrived from a supplier on Thursday. The box contained four left-side parts and no right-side parts. Dev could not build a level table from them.

Nell photographed the labels and the contents. The supplier found that two boxes had been exchanged at its store. A customer in Bellweather had received the right-side parts meant for Sea Glass.

The supplier offered to send the missing parts on Monday. Dev needed them by Saturday. Mara approved a same-day courier because the error belonged to the supplier and the company would not pay the extra charge.

The correct parts arrived on Friday afternoon. Dev checked every piece before the driver left. By Saturday evening, the table could stand, fold, and fit through the narrow marked doorway in the workroom.

Casper asked the actors to use it in the evening show. Iris said no. The paint was dry, but the team had not practised the scene with the new shape.

"It is only a table," Casper said.

"It changes where three people walk and where the clock turns," Iris replied. "It joins the show on Tuesday."

This time, Casper accepted the answer without a long argument.

On Tuesday, Sami nearly hit the table during the first run. Lydia moved her chair to make room. Owen had to enter on the other side. They repeated the scene until no one watched their feet.

At the evening performance, the new table looked as if it had always stood in the clock shop. The audience could not know how many parts, calls, measurements, and disagreements were inside it.

After the show, Casper put his hand on its dark top. "You were right about Tuesday," he told Iris.

"Please write down the date," she said. "Mum will want proof."

Chapter 25: The Last Room Change

Lantern House had one final room problem before the company left. A pipe in Room Four made a low knocking sound at night. Owen could not sleep.

Mae called the heating company. A worker could come on Tuesday, but it was Saturday. The sound was not dangerous, but it was annoying.

Room Nine was free. Owen could move there, but Robin had booked it for the final show weekend. Owen would have to move again on Friday.

"I have lived in five rooms during this production," he said. "One more will make a complete tour of the house."

Nell checked his stays. He had used Rooms Four, Seven, the attic, Three for one afternoon costume change, and now Nine.

"You still need the ground-floor room," she said.

"Save it for my next play."

They moved his clothes on Saturday afternoon. Farah carried the brown coat separately. It did not belong to Owen, but he had become its main keeper.

On Tuesday, the heating worker repaired a loose part. Room Four became quiet. Owen chose to stay in Nine until Friday because he liked the garden view.

When Robin arrived, Owen moved back to Four. He left a small paper clock on the desk for Robin.

"For the next quiet guest," his note said.

Robin knew Owen from the play. He found him at breakfast.

"This clock is five minutes slow," Robin said.

"That is why I could leave it."

Robin set it correctly and kept it beside his bed.

The room changes had once made Nell nervous. Now she checked names, dates, keys, and needs in order. She did not trust memory when a written line could be clear.

On the room board, she moved Owen's card for the last time. Beneath it, the December board was almost empty.

The house would soon lose seven voices, eleven cases, the brown coat, and the long clock bag. Nell would leave with them for three weeks. Mae said she wanted peace, but she had started making too much toast. She was already used to a full table.

Chapter 26: Kit's Decision

Kit came home for the final Morrowbay weekend. The Redwater work would end in December, but the theatre had offered him a new order from February to July.

This time, he did not ask Nell by the harbour. He brought the written offer to the round table.

"I can do some of the work in Morrowbay," he said. "The large pieces must be made in Redwater. I would stay there three nights each week, not five."

The pay would help him buy a better machine for his own workshop. The new work also included training a young theatre carpenter.

"Do you want it?" Nell asked.

"Yes. I also want us to have more ordinary days."

Nell understood. Their talks happened around train times and tired weekends. They had not spent a normal Wednesday together since summer.

"Three nights is better than five," she said. "But it is still away."

"I know."

They did not decide the whole future that evening. Kit accepted the six-month order because it was right for his work. They agreed to keep one weekday free when he was in Morrowbay. It would not become a work evening or a group dinner unless they both wanted that.

"Farah will be unhappy," Kit said.

"Farah will feed us on Tuesday. We can keep Wednesday."

Farah came home while they were still talking.

"I heard my name," she said.

They told her the plan. She thought for a moment.

"Wednesday is a poor dinner night," she said. "You may have it."

Kit went to the final Saturday show. He sat between Nell and Jamie. When the wooden bird came out, Jamie whispered, "My turning boat has a better motor."

Kit told him to be quiet.

After the show, Dev showed Kit the repaired wheel and the folding table. Kit checked the table joints, but he did not change anything.

"It is good work," he told Dev.

The compliment mattered because Kit knew how hard it was to let another person solve a problem in a different way.

Chapter 27: Closing Night

The last Morrowbay performance sold out two days early. Lantern House also filled with returning guests. Ruth Bell came back with a finished book. Clara Dean brought a blue glass clock as a gift for the company.

Peter and Helen travelled from Dunley. Peter wore the same dark coat as before, but this time he had a small overnight bag. He and Helen stayed in Room Six.

"We booked like normal guests," he told Nell. "Your mother made me."

"I stopped him from calling you at home," Helen said.

Before the show, Mae served an early dinner. The company signed a poster for her. Lydia wrote, THANK YOU FOR THE MILK ON THE TOP SHELF. Owen drew a small clock beside his name.

The performance was strong, although everyone felt the ending before it came. In the final scene, Mara decided not to sell the clock shop. She also chose not to keep it exactly as her parents had left it. She opened the door and invited her brother to build something new with her.

Nell had heard the lines many times. On closing night, they seemed to belong to Lantern House too. Mae had kept the old piano and the Low Tide box, but she had also made space for actors, new work, and Nell.

After the bows, Casper invited Mae onto the stage. She did not want to go, but Iris took her hand. The audience clapped for Lantern House.

Peter leaned towards Nell. "They mean you too."

"They mean breakfast."

"Sometimes breakfast is important work."

Nell remembered Robin saying the same thing on her first morning.

The company packed only personal things that night. The clock and scenery would go into the van in the morning. Farah checked every costume. Dev placed the wooden bird in a hard box with its photograph outside.

At Lantern House, the guests stayed up late. Robin played Mae's song on the piano. The actors joined the tune with soft voices, although there were no fixed words for them.

At midnight, Mae locked the front door. "Tomorrow, the house will be quiet," she said.

No one believed that quiet would feel simple.

Chapter 28: Packing the Van

The company van stood outside Sea Glass Theatre at eight. Jamie arrived from Bayline Repairs with the final vehicle paper. The original van had a new battery and had passed the hire company's checks. Mara decided to use it.

Dev had drawn a loading plan. The clock went in first, tied in its travel frame. The folding table lay beside it. Costume rails stood along the other wall.

Nell checked each numbered box as it entered.

SCENERY: 1-8
COSTUMES: 1-5
LIGHTING: 1-6
SOUND: 1-4
PROPS: 1-3

One sound box was still inside the theatre. Benji ran back for it. A costume bag had no number, so Farah added a label before it went into the van.

The hard bird box travelled in the front with Dev. No one wanted it near the laundry bags again.

At Lantern House, seven people brought down eleven cases. Mae checked every room after they left. Lydia had forgotten a green scarf. Owen had left the silver clock beside his bed.

Nell ran to the station with both things. Lydia put on the scarf. Owen set the clock by the train window.

The company was taking the ten thirty train to Bellweather. Nell had one case and a black work bag. Farah, Jamie, Mae, Rosa, Kit, Peter, and Helen stood on the platform.

"It is only three weeks," Nell said.

"Then stop looking as if you are moving to the moon," Farah replied.

Kit kissed her before the train came. They would meet in Bellweather on the second weekend if his work allowed.

Mae gave Nell a small notebook. The first page had the Lantern House address and phone number.

"I know our address," Nell said.

"The rest of the pages are empty."

Nell put it in her work bag. The train arrived, and the company found its reserved seats. Through the window, she saw her family and friends become smaller.

The sea stayed beside the train for ten minutes. Then the line turned inland. Nell opened the notebook and wrote the first tour list.

Bellweather rooms. Theatre keys. Van arrival at four. Company meal at six.

This time, she was not travelling to find a new home. She was taking her work with her and planning to return.

Chapter 29: Bellweather

Bellweather was larger than Morrowbay. Its station had six platforms and two exits. The company hotel was near the old town, while the theatre stood across the river.

Mara had sent a coach to collect the cases. The driver waited at the north exit. Half the company walked towards the south exit because a sign pointed to taxis.

Nell called Sami and guided them back through the station. No one missed the coach, but they lost twenty minutes.

At the hotel, the room list had an old date. It showed Owen in a single room and Lydia sharing with Farah. Farah was not on the tour. The company costume worker in Bellweather was called Erin.

Nell showed the hotel manager Mara's final confirmation. It listed a separate room for Lydia and a twin room for Owen and Benji.

The hotel had followed the earlier list and had already given one single room to another guest. The manager offered a larger family room for Owen and Benji. It had two beds and a sofa.

"We do not need a sofa," Benji said. "But we can live with one."

Lydia got the single room. Erin was not staying at the hotel. The wrong shared booking was removed.

At four, Nell met the van at Bellweather Crown Theatre. The loading door was wide enough for the clock, but a street market filled the road outside. Dev had to wait until the market closed at five.

Nell called the theatre manager and arranged a safe waiting place in its yard. The driver turned off the engine. The boxes stayed locked in the van.

At five ten, the road cleared. The company unloaded before dinner. Every number on Nell's list entered the building.

The Bellweather stage was wider and deeper than Sea Glass. The wooden clock looked small in the middle. Casper was pleased because the empty space made the family shop seem lonely.

Nell returned to the hotel at nine. Her room looked over the river. She sent Mae a picture, then called Kit.

"Did you solve the whole town?" he asked.

"Only two exits, one room list, and a street market. The town can keep the rest."

The next morning, Nell walked the route from the hotel to the theatre alone. Mara's map showed a short path beside the river. A gate on that path was closed for repairs, so the real walk took ten minutes longer.

She photographed the public closure sign and sent the company a new route. It crossed the stone bridge and passed a supermarket. Lydia wanted to buy fruit there after practice, so the longer walk had one useful point.

At the theatre, Dev had begun to unload the smaller prop boxes. The main clock stayed in its travel frame until the stage team was ready. A local worker called Amara Holt was taking photographs of the setup for the theatre record.

Nell first thought Amara was part of Mara Voss's team because of the similar names. She checked before adding her to the contact page.

"Amara with an A," the photographer said. "People often send me Mara's messages. I never open the attached production papers. I tell them they have the wrong person."

Nell wrote both full names in the notebook. Amara Holt would later become the camera operator on The Quiet Mile, although neither of them knew it then.

At noon, the hotel called. A cleaner had found a Sea Glass key card under Owen's bed. It belonged to the old Lantern House room system, not the Bellweather hotel. Owen had carried it from Morrowbay by mistake.

Nell asked Mae to check the number before she sent it back. Mae replied that the card had been cancelled when Owen left. It contained no guest name. Nell cut it across the magnetic strip and disposed of it through the hotel's secure waste process.

The hotel manager also confirmed the corrected room list in writing. Nell placed the old and new versions together with OLD across the first page. She did not destroy it yet because it showed how the mistake had happened.

By the time the company arrived for afternoon practice, the route, room list, and theatre contacts were clear. Nell had not solved Bellweather. She had only made a few paths less confusing.

Chapter 30: A Different Theatre

The Crown Theatre had more staff than Sea Glass. Different people managed lights, sound, the stage, and the audience. Nell needed to learn names quickly.

The first morning, she gave a food delivery to a man called Paul. He worked with lighting, not food. The real kitchen manager was Paula. Paul carried the boxes to her and laughed.

"One letter can move a lot of sandwiches," he said.

The stage floor was level, so the clock moved more easily. However, the roof was higher. The white snow light looked weak above the actors.

Benji worked with Paul to change the light position. Casper watched from the centre seats. By lunch, the snow reached the back wall again.

The first Bellweather show was on Thursday. This audience did not know the story of the missing bird or wet practice room. They saw only the finished play.

Nell stood near the ticket desk. A man arrived with an email for the wrong Thursday. His tickets were for the following week. That show had free seats, but the current evening was full.

The ticket manager offered to keep his original booking or move it to Friday. The man chose Friday and thanked them for checking before the lights went down.

Nell realised that many problems did not need a clever answer. They needed a calm person to find the correct date.

After the show, Mara Voss spoke to her.

"The Greyminster venue needs updated travel numbers by Monday," she said. "Can you prepare them?"

Nell checked the company list. Two actors would travel from home, while the rest would go from Bellweather. The van had a separate route.

She worked in the hotel breakfast room the next morning. She confirmed names with each person instead of guessing. By noon, Mara had one page with train, van, and arrival details.

Mara read it and made one change. She would travel a day later for another meeting.

"You have made this tour quieter," she told Nell.

Nell thought about the noisy hotel corridor and the clock wheels on the stage. The tour did not feel quiet. Then she understood that Mara meant fewer surprises.

Chapter 31: Kit's Saturday

Kit planned to visit Bellweather on the second Saturday. His train from Redwater should have arrived at eleven. At ten, he called Nell.

"The morning work is not finished," he said. "A wall panel came back in the wrong size."

He could leave at two and arrive at five thirty. Nell had work until five. They would still have the evening and Sunday morning.

At three, another call came. Kit sounded tired.

"I am still here. If I take the last train, I will arrive after nine and leave again at noon tomorrow."

Nell sat in an empty theatre office. She had kept the evening free. Their hotel dinner booking was at seven.

"Do not come for three tired hours," she said. "We can meet when I return to Morrowbay."

"I wanted to see Bellweather with you."

"I wanted that too."

They cancelled the hotel dinner. Nell was sad and angry, although she knew Kit had not made the panel wrong.

Lydia found her in the theatre hall after the afternoon show.

"Come to dinner with us," she said. "Sami has found a place by the river."

Nell almost said no. She had wanted a quiet evening with Kit, not a company meal. Then she remembered Farah saying that two feelings could sit together.

She joined the group. They ate at a long table near a window. Boats with small lights moved along the river. Owen told a terrible story about a school play in which a tree fell on him.

Nell laughed. The evening was not the one she had planned, but it was still hers.

Kit called when she returned to the hotel. He had finally finished the wall panel.

"Show me the river," he said.

Nell stood by her window and turned the phone towards the lights. Kit showed her the empty Redwater workshop. For a minute, two faraway places shared one small screen.

Chapter 32: Snow on the Line

The company left Bellweather on a cold Monday. Light snow began before the train reached Greyminster. At first, it melted on the windows. Then it covered the fields.

Their train stopped at a small station called Alder Cross. A message said there was a points problem ahead. The staff could not give a new arrival time.

The company had planned to reach Greyminster at one. The first theatre meeting was at three. The van was already there with the scenery.

Nell contacted the Greyminster theatre manager. She explained that the whole cast was on the same delayed train. The manager moved the stage meeting to the next morning. Dev, who had travelled with the van, could begin checking scenery alone.

Mara considered hiring cars, but the snowy roads were also slow. They stayed on the train and waited for reliable information.

At three, the train moved again. It reached Greyminster after five. The hotel had kept their rooms, but the kitchen closed at six.

Nell called from the train and ordered simple meals for the company. The hotel put them aside. When everyone arrived, cold and tired, food was waiting.

The snow continued overnight. The theatre opened late the next day. The first performance was not until Wednesday, so the company still had time.

On Tuesday afternoon, Lydia stood on the Greyminster stage and looked at the white roofs outside. "The play finally has real snow," she said.

The Greyminster theatre was the smallest on the tour. Dev's folding table fitted through its narrow door. The clock had only a hand of space on each side when it rolled.

Casper changed two actor movements. No one complained about the smaller room. The trip had already shown them that plans could bend without breaking.

Nell wrote to Mae that night. Lantern House had no snow, only rain. Mae replied with a picture of the brass lamp above the blue door.

The picture made Nell want to go home. It also reminded her that home would still be there when the last train ran.

Chapter 33: The Cracked Wing

During the second Greyminster show, the wooden bird fell. Lydia caught it before it reached the floor, but one blue wing cracked near its edge.

The bird still looked whole from the audience. Dev put it in the hard box after the show and sent pictures to Kit.

Kit said the crack could be repaired, but the glue needed time to dry. Dev used the simple spare bird for the next performance.

Casper worried that its still wings would weaken the midnight scene. Lydia practised lifting it so the shadow looked alive on the wall.

"The audience will follow her hands," Owen said. "They did when there was no bird at all."

The next night, the spare bird worked. Its shadow crossed the clock face, and no one outside the company knew it was different.

On Friday, Kit travelled to Greyminster with a small repair case. His Redwater order had finally reached a quiet week. Nell met him at the station.

"You came for the bird," she said.

"I heard someone else might be here too."

They carried the hard box to a work table at the theatre. Kit looked at the crack and spoke with Dev. Together, they repaired it and left it safely in the box overnight.

Then Kit and Nell had their missed dinner. The snow had become soft rain, so they stayed in the hotel restaurant. They talked about ordinary things: Jamie's new room, Farah's course, Mae's coffee pot, and Kit's new machine.

"This is our first dinner in Greyminster," Nell said.

"It may also be our last."

"That is still enough to be real."

On Saturday, the blue bird returned to the clock. Kit watched from beside Dev, not from the audience. At midnight, the repaired wings opened.

Nell saw Kit smile in the dark. His work had travelled through three towns and returned to life after falling. In the morning, he took the train back to Morrowbay, where his own workshop waited.

Chapter 34: The Final Tour Show

The final performance took place on a Sunday afternoon. The Greyminster theatre was full of families, older couples, and a school group.

Before the doors opened, Mara gathered the company on stage. She thanked them for twenty-one performances and three different theatres. She also announced that another tour might happen in spring, but no dates were ready.

"Today is not an interview for the next tour," she said. "It is the last day of this one. Be here for it."

The company performed with tired bodies and clear attention. Small moments became special. Sami placed the cup exactly where Casper had asked weeks before. Owen closed the invisible door against the snow. Lydia held the bird for one extra second.

At the final bow, the audience stood. The cast bowed again. Then the work lights came on, and the story became scenery, clothes, and lists.

Nell checked boxes while Dev loaded the van. Some scenery would go to storage in Northcross. The clock would return to Kit's workshop until Mara decided its future.

Lydia packed the brown coat in Costume Bag Two. Farah had asked for a picture of its hidden ties before it left.

Owen found the first Sea Glass poster in his case. His face was still behind Lydia's. He asked every cast member to sign it.

At the hotel, they ate one last dinner together. Casper gave Iris a small model table with folding legs. He had made it from card.

"For stopping my best ideas," he said.

"For fitting them through doors," Iris replied.

Nell received a gift too. Benji gave her a toy bird with a train ticket around its neck.

The ticket said MORROWBAY - RETURN.

The next morning, the company separated. Lydia and Sami travelled east. Owen went north. Casper stayed for meetings. Nell and Benji took the Morrowbay train with the last two costume cases.

Before Nell left, she completed the hotel room check. Owen and Benji had moved the sofa near the window, so they returned it to its original place. Lydia had used one cup from the room tray and told the desk. Mara's room charge showed two breakfasts on a day when she had left early.

The hotel manager checked the meal record. One breakfast belonged to another room with a similar number. He corrected the final bill before Mara paid it.

Nell also returned four theatre keys. One had been in Casper's coat pocket, and another was attached to Benji's green case. The theatre manager signed a line for each returned key.

"You are very careful at the end," Benji said as they walked to the station.

"The end is when tired people leave things behind."

At the platform, Lydia discovered that her train ticket began from Bellweather, not Greyminster. The booking office checked the route. Her ticket was valid only from the earlier town because the company office had copied an old journey.

There was enough time to buy the correct part of the trip. Mara recorded the extra cost and the reason. Lydia caught her train without running.

Owen's northbound service left next. He hugged Lydia and Sami, then put the signed poster under his arm. His silver clock was safely inside his bag.

Casper stood near the exit, already speaking on the phone about another production. Iris had returned to Morrowbay two days earlier for work. Dev drove away with the scenery van.

The group became smaller every few minutes.

Nell and Benji finally boarded their train. The costume cases took the luggage space beside them. Benji checked the labels three times.

"Do you think Farah will know if the brown coat is missing?" he asked.

"Farah will know before the train does."

They opened the bag only far enough to see the coat's brown sleeve. Then they closed it again and watched Greyminster disappear behind wet glass.

As the sea appeared outside the window, Nell felt the shape of the tour behind her. It had been only three weeks, but she was not the same person who had left.

Chapter 35: Home in December

Farah and Jamie met Nell at Morrowbay station. Jamie held a sign that said LOCAL ASSISTANT, although there was no one else to collect.

"I know who I am," Nell said.

"The sign is for us. We forgot."

Farah hugged Nell and took one costume case. Benji carried the other to Mrs Lowe's house. His work with the company had ended, but he was staying in Morrowbay for two days before going home.

The flat had changed. Jamie's bedding was gone from the sofa. Farah's course timetable was on the fridge. A new green plant stood in Nell's room.

"The first plant became sad," Farah said. "This one is stronger."

Nell unpacked slowly. She put Mae's notebook in the wooden box beside her bed. It held train times, room changes, food orders, and one drawing of the wooden bird.

At four, she went to Lantern House. The brass lamp was already on because winter days were short.

Mae opened the door before Nell rang. "You still have a key."

"I wanted to arrive like a guest."

Mae hugged her, then showed her every change. The coffee pot had a new handle. Room Four was quiet. Robin's old chair had moved back beside the piano.

Only three guests were staying. The house felt larger without the company.

Nell returned to work two days later. She remembered the breakfast orders, keys, and phone greeting. Nothing had become too small after the tour. It felt solid.

Kit finished in Redwater the following week. On their first free Wednesday, he and Nell walked from the station to Lantern House. He returned the blue glass bead. Nell tied it by her bedroom window again.

At the round table, Farah served bread from her first course practice. Jamie brought the tyre lamp from his new room because he said the table needed more light. Mae, Iris, Rosa, and Tom joined them.

There were no paper birds. Farah had hung the little tour bird with its return ticket above Nell's chair.

Christmas brought Peter and Helen back to Lantern House. They booked the ground-floor room because Helen found long stairs uncomfortable after a fall earlier that year. Mae put their family name in the same book as every other guest.

Jamie joined them for dinner after his Bayline shift. He had to work on 26 December because delivery vans still needed repairs. Peter did not complain about the hours. He had begun to see that his children's new jobs were not short holidays.

Kit came with Rosa. Farah made bread shaped like small stars. Iris arrived late from Redwater with a case full of clean theatre tape that she had forgotten to remove.

Robin played the Low Tide song after dinner. Mae sang only the first line, then let the piano carry the rest. The green box remained in the cupboard. She had received one letter from June Bell, but she kept it private.

During the quiet week after Christmas, Nell sorted her tour notebook. She made copies of important payment records for Mara and returned two original meal receipts. She kept no guest details that she no longer needed.

Mara sent a final message:

Tour records received. All expenses agreed. Thank you for making the journey possible. I hope we work together again.

Nell printed the message and put it beside Mae's first job offer in her wooden box.

In January, ordinary life began again. Farah caught the six-forty train to Northcross every Monday. Kit worked in Morrowbay on Tuesdays and Wednesdays. Jamie ate at the flat on Fridays, although he now had his own plates.

Nell liked the repeated days. They were not empty after the tour. They gave shape to everything she had brought home.

Chapter 36: A New Map

Winter passed quietly. Farah began her Monday course. Kit spent three nights a week in Redwater from February. Jamie settled into Bayline Repairs. Nell worked at Lantern House and helped Iris with short theatre visits.

In March, one year after Nell first arrived, Mara Voss came to Morrowbay. She was not planning another theatre tour. She had joined a small film company called Northlight Pictures.

The company wanted to make an original film called The Quiet Mile. It was a family story set in a house beside an old railway. They needed to find places for filming across the coast and inland towns.

Mara put a map on the Lantern House dining table. Red lines joined Morrowbay, Eastmere, Alder Cross, and places Nell had never visited.

"We need a junior location coordinator for twelve weeks," she said. "The person will contact property owners, arrange visits, keep travel records, and make sure the film team has accurate information."

Nell looked at the map. This was not a three-week tour with people she already knew. It was a larger job and a new kind of work.

"Why are you asking me?" she said.

"You check the door before you promise that a table will fit. You also know that a place is not empty just because a film wants to use it."

Mae listened from the kitchen door. She did not answer for Nell.

The work would begin in June. Nell could spend spring learning with the location manager, Gabriel Stone. Most journeys would start from Morrowbay, but some weeks would be away.

Mara gave her a full job outline. This time, Nell did not feel shocked by her own name at the top.

"I need to speak to Mae and think about the dates," she said. "Then I will give you a clear answer."

Mara smiled. "That is the answer I expected today."

After she left, Mae and Nell stood over the map. One red line followed the coast. Another crossed the hills to a lake called Westmere. A third ended at a small town named Orison Bridge.

"You will go," Mae said.

"I think so."

"Then we will plan."

Nell touched Morrowbay on the map. It was no longer the end of the train line in her mind. It was the place from which every new line began.

That evening, Nell carried the job outline to the round table. Farah was studying a bread recipe from her course. Jamie had come to repair the loose kitchen-radio button, although the radio worked without it. Kit arrived on the late train from Redwater.

They read Mara's dates one at a time. The film preparation would begin with two days each week, then become full time in June. Some location visits would require early trains. Two weeks might be spent near Westmere.

"You can keep this flat as your home," Farah said before Nell asked. "Your rent does not change because you own a map."

Kit looked at the red lines. His Redwater order would end in July. Their free Wednesdays would become less regular again.

"This is longer than the theatre tour," he said.

"I know."

"I still think you should go. I am only saying that it will change things."

Nell was glad he did not pretend distance was easy. They talked about the dates they knew and left the unknown weeks alone.

Jamie pointed to Alder Cross. "Bayline has a customer there. I have driven that road. The map makes it look flat, but it climbs for miles."

"You may be useful," Nell said.

"I have a job already."

"So did I when theatre found me."

On Friday, Nell accepted the role. Mae signed the new work plan with her. Lantern House would employ Rosa for more hours during summer, and Nell would return for some Sunday breakfasts when she was in town. No one described the arrangement as a favour. It was a clear change between two jobs that both mattered.

Mara introduced Nell to Gabriel Stone by video. He had managed film locations for fifteen years. His first words were not about beautiful houses.

"A place is somebody's work, home, memory, or responsibility before it becomes our picture," he said. "We start by remembering that."

Nell looked at Lantern House around her. The film map waited on the desk, but the breakfast room still needed flowers for morning. She could understand both kinds of work at once.
