English A2: Wider Horizons
Word Hunter Originals

Introduction

Six months after arriving in Greenford, Maya can manage familiar situations and is ready to explain more. This book follows her through a year of changes. Read in order, notice how ideas connect, and reuse the practical language in your own life. Listening-style tasks use scripts that a partner can read or that you can record yourself.

Lesson 1: A Busy New Term

Maya now works at Greenford Bakery three mornings a week and studies English in the afternoons. She usually cycles to work because it is faster than the bus. This month, however, she is taking the bus because her bicycle needs a new wheel. On Wednesdays she attends a photography club, and she often meets friends there.

"Are you still enjoying the bakery?" Hana asks.
"Yes. It can be tiring, but I like talking to customers. I'm also learning to decorate cakes."

Speaking: describe your usual week, then explain one temporary change. Use usually, at the moment, still, also, and because.

Lesson 2: Getting to Know People

A new student named Ellis joins the class. He grew up in a village near Cardiff, moved to Greenford last month, and is looking for office work. He seems quiet at first, but he becomes lively when the group discusses music.

ELLIS: How long have you lived here, Maya?
MAYA: For six months. I came here in September.
ELLIS: Do you miss home?
MAYA: Sometimes, especially my family, but Greenford feels more familiar now.

Use "for" with a period and "since" with a starting point. Writing: introduce yourself to a new group in 70 words. Include your background, current activities, interests, and one goal.

Lesson 3: Better Habits

During winter, Maya often stayed up too late and felt tired at work. She has recently changed her routine. She has stopped using her phone in bed, started preparing breakfast the night before, and joined a Saturday swimming session. She has already noticed more energy, although getting up early is still difficult.

"You should set a regular bedtime," Omar suggests. "And don't drink coffee late in the day."

Make a realistic habit plan. Include something you do too much, something you do not do enough, and two small changes. Explain why each change may help. Compare your plan with a partner's and offer gentle advice using could, should, and why don't you.

Lesson 4: A Family Visit

Maya's parents visited Greenford last weekend. On Friday she met them at the airport, but their luggage did not arrive. They completed a lost-bag form and went to the hotel. On Saturday the weather improved, so they explored the market and walked by the river. The airline delivered the bag that evening. On Sunday they had lunch with Maya's friends before flying home.

Writing: retell the weekend in order. Use at first, then, while, later, and finally. Speaking: ask Maya five past-tense questions, including one about the missing bag and one about her parents' opinion of Greenford.

Lesson 5: Memories and First Times

At the photography club, members discuss childhood memories.

"I learned to use a camera when I was ten," says Hana. "My aunt gave me her old one."
"I've taken thousands of photos since then," says Maya, "but I've never used a film camera."
"I have," Ellis replies. "I tried one during a school project last year."

Use the past simple for a finished time: last year, in 2019, when I was ten. Use the present perfect for life experience when the time is not stated. Speaking: ask "Have you ever ...?" questions, then ask when, where, or why for details.

Lesson 6: The Search for a Flat

Maya and Sam need to move because their landlord is selling the flat. They compare two advertisements.

RIVER VIEW: One-bedroom flat, modern kitchen, balcony, ten minutes from town center. Rent: GBP 925 per month. No pets.

PARK HOUSE: Two bedrooms, older kitchen, shared garden, bus stop outside. Rent: GBP 870 per month. Small pets welcome.

River View is brighter and more modern, but it is also more expensive and smaller. Park House is not as central, although it has an extra room. Reading: list two advantages and two disadvantages of each. Speaking: choose one and explain what matters most to you.

Lesson 7: Viewing a Home

An agent shows Maya around Park House.

MAYA: How long has the flat been empty?
AGENT: Only two weeks. The last tenants moved for work.
MAYA: Is heating included in the rent?
AGENT: No, but water is included. You pay electricity and internet separately.
MAYA: Could the landlord repair this window before we move in?
AGENT: I'll ask. You can add that request to the application.

Useful questions concern the deposit, bills, repairs, furniture, neighbors, and length of the agreement. Writing: send the agent a short follow-up message. Thank them, confirm your interest, and ask two unanswered questions.

Lesson 8: Moving Day

The friends rent a van and label every box by room. Heavy rain begins while they are carrying the sofa outside. They cover it with a sheet, but one corner gets wet. At the new flat, the sofa will not fit through the front door. Omar removes its legs, and at last they get it inside.

"We should have measured the door," Sam says.
"Perhaps, but we solved it," laughs Maya.

Listening-style task: Have someone read the story without showing you. Number these events: it rains; Omar removes the sofa legs; they label boxes; the sofa gets wet; they enter the flat. Then retell the day from Sam's point of view.

Lesson 9: Neighbors and House Rules

Maya meets Mrs Khan from the flat below. Mrs Khan explains that rubbish is collected on Tuesday mornings and bicycles must stay in the shed. The building should be quiet after 10:30 p.m. She also gives Maya the caretaker's number in case the main door stops working.

NOTICE TO RESIDENTS
Please flatten cardboard boxes. Do not leave bags in the hall. Report water leaks immediately. The lift will be unavailable on 14 March.

Reading: Which rule is about noise, safety, recycling, and transport inside the building? Speaking: politely tell a new neighbor about the rules without sounding unfriendly.

Lesson 10: A Problem in the Kitchen

On Monday evening, water starts dripping from under the kitchen sink. Sam turns off the small valve while Maya calls the emergency repair line.

OPERATOR: Can you describe the problem?
MAYA: There's a leak under our sink. It started about ten minutes ago.
OPERATOR: Is the water still running?
MAYA: No, we've turned it off, but the cupboard floor is wet.
OPERATOR: A plumber will arrive within two hours.

Writing: complete a repair report with the location, time, description, action already taken, and access information. Speaking: explain the same problem first to a friend, then more formally to a property manager.

Lesson 11: New Duties at Work

Ms Lee asks Maya to help train a new bakery assistant. Maya demonstrates how to open the shop: first switch off the alarm, then turn on the lights, check the fridges, place signs outside, and count the money in the till. Food must remain covered, and staff have to wash their hands before serving customers.

"What should I do if a price is missing?" asks the assistant.
"Check the list beside the till. If it isn't there, ask Ms Lee. Don't guess."

Write instructions for a familiar work, study, or home task. Use sequence words and include one warning with must not or do not.

Lesson 12: Applying for a Course

Maya finds an evening course called Digital Photography for Beginners. It lasts ten weeks and includes outdoor practice. Applicants do not need special equipment because the college lends cameras, but they must be free on two Saturday afternoons.

APPLICATION MESSAGE
Dear Course Team,
I would like to apply for the evening photography course. I have used a phone camera for several years, but I have not studied photography before. I am available on Tuesdays and both practice Saturdays. Could you tell me whether I need to bring a laptop?
Kind regards,
Maya Costa

Writing: adapt this message for a course you would like to take. Explain experience, availability, and one practical question.

Lesson 13: Learning Strategies

Daniel asks the class to share successful ways of learning. Hana remembers words by drawing tiny pictures. Ellis listens to short reports and writes key points. Maya keeps a phrase notebook instead of writing isolated words. She reviews it on the bus and uses each phrase in a new sentence.

"I understand more than I can say," Omar explains.
"Try recording a one-minute summary," Daniel suggests. "Listen once, then say it again more clearly."

Speaking: explain which methods work for you and which do not. Make a seven-day learning plan with short, varied activities for reading, writing, speaking, listening, vocabulary, and review.

Lesson 14: A Community Event

Greenford Library plans an International Evening. Visitors can hear short stories, learn greetings in six languages, and bring a snack from home. Maya volunteers at the welcome desk. Ellis will manage the music, while Hana is going to lead a drawing activity for children.

EVENT UPDATE
The evening begins at 6:30, not 6:00. Volunteers should arrive by 5:45. Please use the side entrance. We still need two people to move chairs after the event.

Reading: identify every change or instruction. Writing: reply as a volunteer, confirm when you will arrive, and offer help with one task.

Lesson 15: News and Media Habits

Maya checks local news online each morning, but she does not share stories before reading them. Ellis prefers a daily podcast because he can listen while walking. Hana follows an arts magazine, and Omar sometimes watches the evening news with subtitles.

They compare two headlines about the same event. One says, "Storm Destroys Weekend Plans," while another says, "Parks Close for One Windy Afternoon." The first sounds more dramatic, although both refer to the same closures.

Speaking: describe how you get news or entertainment. Reading: explain which headline gives more specific information and which tries harder to attract attention. Suggest two checks before sharing a story.

Lesson 16: Choosing What to Watch

The friends want a film night. Leo suggests an action film, but Hana has already seen it twice. Maya would rather watch a comedy because work has been stressful. Omar likes documentaries as long as they are not too long.

LEO: How about "North Bridge"? It has excellent reviews.
HANA: I'm not keen on action films. Could we choose something lighter?
MAYA: What about the new comedy? It's shorter than the documentary.
OMAR: Fine with me. Let's watch the trailer first.

Useful phrases: I'd rather, I don't mind, I'm not keen on, as long as, and that sounds fair. Negotiate a group choice among three programs with different lengths and genres.

Lesson 17: Buying and Returning Goods

Maya orders headphones online. They arrive on time, but the left side has no sound. She keeps the packaging and contacts customer service.

AGENT: Have you tried them with another device?
MAYA: Yes, I've tested them on my phone and laptop.
AGENT: Would you prefer a replacement or a refund?
MAYA: A replacement, please. How do I return this pair?
AGENT: Print the free return label and take the parcel to any collection point.

Writing: send a clear return request. Include the product, order date, exact fault, checks you made, and the result you want. Keep the tone calm and factual.

Lesson 18: Banking and Bills

After moving, Maya notices that her electricity payment is much higher than usual. She checks the meter and calls the company.

"My latest bill says 482 units, but the meter shows 428," she explains. "Could the numbers be in the wrong order?"
The adviser asks her to upload a photograph. The company pauses the payment while it checks the account.

A bill may include a fixed charge, the amount used, tax, previous payments, and a due date. Reading task: On a bill of GBP 64 due on 18 April, GBP 50 is for energy and GBP 14 is a fixed charge. What portion can change with use? Speaking: explain the problem and request next steps.

Lesson 19: A Day Trip Plan

The photography class plans a trip to Alder Castle. The train is cheaper if they book as a group, but the coach goes directly to the entrance. The train journey takes forty minutes, followed by a twenty-minute walk. The coach takes seventy minutes and may be delayed by traffic.

"The train is quicker, but carrying equipment on the walk may be difficult," says Maya.
"The coach is more convenient," Hana replies, "although we would have less time at the castle."

Compare cost, speed, comfort, and flexibility. Decide as a group, give two reasons, and write a message confirming departure place, time, price, and what to bring.

Lesson 20: A Missed Connection

On the trip, the first train stops outside a station because of a signal problem. The class misses its connection.

MAYA: Excuse me, our train was delayed and we've missed the 10:05 to Alder. What should we do?
CLERK: Your tickets are valid on the 10:35 service.
MAYA: Do we need to change anywhere?
CLERK: Yes, at West Bay. The platform will be on the screen.

Useful travel language: delayed, cancelled, connection, valid, replacement bus, platform change, and expected arrival. Speaking: role-play asking for information after a cancellation. Ask about the next service, ticket validity, changes, and arrival time.

Lesson 21: The Wrong Room

At the guesthouse, Maya receives a room facing a busy road, although she requested a quiet room. The receptionist checks the booking and finds the request, but the guesthouse is full that night.

"I'm sorry we can't move you now," he says. "A garden room will be free tomorrow. We can also provide earplugs and reduce tonight's price."
"Thank you," Maya replies. "I'll accept that if you can confirm the move for tomorrow morning."

Speaking: practise stating a problem without anger, referring to the original request, and agreeing on a solution. Writing: leave a brief record of what was promised and when.

Lesson 22: Feeling Unwell Away from Home

Hana slips on a wet path and hurts her wrist. It is painful and slightly swollen, but she can move her fingers. The hotel receptionist directs them to a nearby urgent treatment center.

NURSE: Tell me how it happened.
HANA: I was walking downhill when I slipped. I landed on my right hand.
NURSE: Did you hit your head?
HANA: No, and I didn't lose consciousness.

The nurse examines the wrist and advises ice, rest, and a follow-up if the pain gets worse. Writing: describe an accident using while, when, and sequence words. Include symptoms and action taken.

Lesson 23: Asking a Public Service for Help

Back in Greenford, Ellis loses his wallet. The transport office says someone found it on a bus, but he must describe it before collection.

"It's a dark brown leather wallet with a small silver button. There was about twenty pounds inside, a library card, and a bank card with my name on it. I was sitting upstairs on the number 44 at around 6:15."

LOST PROPERTY HOURS: Monday-Friday 9:30-16:30. Bring photo identification. Items are kept for 28 days.

Reading: what details prove the wallet may be Ellis's? Speaking: report a lost item, including appearance, contents, place, and approximate time.

Lesson 24: Giving Clear Explanations

At work, the card reader loses its internet connection. Maya explains a temporary payment method to a customer.

"The card machine is offline, so it cannot approve payments at the moment. You can pay in cash, or I can hold your order for thirty minutes while you visit the cash machine. I'm sorry for the inconvenience. We have already called the service company."

A clear explanation states what happened, how it affects the listener, what choices are available, and what is being done. Speaking: explain one situation: a class changes rooms, an item is unavailable, or an appointment runs late. Offer at least two practical choices.

Lesson 25: Festivals and Traditions

For International Evening, Maya describes a June festival from her hometown. Families decorate streets with colored flags, musicians play outdoors, and people share corn cakes and warm drinks. Maya enjoyed the games as a child, but now she most values the chance for neighbors to meet.

Hana notices similarities with a summer event in her town, although the food and music are different. They agree that traditions can change while their purpose remains important.

Writing: describe a local, family, or invented tradition in 100 words. Explain when it happens, what people do, how it began if known, and why it matters to you.

Lesson 26: Plans, Hopes, and Possibilities

Maya's photography course will end soon. She is going to make a small online portfolio, and she hopes to photograph community events. She might apply for an advanced class if her work schedule allows it. Ellis intends to begin a new office job, while Hana is thinking about a summer course abroad.

"Where do you see yourself next year?" Daniel asks.
"I hope I'll be more confident at work," Maya says. "I may also move to a full-time course, but I haven't decided yet."

Speaking: separate fixed arrangements, intentions, hopes, and uncertain possibilities in your own next six months. Use present continuous, going to, hope, may, and might appropriately.

Lesson 27: Messages for Different Readers

Maya cannot attend Tuesday's class. She writes to three people.

To Hana: "Hi! I can't make class Tuesday because I'm covering a shift. Could you send me the homework? Thanks!"

To Daniel: "Dear Daniel, I am unable to attend Tuesday's lesson because a colleague is ill. I will check the course page and complete the homework. Please let me know if there is any additional work. Best wishes, Maya"

To work: "I confirm that I can cover Priya's shift on Tuesday from 1:00 to 5:00."

Compare greeting, detail, contractions, and closing. Writing: report one change to a friend, a teacher, and a service provider, adjusting the tone each time.

Lesson 28: The International Evening - Integrated Practice

Reading: The library's International Evening attracted 160 visitors, forty more than expected. The welcome desk became crowded at 6:20 because many guests arrived early. Maya asked people who already had tickets to use a second entrance. This reduced the queue within ten minutes. Later, a storyteller's train was delayed, so Hana moved her drawing activity to the main hall and kept families busy. The story session began at 7:40 instead of 7:15. Feedback was positive, but several visitors requested clearer signs and more chairs next year.

Answer: What caused the first problem? How was it solved? Why did the schedule change? Which response shows that guests generally enjoyed the event? What two improvements were suggested?

Listening-style task: Cover the announcement and ask someone to read it twice: "Attention, volunteers. The food table will close at eight forty-five. Please take reusable dishes to the kitchen, put paper cups in the blue bin, and leave unopened food on the marked shelf. The final music performance will now begin at nine o'clock in Room Two." Note the closing time, three cleanup instructions, and the changed performance details.

Writing: Write a 120-word email from Maya to the library manager. Describe one successful part of the evening, explain two problems, and recommend two improvements. Use a polite opening and closing.

Speaking: Give a two-minute summary of the event with a clear beginning, middle, and end. Then role-play a conversation in which a visitor cannot find a reserved activity. Ask questions, explain the reason for a room change, compare two alternatives, and agree on a solution.
