English B1: Independent Voices
Word Hunter Originals

Introduction

Maya has lived in Greenford for two years. She can now handle most everyday situations, but she wants to tell richer stories, defend an opinion, and communicate confidently beyond familiar routines. The lessons form one continuous year in which she joins a community magazine and accepts a challenging work trip. Listening-style tasks can be completed when a partner reads the script or when you record it for later use.

Lesson 1: Taking the Next Step

Maya still works at the bakery, where she now supervises weekend shifts, but photography has become more than a hobby. Her pictures from local events have appeared on the library website, and she has begun to wonder whether communication work might suit her. When Greenford Voice advertises for volunteer contributors, she decides to attend an introductory meeting.

"I'm nervous because I've never written for a magazine," she tells Hana.
"They aren't expecting experts," Hana replies. "You notice details and listen to people. Those skills matter."

Speaking: describe a step you would like to take in work, study, or a personal interest. Explain your motivation, relevant experience, and main concern.

Lesson 2: A New Team

The magazine team includes Ruth, the editor; Dev, who covers local news; and Callum, who manages the website. During the meeting, Ruth asks everyone to suggest a first assignment. Maya proposes a photo essay about independent shops, while another volunteer wants to interview young musicians.

"Both ideas are worthwhile," Ruth says. "However, our next issue focuses on changing town centers. Could you combine them by asking shop owners how street events affect business?"

The team agrees on deadlines and responsibilities rather than competing for one idea. Writing: summarize the decision in a message to an absent member. State the theme, chosen approach, individual tasks, and deadline without repeating the entire discussion.

Lesson 3: Telling the Story of a Place

Maya visits Patel's Hardware, a narrow shop that has served Greenford for forty years. Mr Patel remembers when customers came mainly for tools and paint. Nowadays, the shop also repairs lamps and rents equipment because large online stores have changed buying habits.

"We have adapted, but the purpose is the same," he explains. "People arrive with a practical problem, and we help them solve it."

Maya photographs his handwritten repair book beside a modern card reader. The contrast becomes the center of her article. Writing: portray a familiar place through three specific details, one short memory, and one change over time. Give the description a clear focus rather than a list of facts.

Lesson 4: Conducting an Interview

Before interviewing a cafe owner, Maya prepares open questions: "How has the street changed?" and "What would encourage more visitors?" She avoids questions that can be answered only yes or no. During the conversation, she listens for interesting points and asks follow-up questions instead of following her list too strictly.

OWNER: Outdoor concerts bring new customers, although litter can be a problem.
MAYA: Could you give me an example?
OWNER: After last July's concert, our morning staff spent an hour cleaning the entrance.

Speaking: interview a partner about a change in their area or field. Ask for an example, clarify one point, and check a fact by saying, "So, if I understand correctly ..."

Lesson 5: From Notes to a Connected Article

Maya's first draft contains useful facts, but it jumps from one shop to another. Ruth suggests organizing it around a question: Can events help a shopping street without making life harder for local businesses? Maya begins with the lively market, presents benefits, then introduces concerns about noise and waste. She ends with practical proposals from the owners.

Connectors guide the reader. "While" can contrast two views; "as a result" shows a consequence; "in addition" adds a point; "for instance" introduces evidence. Writing: plan four paragraphs on a local change. Give each paragraph one job and choose connectors that show how the ideas relate.

Lesson 6: Receiving and Using Feedback

Ruth returns the draft with comments. She praises the strong photographs but says several claims are too general. Instead of writing "everyone enjoys street events," Maya changes the line to "most customers interviewed welcomed the monthly market." She also removes a dramatic sentence that her evidence cannot support.

At first, the number of corrections disappoints her. Then she realizes that feedback is about the draft, not her ability. She asks Ruth which change matters most and revises that section first.

Speaking: respond to critical feedback professionally. Ask for clarification, identify one point you accept, explain one choice you wish to keep, and agree on a practical next step.

Lesson 7: A Job Opportunity

Callum tells Maya that a regional food company needs a junior communications assistant. The job involves updating web pages, answering public enquiries, and visiting partner businesses. Applicants need clear writing and good organization; professional media experience is useful but not essential.

Maya's bakery knowledge is directly relevant, and her magazine work demonstrates initiative. However, she has never worked full time in an office. She decides to apply rather than rejecting herself before the employer can consider her.

Writing: match experience to a job requirement in a short supporting statement. Do not merely say you are organized; describe a situation in which you planned tasks, dealt with a change, and achieved a result.

Lesson 8: The Application

Maya writes:

Dear Hiring Manager,

I am applying for the position of Junior Communications Assistant. In my current role at Greenford Bakery, I supervise busy weekend shifts and respond to customer enquiries. As a volunteer for Greenford Voice, I interview local residents, check factual details, and produce articles to a deadline. These experiences have taught me to communicate calmly and adapt information for different readers. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my practical knowledge of local food businesses could support your team.

Yours faithfully,
Maya Costa

Writing: draft your own formal application paragraph. Support two qualities with evidence and relate them directly to the role.

Lesson 9: Preparing for an Interview

Hana helps Maya practise answers. For a question about a difficult situation, Maya describes a morning when two bakery colleagues were absent. She explains the situation briefly, states her responsibility, outlines how she reorganized breaks and reduced the menu, and reports that orders remained on time. Her first answer is too long, so she removes details that do not affect the result.

"What would you like to ask us?" Hana says at the end.
Maya asks how the team measures successful communication and what training new staff receive.

Speaking: prepare one connected example of teamwork or problem solving. Include context, action, result, and what you learned.

Lesson 10: The First Week

Maya gets the job. On her first day, her manager, Nathan, explains the content system and introduces colleagues in sales, food safety, and design. The amount of information is overwhelming, so Maya keeps a list with three headings: tasks I can do, tasks I need to practise, and questions for Friday.

When a colleague uses an unfamiliar abbreviation, she says, "Could you explain what DPL means in this project? I want to make sure I use it correctly." Asking a precise question helps her learn faster than pretending to understand.

Writing: create a first-week update to a manager. Mention progress, one obstacle, support already received, and a priority for next week.

Lesson 11: Clear Workplace Communication

Nathan asks Maya to update a product page by Thursday. On Wednesday, the food safety team changes two ingredients, but the designer is away and cannot replace the label image. Maya warns Nathan before the deadline rather than waiting until the task is late.

"The written update will be ready tomorrow," she explains. "The new image may take until Monday because design needs the approved label. I can publish the text first or hold the entire update. Which option do you prefer?"

This message states progress, cause, likely effect, and choices. Speaking: report a delay in a project. Avoid blame, distinguish facts from guesses, and propose a solution.

Lesson 12: Working Across Cultures

Maya's company works with producers from several countries. During an online meeting, one participant gives very direct feedback, while another expresses disagreement indirectly. Maya initially thinks the second person agrees, but their phrase "That may be difficult in the current form" actually signals a serious concern.

Nathan advises her not to treat one communication style as normal and others as strange. People also differ as individuals. The safest approach is to listen, avoid quick judgments, and confirm decisions in plain language.

Discussion: describe a time when tone, silence, humor, or formality caused confusion. Suggest respectful ways to check meaning without making assumptions about a whole culture.

Lesson 13: Planning Independent Travel

The company invites Maya to photograph a producers' conference in Bellweather, a coastal city she has never visited. She will travel alone, carry valuable equipment, and work to a fixed schedule. She compares rail and air routes, checks baggage rules, maps the hotel-to-venue journey, and stores copies of her booking details separately.

TRAVEL PLAN
Monday: 07:40 train to Bellweather, change at Longmere, arrive 11:25.
Conference registration: 14:00-17:00.
Hotel check-in after 15:00. Venue contact: Elena Ward.

Reading: identify the risky connection and the unplanned period. Add two sensible backup arrangements and one question for the venue contact.

Lesson 14: When the Journey Goes Wrong

Heavy rain closes the line beyond Longmere. Passengers may take a replacement coach, but it will not reach Bellweather until 14:30. Maya calls Elena before choosing what to do.

"I can arrive by coach this afternoon, although I'll miss the start of registration," Maya says. "Alternatively, there is a train to Northport and a local bus from there. It should arrive at 13:20, but the connection is only fifteen minutes."
Elena says the registration photographs can be taken later, so reliability matters more than speed. Maya takes the coach and uses the journey to charge her camera batteries.

Speaking: compare two disrupted travel options, including uncertainty, consequences, and priorities. Reach a reasoned decision.

Lesson 15: Solving a Hotel Problem

At the hotel, Maya discovers that the room safe is locked and the desk lamp does not work. Reception promises to send someone, but nobody arrives within an hour. She needs to leave for the venue and cannot safely store her equipment.

MAYA: I reported both faults at four fifteen. I understand the hotel is busy, but I need a secure place for work equipment before five forty-five.
RECEPTIONIST: I'm sorry about the delay. We can store the case in the manager's safe now and repair your room this evening.
MAYA: That solves the immediate problem. Could you give me a receipt for the case?

Role-play: make a firm but polite complaint, state the impact, request a specific action, and confirm the agreement.

Lesson 16: A Story from the Conference

At the conference, Maya meets Aisha, who turned an unused city car park into a community garden. At first, nearby residents doubted that vegetables would grow there. Volunteers built raised beds, collected rainwater, and tested the soil. The first harvest was small, yet it attracted enough interest for the council to extend the lease.

As Aisha speaks, Maya photographs hands exchanging seeds rather than simply taking formal portraits. Her final sequence shows uncertainty, shared work, and gradual success. Writing: tell a connected story about a project or experience. Establish the situation, introduce a challenge, describe a turning point, and reflect on the outcome.

Lesson 17: Advice and Recommendations

A new volunteer asks how to photograph people at public events. Maya replies, "You should introduce yourself and explain how the image may be used. If someone appears uncomfortable, don't pressure them. It is worth observing the light before the room becomes crowded. You could also prepare a short list of essential shots, but leave space for unexpected moments."

Advice varies in strength. "You must" signals necessity, "you should" is a clear recommendation, "you could" offers an option, and "you might want to" is gentler.

Speaking: advise someone beginning an activity you know. Prioritize the advice and explain the reason behind each suggestion.

Lesson 18: Writing a Formal Complaint

After returning home, Maya notices that the hotel charged her twice. She writes:

Dear Accounts Team,

I am writing regarding two charges of GBP 128 for booking BW417 on 18 June. The attached receipt shows that only one night was booked and paid for. I contacted reception by telephone yesterday, but the second charge remains on my account. Please refund the duplicate payment and confirm when it will be processed. I would appreciate a response within five working days.

Yours faithfully,
Maya Costa

A useful complaint includes evidence, previous action, requested outcome, and reasonable response time. Writing: adapt the structure to a faulty service or incorrect bill.

Lesson 19: Fact, Opinion, and Emphasis

Greenford Voice reports plans to replace an empty warehouse with flats and studios. A council document states that the site has been unused for eleven years. A campaign group calls the proposal "a once-in-a-generation opportunity," while an opponent describes it as "a threat to the character of the town." Those phrases express interpretation, not neutral facts.

Maya checks who produced each claim, whether figures have a source, and what relevant voices are missing. Reading: rewrite both emotional descriptions in neutral language. Discussion: should local reporting avoid strong quotations, or can they be useful when clearly attributed? Support your view.

Lesson 20: Following a News Story

The first report says the project will provide 80 homes. A later planning document reveals that 18 will be offered below market rent. The developer emphasizes new public space, whereas residents raise concerns about traffic. A transport study has been requested but not yet published.

Maya creates a timeline so that proposals, confirmed decisions, and unanswered questions do not become mixed. She updates the online article instead of presenting old information as current.

Writing: produce a 120-word update. Begin with the newest verified development, explain how it changes the earlier story, attribute opposing views, and name one fact that remains unknown.

Lesson 21: Culture and Belonging

The magazine hosts a discussion called "What Makes a Place Feel Like Home?" Some speakers value shared traditions; others mention familiar routes, trusted neighbors, or the freedom to be themselves. Maya says that belonging grew slowly for her. It began when people recognized her at the market, but it deepened when she was able to contribute rather than remain only a visitor.

One audience member worries that new customs may replace older ones. Another argues that culture has always developed through contact and change.

Discussion: define belonging in your own words. Respond respectfully to both views and use a personal observation, an example, or a comparison as support.

Lesson 22: An Environmental Choice

The company currently sends sample products in individual plastic containers. A supplier proposes reusable boxes, but they cost more at first and must be returned. Nathan asks Maya to collect staff opinions.

The warehouse manager supports a trial because waste collections are expensive. Sales staff worry that customers may forget to return boxes. Maya suggests testing the system with ten nearby businesses, measuring returns, damage, cost, and waste for three months.

Speaking: hold a decision meeting. Present environmental, financial, and practical factors; acknowledge a disadvantage in your preferred option; and propose a limited trial or compromise.

Lesson 23: A Short Report

After the trial, Maya writes:

Purpose: To assess reusable delivery boxes with ten local customers.

Findings: Ninety-two percent of boxes were returned. Packaging waste fell by approximately two thirds. Drivers spent an extra fifteen minutes per route collecting empty boxes. Two customers found the labels unclear.

Recommendation: Continue the trial for six months, redesign the return label, and include collection time in route planning.

A report uses headings and separates findings from recommendations. Writing: turn notes from a small survey, class activity, or imagined trial into a clear report. Include purpose, method, main evidence, limitation, and next action.

Lesson 24: Preparing a Presentation

Maya is asked to present the trial in five minutes. Rather than placing every figure on slides, she chooses one message: reuse reduced waste without seriously disrupting deliveries. Her opening explains why the issue matters, the middle presents three findings, and the ending recommends an expanded trial.

She rehearses with a timer and marks places to pause. When a slide fails to load, she continues because her notes contain the argument, not instructions such as "read chart."

Speaking: plan a three-minute presentation on a useful change. Include a signposted opening, two evidence-based points, one limitation, and a memorable final recommendation.

Lesson 25: Handling Questions

After the presentation, a director asks whether the trial was too small to be reliable. Maya does not become defensive.

"That's a fair concern," she says. "Ten customers cannot represent our entire network. The trial does show that the process can work locally, but we need a larger and more varied group before making a national decision."

When she does not know the exact fuel cost, she says she will check rather than inventing a figure. Useful phrases include "If I understand your question ...," "The evidence suggests ...," and "I don't have that figure with me, but ..."

Role-play a question session. Clarify one question, answer with evidence, and acknowledge one limit honestly.

Lesson 26: A Difficult Team Conversation

Callum publishes one of Maya's conference photographs without the credit line they had agreed. Maya first assumes he ignored her request, but she asks what happened. Callum explains that a website template removed all captions during an update. He has already restored some by hand but missed hers.

"I was frustrated because credits help me build a professional record," Maya says. "Could we add a check before future articles go live?"
Callum agrees and suggests a shared publication checklist.

Speaking: address a work or group problem using "I" statements. Describe the effect, listen to the explanation, avoid guessing motives, and agree on prevention rather than only blame.

Lesson 27: Looking Back, Looking Forward

At the end of the year, Maya rereads her first magazine article. Its structure now seems simple, but it reminds her how uncertain she felt. Since then, she has handled independent travel, written formal messages, reported news, and presented a workplace proposal. Confidence, she realizes, did not arrive before those tasks; it developed because she attempted them.

She plans to continue in communications and create a long-term photo project about people who improve overlooked spaces. She also wants to mentor the magazine's next new volunteer.

Writing: reflect on a period of learning. Describe an early challenge, two turning points, evidence of progress, and one realistic next step.

Lesson 28: The Riverside Proposal - Integrated Practice

Reading: Greenford Council plans a riverside walking and cycling route. Supporters argue that it will connect two neighborhoods, provide a safer journey to three schools, and attract visitors to local businesses. The estimated cost is GBP 2.4 million. Critics point out that twelve parking spaces would disappear and that construction could disturb wildlife for several months. An independent survey found broad support for the route, but only 38 percent of respondents knew about the parking change. The council has therefore extended consultation by three weeks and promised a public environmental report before any final vote.

Answer: What are the three claimed benefits? Which effects are temporary and permanent? Why might the survey result be incomplete? What has the council changed, and what has not yet been decided? Identify one fact, one prediction, and one opinion implied by the passage.

Listening-style task: Cover the briefing and have it read twice: "The public meeting will take place on Thursday the twenty-third at seven thirty in West Hall, not the council chamber. Speakers who wish to make a prepared statement must register by midday on Wednesday and keep their contribution under two minutes. Questions can be submitted online until Friday evening. A step-free entrance is available from River Lane." Take notes, then give the practical information to someone who wants to speak and needs step-free access.

Writing: Write a 180-word formal message to the council. State whether you support the route, oppose it, or support it with conditions. Refer to evidence from the reading, acknowledge a reasonable opposing concern, request one piece of further information, and recommend a practical action.

Speaking: Give a three-minute public statement with an introduction, connected argument, example, and conclusion. Then role-play a question period. One person is a resident, business owner, parent, or environmental adviser; the other summarizes the concern, asks follow-up questions, and seeks common ground. Finish by reporting the discussion neutrally to a third person.
