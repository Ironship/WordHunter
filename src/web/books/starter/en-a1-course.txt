English A1: First Steps
Word Hunter Originals

Welcome

This book follows Maya as she begins a new life in the town of Greenford. Read the scenes in order. Say the useful phrases aloud, change the details to make them true for you, and complete the short tasks. You do not need special recordings: a partner can read the listening scripts, or you can cover the text and ask someone to read it twice.

Lesson 1: Hello, Greenford

Maya arrives at her new language school. A man at the front desk smiles.

"Good morning. I'm Daniel. What's your name?"
"Hello. My name's Maya Costa. Nice to meet you."
"Nice to meet you too. Welcome to Greenford!"

Use "Good morning" before midday, "Good afternoon" later in the day, and "Good evening" when you meet someone in the evening. "Hi" and "Hello" are friendly at any time. When you leave, say "Goodbye," "See you later," or "Have a nice day."

Speaking: Greet three imaginary people: a teacher at 9:00, a friend at 14:00, and a hotel receptionist at 20:00. Introduce yourself each time.

Lesson 2: Personal Details

Daniel gives Maya a simple form.

FIRST NAME: Maya
FAMILY NAME: Costa
AGE: 22
ADDRESS: 18 King Street, Greenford
PHONE: 07700 900 825
EMAIL: maya.costa@example.com

"How do you spell your family name?" Daniel asks.
"C-O-S-T-A."
"And what's your phone number?"
"It's zero double seven zero zero, nine double zero, eight two five."

In English, an email address has "at" and "dot." Speaking: spell your name and say a phone number slowly. Writing: make your own form. You can invent details if you prefer.

Lesson 3: Countries and Languages

In class, everyone stands near a world map. Maya is from Brazil and speaks Portuguese and English. Hana is Japanese. She is from Osaka and speaks Japanese. Omar is from Egypt. He speaks Arabic and some French. Leo and Sofia are from Italy, so they speak Italian.

"Are you from Spain, Maya?" asks Leo.
"No, I'm not. I'm from Brazil. What about you?"
"I'm Italian, but I live in Greenford now."

Useful phrases: "Where are you from?" "What languages do you speak?" "I speak a little ..." Speaking: introduce two people from the class. Then say where you are from or where you live.

Lesson 4: Numbers Everywhere

Maya sees numbers on doors, buses, prices, and tickets. Her classroom is number 14. Her bus is the 62. A notebook costs three pounds fifty, and her ticket number is 108.

Say 13 carefully: thirteen. Say 30 with a strong first part: thirty. The same difference appears in fourteen and forty, fifteen and fifty. For 108, say "one hundred and eight." For 2,450, say "two thousand four hundred and fifty."

Listening-style task: Ask a partner to read these numbers in a different order while you write them: 16, 60, 19, 90, 117, 740. Check by reading your list back.

Lesson 5: Dates and the Calendar

The school term starts on Monday, 7 September. Maya's birthday is on 12 May. The class test is on Friday, 25 September. English dates can be said as "the seventh of September" or "September seventh."

"What day is it today?"
"It's Wednesday."
"What's the date?"
"It's the ninth of September."

There are twelve months, from January to December. Weekdays are Monday to Friday; the weekend is Saturday and Sunday. Writing: make a four-line calendar with a birthday, an appointment, a class, and a free day. Ask a partner, "When is your appointment?"

Lesson 6: What Time Is It?

Maya's class begins at nine o'clock. She takes a break at half past ten and eats lunch at quarter to one. Class ends at three fifteen, or quarter past three.

"Excuse me, what time is it?"
"It's twenty past eight."
"Oh! My bus leaves at eight twenty-five."

Use "at" with a time: at 7:30. Use "from ... to ..." for a period: The library is open from nine to six. Speaking: say when you get up, eat lunch, and go to bed. Then plan a meeting: "Are you free at half past five?"

Lesson 7: Meet the Family

Maya calls her mother, Rosa. Rosa lives with Maya's father, Paulo, and her younger brother, Tiago. Maya also has an older sister, Clara. Clara is married to Ben, and their daughter, Eva, is Maya's niece. Ben is Maya's brother-in-law.

"Do you have any brothers or sisters?" Hana asks.
"Yes, I do. I have one brother and one sister."
"Is your family big?"
"Not very big, but I have many cousins."

Draw or imagine a family tree. Speaking: describe four relationships with "This is ..." and possessive forms such as "Maya's sister" and "her niece."

Lesson 8: People and Personalities

Hana is short and has straight black hair. Leo is tall, with dark eyes and a beard. Daniel wears glasses. Appearance helps us identify people, but personality tells us how they act. Hana is quiet but friendly. Leo is funny and talkative. Daniel is patient and helpful.

"Which person is your teacher?"
"He's the tall man with glasses."
"What's he like?"
"He's kind and very calm."

"What does she look like?" asks about appearance. "What's she like?" asks about personality. Writing: describe a real or imaginary friend in four sentences. Include two appearance words and two personality words.

Lesson 9: A New Home

Maya rents a small flat. There is a kitchen beside the living room, a bedroom at the back, and a bathroom near the front door. There are two large windows, but there is not a garden. The flat has a table, a sofa, a bed, three chairs, and a bookcase.

Maya texts Hana: "My new flat is small but bright. There are boxes everywhere! Would you like to visit on Saturday?"

Use "there is" for one thing and "there are" for more than one. Writing: describe one room. Say what is in it and where two things are: on, under, next to, between, or opposite.

Lesson 10: Where Is the Key?

Maya cannot find her key. She looks on the table, under the sofa, inside her bag, and behind the door. At last, she finds it behind a blue bowl on the bookcase.

"Is it in your coat pocket?" Hana asks.
"No, it isn't."
"What about the blue bowl?"
"Yes! There it is, behind the bowl."

Useful requests: "Where is ...?" "Can you help me look for ...?" "Here it is." Speaking: put a small object somewhere in a room. Give a partner three clues. Writing: leave a message explaining where you put a spare key.

Lesson 11: A Weekday Routine

On weekdays Maya gets up at seven, has a shower, and gets dressed. She usually eats toast and fruit for breakfast. She leaves home at eight fifteen and walks to the bus stop. After class, she studies in the library. In the evening, she cooks, calls her family, and sometimes watches a series. She goes to bed around eleven.

"Do you always walk to school?"
"No. I usually take the bus, but I walk on sunny days."

Put frequency words before most verbs: I often cook. Put them after "be": I am often tired. Write six sentences about your normal day using always, usually, often, sometimes, and never.

Lesson 12: Chores at Home

On Saturday morning, Maya and her flatmate Sam share the chores. Sam cleans the bathroom and takes out the rubbish. Maya washes the dishes, does the laundry, and vacuums the living room. They both tidy the kitchen. Neither person likes ironing.

SAM'S NOTE
Please water the plants today. The clean towels are in the cupboard. Can you buy rubbish bags? Thanks!

"Can you clean the cooker, please?" is a polite request. "Sure," "No problem," and "Sorry, I can't right now" are natural replies. Speaking: divide six chores between two people. Explain when you can do each one.

Lesson 13: Breakfast, Lunch, and Dinner

Maya keeps eggs, cheese, milk, and vegetables in the fridge. There is some rice and there are some apples in the cupboard. There is not any coffee, and there are not any bananas.

"What do you have for lunch?" asks Omar.
"I often have soup or a sandwich. Today I've got a salad."
"Would you like some tea?"
"Yes, please, but no sugar."

Use "some" in positive statements and often in offers. Use "any" in most questions and negatives. Writing: list what you have at home. Then plan one simple meal from those ingredients.

Lesson 14: At the Grocery Store

Maya needs a loaf of bread, a bottle of oil, a carton of milk, six eggs, and two kilos of potatoes. At the market, she also asks for half a kilo of tomatoes.

"How much are these oranges?"
"They're two pounds a kilo."
"Can I have one kilo, please?"
"Of course. Anything else?"
"That's all, thank you."

Count apples with "How many?" Ask about rice or money with "How much?" Speaking: make a shopping list for two days, then role-play the customer and shop assistant. Remember "I'd like ..." and "Can I have ...?"

Lesson 15: At the Cafe

After shopping, Maya meets Leo at the Corner Cafe.

SERVER: Are you ready to order?
MAYA: Yes. I'd like the vegetable soup, please.
LEO: Can I have a toasted cheese sandwich without onion?
SERVER: Certainly. Would you like anything to drink?
MAYA: A glass of water for me, please.

Later Maya says, "Excuse me, can we have the bill, please?" The soup is hot and tasty, but Leo's sandwich is cold. He politely says, "Sorry, I ordered a toasted sandwich. Could you heat this, please?" Role-play a meal. Include an order, a change, and a request for the bill.

Lesson 16: Clothes for the Weather

It is cool and rainy, so Maya wears jeans, boots, a green sweater, and a waterproof jacket. Hana has a red umbrella. In summer they wear lighter clothes, such as T-shirts, shorts, skirts, and sandals.

"Whose scarf is this?"
"It's mine. The black gloves are Leo's."
"Are these your boots?"
"No, they're Sam's."

Useful words: this jacket, that coat, these shoes, those socks. Speaking: describe what you are wearing now. Then choose clothes for a hot beach day, a cold evening, and a formal dinner.

Lesson 17: Shopping for a Jacket

Maya tries on a jacket in a department store.

ASSISTANT: Can I help you?
MAYA: Yes, I'm looking for a light jacket.
ASSISTANT: What size are you?
MAYA: Medium. Can I try this one on?
ASSISTANT: Of course. The changing rooms are over there.
MAYA: It fits, but the sleeves are too long. Do you have a smaller size?

The jacket costs forty-five pounds. Maya pays by card and keeps the receipt. Speaking: role-play buying shoes or a shirt. Ask about color, size, price, and the place to try it on.

Lesson 18: Weather Today

Greenford's morning is cloudy and windy. At midday it rains, but by four o'clock the sun is shining. The forecast says: "Dry tonight, with a low of eight degrees. Tomorrow will be warm and sunny, with a high of nineteen."

"What's the weather like?"
"It's raining, and it's quite cold."
"What will it be like tomorrow?"
"It will be warmer."

Keep a three-day weather note. Include temperature, sun, cloud, rain, or wind. Speaking: compare today's weather with your favorite weather and say what you can do in each.

Lesson 19: Free Time

Maya enjoys reading, taking photos, and listening to music. She likes cooking, but she does not like washing dishes. Hana loves drawing. Omar collects old postcards, while Leo plays the guitar.

"What do you like doing in your free time?"
"I love cycling. What about you?"
"I prefer walking. I don't have a bike."

After like, love, enjoy, and hate, use a noun or an -ing form: "I enjoy tennis" or "I enjoy playing tennis." Writing: make a short profile for a hobby club. Include what you enjoy, when you are free, and what you want to try.

Lesson 20: Sport and Exercise

Every Tuesday, the class meets in the park. Omar can run fast and play football well. Maya can swim, but she cannot play tennis. Hana is learning to skate.

"Can you play volleyball?"
"A little. I can serve, but I can't hit the ball very hard."
"Let's practise together."

Use "can" for ability: I can dance. It does not change with he or she: She can dance. For a healthy week, Maya walks three times, swims once, and rests on Sunday. Plan your own balanced week. Speaking: ask a partner about four abilities and suggest one activity together.

Lesson 21: An Invitation

Maya sends a message:

Hi everyone! I'm having a small dinner at my flat on Friday at 7:00. Would you like to come? Please tell me by Thursday. You don't need to bring food. See you! Maya

Hana replies: "Thanks for inviting me. I'd love to come. Can I bring my sister?" Omar writes: "I'm sorry, I can't come because I work on Friday evening. Maybe next time."

Useful phrases: "Are you free on ...?" "Would you like to ...?" "That sounds great." "I'm afraid I can't." Writing: invite a friend to an activity. Give the day, time, place, and any important information.

Lesson 22: In the Classroom

The class has a whiteboard, a projector, dictionaries, and a shelf of readers. Today students work in pairs.

"What does 'quiet' mean?" Maya asks.
"It means 'not noisy,'" Daniel says.
"How do you pronounce this word?"
"Listen: comfortable. Now say it again."
"Could you repeat that more slowly, please?"

Other useful phrases are "How do you spell it?" and "Which page are we on?" Reading task: choose five words from this lesson. Write a simple definition or example for each. Speaking: ask for help with one unknown word without using your first language.

Lesson 23: School and Work

Hana is a design student. She studies from Monday to Thursday and works in a bakery on Saturdays. Omar is a mechanic. He repairs cars in a busy garage. Sam works from home as a customer service assistant. Maya is looking for part-time work.

BAKERY ASSISTANT WANTED
Saturday, 8:00-16:00. Help customers, pack bread, and keep the shop tidy. Friendly person needed. Email jobs@greenfordbakery.example.

Writing: make a short work or study timetable. Then answer the advertisement in three sentences: introduce yourself, say when you are available, and ask one question.

Lesson 24: Places in Town

Greenford has a library beside the town hall, a pharmacy opposite the bank, and a post office between a cafe and a small museum. The police station is behind the bus station. There is a sports center near the river, but there is no cinema.

"Is there a cash machine near here?"
"Yes. There's one outside the bank."
"Where can I buy stamps?"
"At the post office."

Draw a simple town map with eight places. Ask and answer where to borrow a book, see a doctor, buy medicine, catch a bus, and send a parcel.

Lesson 25: Asking for Directions

Maya is outside the station.

MAYA: Excuse me, how do I get to the library?
PASSER-BY: Go straight ahead and take the second left. Walk past the bank. The library is on your right, opposite the park.
MAYA: Is it far?
PASSER-BY: No, it's about five minutes on foot.
MAYA: Thank you very much.

Useful direction verbs are go, turn, cross, follow, and walk past. Speaking: use your map from Lesson 24. Give directions from the bus station to three places. The listener traces each route and says where it ends.

Lesson 26: Buses, Trains, and Bikes

Maya usually travels by bus. A single ticket costs two pounds, and a day ticket is five pounds. Today the 62 is late, so she checks the timetable.

BUS 62 TO HILLCREST
Greenford Station 09:10
Market Square 09:18
College Road 09:27
Hillcrest 09:41

"Does this bus go to College Road?"
"Yes, but you need the next bus. This one is full."

We go by bus, train, car, or bike, but we go on foot. Reading: How long is the trip from the station to Hillcrest? Speaking: compare two ways to reach school or work.

Lesson 27: A Train Journey

At the ticket office, Maya plans a day trip.

MAYA: A return to Seaton, please.
CLERK: When would you like to travel?
MAYA: This Saturday. I'd like to leave around nine.
CLERK: The 9:12 is direct. It arrives at 10:05.
MAYA: Which platform does it leave from?
CLERK: Platform four. Please check the screen on Saturday.

A single ticket is one way; a return includes the journey back. Direct means you do not change trains. Writing: note the destination, date, departure time, arrival time, platform, and ticket type for Maya's journey.

Lesson 28: At the Hotel

In Seaton, Maya stays one night at the Harbor Hotel.

RECEPTIONIST: Good afternoon. Do you have a reservation?
MAYA: Yes, it's under Costa.
RECEPTIONIST: A single room for one night, with breakfast.
MAYA: That's right. What time is breakfast?
RECEPTIONIST: From seven to ten. Here is your key card. Room 306 is on the third floor.

At 8:00 Maya calls reception: "Hello, there aren't any towels in my room. Could you bring some, please?" Role-play checking in. Ask about Wi-Fi, breakfast, checkout time, and one room problem.

Lesson 29: The Body

Maya joins a dance class and learns movement words: raise your arms, bend your knees, turn your head, and touch your toes. After class, her left foot hurts a little.

The main body words include head, face, neck, shoulder, arm, hand, finger, chest, stomach, back, leg, knee, foot, and toe. We have eyes, ears, a nose, and a mouth on the face.

Speaking: give a partner five safe movement instructions. Then describe a simple problem: "My back hurts" or "I've got a sore knee." The listener points to the correct body part and suggests rest.

Lesson 30: At the Doctor's

Maya wakes up with a sore throat and a headache. She calls the health center and gets an appointment.

DOCTOR: What's the matter?
MAYA: I've got a sore throat, and I feel tired.
DOCTOR: Do you have a temperature?
MAYA: Yes, it's thirty-eight degrees.
DOCTOR: Drink plenty of water and rest. Call us again if you feel worse.

Useful phrases: "I feel sick/dizzy/hot." "It hurts here." "How long have you felt like this?" Writing: make a symptom note with the problem, the start time, your temperature if known, and any medicine you take.

Lesson 31: At the Pharmacy

The doctor says Maya can ask a pharmacist about her sore throat.

PHARMACIST: How can I help?
MAYA: Do you have anything for a sore throat?
PHARMACIST: These tablets may help. Take one every four hours, after food. Do not take more than four in one day.
MAYA: Can I take them before bed?
PHARMACIST: Yes. Read the label, and ask a doctor if you are worried.

Reading: Underline how often, the maximum number, and one safety instruction. Speaking: role-play asking for something for a cough, a small cut, or dry skin.

Lesson 32: Phone Calls and Voicemail

Maya calls the bakery about the Saturday job.

RECEPTIONIST: Greenford Bakery. How can I help?
MAYA: Hello. Could I speak to Ms Lee, please?
RECEPTIONIST: I'm afraid she's busy. Can I take a message?
MAYA: Yes. This is Maya Costa. Please ask her to call me on 07700 900 825.

Voicemail: "Hi Maya, this is Karen Lee from the bakery. Please call me before five today."

On the phone, identify yourself and speak numbers clearly. Writing: leave a message with your name, reason for calling, number, and a good time to call back.

Lesson 33: Everyday Services

Maya has three jobs in town. At the post office she says, "I'd like to send this parcel to Italy. How much is it?" At the bank she asks, "Can I change my address, please?" At the hairdresser's she says, "I'd like a trim, but not too short."

POST OFFICE NOTICE
Take a number and wait for your turn. Keep your receipt. Last collection: 4:30 p.m.

Match each need to a service: send a birthday card, open an account, repair a phone, clean a coat, or cut hair. Speaking: choose one service and make a polite request. Ask about price and completion time.

Lesson 34: Requests and Small Problems

At the library, loud music comes from a phone.

MAYA: Excuse me, could you turn the music down, please?
MAN: Oh, sorry. I didn't know it was loud.

Later Maya tells the librarian, "The printer isn't working. Could you help me?" The librarian replies, "Certainly. Try this printer instead." Polite requests often use "Can you ...?" or "Could you ...?" Add "please" and thank the person.

Speaking: ask someone to open a window, repeat information, move a bag, or show you a machine. Reply positively once and give a polite reason for saying no once.

Lesson 35: Safety First

Maya reads signs in a public building.

FIRE EXIT - KEEP CLEAR
WET FLOOR - WALK CAREFULLY
STAFF ONLY - DO NOT ENTER
IN AN EMERGENCY, CALL 999

During a fire alarm, leave your things, follow the green exit signs, use the stairs, and meet outside. Do not use the lift. If someone is in immediate danger, call the emergency services. Say your location, describe the problem, and answer questions.

Reading: explain each sign in simple words. Speaking: practise a calm emergency call about smoke in a kitchen. Use an imaginary address and never make a real emergency call for practice.

Lesson 36: A Celebration

Maya's class finishes its first month, so they have a picnic. Hana brings fruit, Leo makes sandwiches, and Omar brings juice. Maya is carrying a small cake when everyone calls, "Congratulations!"

"Happy birthday!" is for a birthday. "Happy New Year!" welcomes a new year. "Congratulations!" is useful for good news such as a new job, a wedding, or passing a test. We often answer, "Thank you. That's very kind."

Writing: create a card for one occasion. Speaking: plan a small celebration. Decide the place, time, guests, food, music, and what each person will bring.

Lesson 37: What Happened Yesterday?

Yesterday Maya missed her bus. She walked to school, arrived ten minutes late, and apologized to Daniel. At lunch she met Hana, and they ate at a new cafe. In the evening, Maya cooked pasta, studied for an hour, and went to bed early.

"Did you have a good day?" Sam asks.
"Yes, but the morning was difficult. I didn't hear my alarm."

Regular past verbs often end in -ed: walked, arrived, cooked. Some common verbs are irregular: go-went, meet-met, eat-ate, have-had. Write six sentences about yesterday, including one negative sentence.

Lesson 38: Plans for Next Week

Maya is going to start her bakery job on Saturday. She is meeting Ms Lee at eight, and she is going to wear comfortable shoes. On Sunday she hopes to rest and call her parents.

"What are you going to do after class?"
"I'm going to buy groceries. Are you doing anything tonight?"
"Yes, I'm meeting Leo at seven."

Use "going to" for intentions. The present continuous can describe a fixed arrangement with a time. Writing: make plans for three days. Include one arrangement, one intention, and one thing you are not going to do.

Lesson 39: Forms, Messages, and Signs

Maya joins the library.

GREENFORD LIBRARY MEMBERSHIP
Full name: Maya Costa
Date of birth: 12 May 2004
Home address: 18 King Street, Greenford
Signature: Maya Costa

She receives a text: "Your library card is ready. Collect it after 10:00. Bring photo ID." At the desk, a sign says, "Please queue here. Cards must not be shared."

Reading: What must Maya bring, and when can she collect the card? Writing: complete an imaginary club form, then write a message under 25 words telling a friend where and when to meet you.

Lesson 40: A Day in Greenford - Integrated Practice

Reading: Maya's cousin Luis is visiting on Friday. He arrives at Greenford Station at 10:20. Maya plans to meet him by the ticket office. They will leave his bag at her flat, have lunch at the Corner Cafe, and visit the town museum. The museum is free and closes at 5:00. In the evening, they are going to cook together. Luis does not eat meat, and he needs to catch the 9:15 train on Saturday morning.

Answer in full sentences: Where and when will they meet? Why will they go to Maya's flat first? Which activity has a time limit? What food should Maya buy? What must Luis do on Saturday?

Listening-style task: Cover this paragraph. Ask a partner to read it twice, or record yourself and listen later: "The museum tour starts at quarter past two. Please arrive ten minutes early at the main door. The tour takes one hour. Large bags must stay at reception." Note the start time, arrival time, meeting place, duration, and bag rule.

Writing: Write Maya's message to Luis. Include the meeting details, lunch plan, afternoon activity, weather advice, and one question about food. Write 60-80 words.

Speaking: Role-play two scenes. First, Luis phones because his train is twenty minutes late; exchange greetings, identify the problem, and change the meeting time. Second, order a meat-free lunch at the cafe and ask about one ingredient. Finish by speaking for one minute about a visitor's ideal day in your town. Use times, directions, transport, food, and a polite invitation.
