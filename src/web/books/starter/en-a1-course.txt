Lantern House: An A1 Graded Reader

Chapter 1: A New Stop

Nell Carter sat by the train window. She had one blue bag and one small case. Her coat was on her knees. Outside, green fields went past. Then she saw the sea.

The sea was grey under a pale sky. White birds flew over the water. Nell put her hand on the cold glass. She had seen the sea many times, but she had never lived near it.

The train became slow. A voice came from a speaker.

"The next stop is Morrowbay. This train ends here."

Nell stood up. Her case hit the seat.

"Sorry," she said to an old man across the aisle.

He smiled. "First day in town?"

"Yes. Is it easy to find the harbour?"

"Very easy. Go out of the station and walk down the hill. You cannot miss the sea."

The station was small. Its walls were yellow, and there were flower boxes under the windows. A clock above the door said four twenty. Nell checked the message on her phone.

LANTERN HOUSE
14 Beacon Lane
Please arrive before 5:00 p.m.
Ask for Mae Chen.

She walked out of the station. The town went down a long hill. Nell could see red roofs, a church tower, and the harbour. The air smelled of rain and salt.

Her bag was heavy. One wheel on the case made a hard sound on the road. At the first corner, she stopped beside a map. Beacon Lane was near the water, but the little streets were not straight.

A man was closing a flower shop. He wore a green apron.

"Excuse me," Nell said. "Where is Beacon Lane?"

"Turn left at the clock. Then take the second street on the right. Lantern House has a blue door."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome. Walk quickly. More rain is coming."

Nell found the clock in a small square. It stood on a black post. Under it, two teenagers waited for a bus. She turned left and passed a bakery, a bookshop, and a narrow cafe.

The rain began as she reached Beacon Lane. It was soft at first. Then large drops hit her face. She ran with her case.

Number 14 was a tall white house. A brass lantern hung over its blue door. In one window there was a card.

ROOMS AND BREAKFAST
VACANCIES THIS WEEK

Nell rang the bell. A woman with short dark hair opened the door. She looked at Nell's wet coat and broken case wheel.

"You must be Nell," she said. "Come in before the weather follows you."

Nell stepped into a warm hall. The door closed behind her. For the first time that day, she put down both bags.

"I am Mae Chen," the woman said. "Welcome to Lantern House."

Chapter 2: Lantern House

The hall had a red floor and white walls. There were old pictures of Morrowbay beside the stairs. A wooden desk stood near the front door. Behind it, many keys hung on small hooks.

Mae gave Nell a clean towel.

"Your room is at the top," she said. "It is not large, but it is quiet. You can stay there while you look for a flat."

"Thank you. I did not expect a room with the job."

"It is only for six weeks. After that, we can talk again."

Nell knew this. The job was for six weeks too. She was the new morning assistant. She would serve breakfast, answer the phone, and help guests.

Mae looked at the clock. "Have you eaten?"

"Only a sandwich on the train."

"Then leave your bags here. Dinner is ready."

They walked through a small sitting room. Two guests were reading near the fire. One was a woman in a purple hat. The other was an older man with silver hair. He looked up from his newspaper.

"New guest?" he asked.

"New member of staff," Mae said. "This is Nell Carter. Nell, this is Robin Vale. He stays with us every spring."

Robin folded his paper. "A brave time to start. Easter week is never quiet."

"Do not frighten her," Mae said.

The kitchen was at the back of the house. It had green cupboards and a long table. A pot of soup was on the stove. Nell sat down while Mae cut bread.

"Lantern House has nine guest rooms," Mae said. "Rooms one to five are on the first floor. Six to nine are above them. My flat is behind the kitchen. Your room is in the attic."

"How many guests are here now?"

"Seven. Tomorrow we will have thirteen."

Nell stopped with her spoon in the air. "Thirteen people and nine rooms?"

"Some rooms have two people." Mae smiled. "Do not worry. We begin early, but we do one thing at a time."

After dinner, Mae showed her the dining room. Eight small tables filled the room. Each table had a white cloth and a little glass jar. Mae put yellow flowers in the jars every morning.

On the wall was a simple breakfast list.

BREAKFAST: 7:00-9:30
Tea or coffee
Toast and jam
Fruit and yoghurt
Eggs on request

"Most guests are kind," Mae said. "Some are tired. Some are in a hurry. We stay calm."

At last, Nell carried her bags upstairs. Her room had a bed, a chair, and a round window. The window looked over the roofs to the harbour lights.

She called her younger brother, Jamie.

"Are you there?" he asked.

"Yes. The house is old, my room is tiny, and I start at six thirty."

"That sounds terrible."

Nell looked at the lights on the dark water.

"No," she said. "It sounds new."

Chapter 3: First Morning

Nell's alarm rang at six. For a few seconds, she did not know where she was. Then a sea bird cried outside the round window.

She put on black trousers and a clean white shirt. Mae had told her to wear comfortable shoes. Nell went down three flights of stairs. The house was silent.

In the kitchen, Mae was already making coffee.

"Good morning," Mae said. "Did you sleep?"

"Yes, but I dreamed about train times."

"Soon you will dream about eggs."

Mae showed Nell the breakfast orders. Each room had a line on a sheet.

ROOM 2: tea, toast
ROOM 3: coffee, two eggs
ROOM 5: tea, one egg, no butter
ROOM 7: coffee, fruit

"Read every line," Mae said. "A fast mistake is still a mistake."

Nell carried cups into the dining room. She put knives on the right and forks on the left. She checked each table twice.

At seven, Robin Vale came in. He wore a brown jacket and a bright yellow scarf.

"Good morning, Nell. Coffee, please. Strong enough to wake the street."

"I can bring strong coffee. The street must wake by itself."

Robin laughed. Nell felt less afraid.

More guests came down. A family from Halewick sat by the window. Their little boy wanted hot chocolate, not tea. A woman in Room 5 asked for butter. Nell looked at the order sheet.

"I thought you asked for no butter," she said.

"My husband does not eat butter. I do."

"Of course. I will bring some."

Nell went back to the kitchen. Mae was putting eggs on plates.

"Room 5 needs butter," Nell said. "The note was for one person, not both."

"Good. You asked before you guessed."

At eight fifteen, every table was full. Cups came back empty. Toast became cold. A spoon fell on the floor. The phone rang while someone called for more coffee.

Nell took a slow breath. She answered the phone first.

"Good morning, Lantern House. Nell speaking."

A man wanted a room for Friday night. Nell opened the booking book.

"One single room is free," she said. "It is sixty-eight pounds with breakfast."

He gave his name, and she wrote it carefully. When the call ended, she read the details to Mae.

By nine thirty, the dining room was empty. It looked like a small storm had passed through it. There were crumbs on every table.

Nell picked up a cup and found a five-pound note under it. Robin stood in the hall, putting on his scarf.

"Mr Vale, you left some money."

"That is for you and Mae."

"Oh. Thank you."

"You kept the coffee moving. That is important work."

When the door closed, Mae gave Nell half of the money.

"You did well," she said. "Tomorrow will feel easier."

Nell looked at the full washing-up tray.

"When does it feel easy?"

Mae laughed. "I said easier."

Chapter 4: Room Seven

After breakfast, Nell helped Mae clean the empty rooms. Mae opened the windows. Nell changed the sheets and put fresh towels by each bed.

Room Seven was different from the others. It was at the front of the house and had a view of the sea. The curtains were dark blue. A small writing desk stood by the window.

"This is our best room," Mae said. "A couple will arrive at three. They are here for their wedding anniversary."

Nell put two clean glasses on a tray. Mae added a card.

WELCOME TO MORROWBAY
We hope you enjoy your stay.

At two thirty, the room was ready. At three, no one came. At four, the front bell was still quiet.

Mae checked the booking book. "Mr and Mrs Fox. Two nights. They asked for a sea view."

At four twenty, a taxi stopped outside. A tall man got out. Then the driver took a wheelchair from the back of the taxi. A woman sat in it.

Nell opened the door.

"Welcome to Lantern House," she said.

The woman looked at the stairs. "Is there a lift?"

Nell's stomach became cold. Lantern House had no lift. Room Seven was two floors up.

Mae came into the hall. "Mrs Fox, I am very sorry. I did not receive a note about the wheelchair."

Mr Fox showed her a printed email. The request was there. It had arrived on a day when Mae was away. A temporary worker had read it, but she had not put it in the booking book.

"We cannot use those stairs," Mr Fox said. He was not angry, but he looked tired.

Mae thought for a moment. There was one room on the ground floor, behind the sitting room. Mae used it for boxes and extra chairs.

"Please have tea with us," Mae said. "Give us twenty minutes."

Nell and Mae ran to the back room. They moved six boxes into the kitchen. Nell carried out two chairs. Mae found clean bedding. The room had a wide door and a small bathroom. It did not have a sea view, but its window looked into the garden.

Nell brought in yellow flowers. Then she took the card from Room Seven and put it on the table.

Mrs Fox came to see the room. She touched the flowers.

"This is lovely," she said. "I like gardens more than stairs."

Mr Fox smiled for the first time.

That evening, Nell sat on the front steps with a cup of tea. She felt tired and angry about the missed note.

Mae sat beside her. "We solved today's problem," she said. "Tomorrow I will change our booking form. Important needs must be at the top, not in a long message."

"Will the back room stay a guest room?"

"Yes. We should have done it years ago."

Through the open window, Nell could hear Mrs Fox laughing. Room Seven stayed empty that night. The smaller room was the right room.

Chapter 5: Two Blue Bags

Friday was busy. Three guests left before eight. Four new guests arrived after lunch. Nell carried bags, checked names, and answered the phone.

At three, two men came in at the same time. Both had blue bags.

The first man was young and wore a black cap. "Booking for Adam Reed," he said.

The second man had grey hair. He looked at Nell. "My name is Andrew Reed. I also have a booking."

Nell opened the book. She saw only one line.

A. REED - SINGLE - FRIDAY - TWO NIGHTS

She checked the paper forms. There were two forms, but one was under the wrong date. Both men had paid a small amount before their visit. Both needed a room.

"I am sorry," Nell said. "Please sit down. I need to check this with Mae."

Mae was at the market. No single rooms were free. The only empty room was Room Seven, with one large bed.

The two men did not know each other. They did not want to share.

Nell called three other guesthouses. The first was full. The second did not answer. The third had a room, but it cost more.

"Lantern House will pay the extra cost," Nell told Andrew. "It is two streets away, and I can carry your bag there."

Andrew looked at Adam. "Why must I move?"

Adam took off his cap. "I can go. I know this town, and you do not. My sister lives near the other place."

Nell was grateful, but she wanted the choice to be fair.

"Room Seven is a double room," she said. "It costs more, but there will be no extra charge. One of you can stay there. The other can stay at Rosebank House, and we will pay the difference."

Andrew chose Room Seven. Adam said Rosebank House was fine.

Nell put a new label on Adam's blue bag. She carried it to the other guesthouse. When she came back, Mae was at the desk.

"I made the booking mistake," Mae said. "I wrote one name before the second form arrived. Then I put the form under Saturday."

"Both names looked the same in the book."

"That is why first names matter."

Mae changed the page. She wrote full names and phone numbers for every booking. She also wrote the room number beside each name.

At six, Adam returned to Lantern House. Nell was surprised.

"Is there a problem at Rosebank?" she asked.

"No. I came for this." He held up a blue bag.

Nell looked behind the desk. Another blue bag was there.

Andrew came down the stairs. He opened the bag in Adam's hand. Inside were Andrew's shoes and shirts. The men had taken each other's bags from the taxi.

For one second, the hall was silent. Then Andrew began to laugh.

"Two A. Reeds and two blue bags," he said. "Your Friday was always going to be difficult."

Chapter 6: Farah's Table

Nell had Monday afternoon free. She needed a few things for her attic room: a lamp, a warm blanket, and a cup. Mae told her about a small shop on Gull Street.

"Farah Malik works there on Mondays," Mae said. "Tell her you are from Lantern House. She knows everyone."

The shop was called Second Morning. It sold old chairs, dishes, books, and clothes. A yellow bicycle stood in the window. Inside, music played softly.

A woman with long black hair was painting a small table. There was a green line on her cheek.

"Are you Farah?" Nell asked.

"That depends. Do I owe you money?"

Nell smiled. "Mae sent me."

"Then I am Farah. You must be the new person in the attic."

Nell wondered how Farah knew this already.

Farah showed her three lamps. One was beautiful but expensive. One did not work. The third was plain and blue.

"This one is twelve pounds," Farah said. "It also has a new plug. That is the useful part."

Nell chose the blue lamp and a red cup. She found a soft grey blanket in a basket.

At the back of the shop, there was a little table with a card on it.

ROUND KITCHEN TABLE
Good condition
Seats four
Collection only
GBP 25

"I like that," Nell said. "But I do not have a kitchen."

"I do," Farah said. "My old flatmate moved out last week. I have a table and no one to sit at it."

Farah made tea in the shop kitchen. They sat at the round table. Nell told her about the job and the tiny attic room.

"Are you looking for a flat?" Farah asked.

"Yes, but only when Mae decides to keep me. My job may end in six weeks."

"My spare room is free now. I can wait a few weeks for the right person."

Nell looked at her. "We met ten minutes ago."

"That is true. You may be terrible. I may be terrible too. We can have dinner first."

Farah worked in the shop only one day a week. On other days, she made cakes at Harbour Kitchen. She also made clothes for a small theatre group.

"I am busy, but I always eat on Tuesday evening," she said. "Come at seven. You can see the flat."

Nell wrote the address on her phone.

When she left, the rain had stopped. Farah stood in the shop door.

"You forgot your lamp!"

Nell went back for it.

"Perhaps I am terrible," she said.

"Perhaps," Farah said. "But you have a good cup."

Chapter 7: The Window in the Rain

The wind became strong on Tuesday night. Rain hit Nell's round window. At midnight, a drop of water fell on her pillow.

Nell moved the bed. She put a bowl under the window. By morning, the bowl was half full.

Mae looked at the wet wall after breakfast.

"The frame is old," she said. "Kit can look at it today."

"Who is Kit?"

"He repairs half the doors and windows in Morrowbay. He also forgets to send bills."

At eleven, someone knocked on the attic door. Nell opened it. A man stood on the stairs with a red tool bag. He had rain on his dark curls.

"Kit Morgan," he said. "I am here to meet a bad window."

Nell pointed to it. "It is over there. It began the fight."

Kit moved the chair and looked at the frame. He pressed the wood with one finger.

"This part is weak. I can stop the water today, but the frame needs more work when the weather is dry."

He went outside to get a ladder. Nell watched from the window as he put it against the wall. The wind pulled at his jacket.

Mae came upstairs. "Kit will not work high in this wind," she said. "The window can wait."

Kit came back in at once. "Mae is right. I can work from inside today."

He fixed a clear cover around the weak part. Then he put a clean line of material along the glass. He did not hurry.

"Try the window now," he said.

Nell opened it a little and closed it again. The handle moved well.

"How long have you done this work?" she asked.

"Nine years. My aunt had a building company. I learned from her. Now I have my own workshop."

He saw the blue lamp from Second Morning.

"Farah sold you that."

"Does everyone know every lamp in this town?"

"Only the important lamps."

Kit wrote a short note for Mae.

ATTIC WINDOW
Temporary repair complete.
Keep the bed away from the wall until the wall is dry.
New lower frame needed in calm weather.

Before he left, he looked at the sea through the glass.

"The view is good from here," he said.

"It is better when it is not coming into the room."

Kit smiled. "Fair point."

That evening, Nell went to Farah's flat for dinner. The flat was above a closed bank. It had two bedrooms, a bright kitchen, and the round table from the shop.

Kit was there with a bag of bread.

"You know the table too," Nell said.

"It is a very important table," he replied.

Chapter 8: Tuesday Dinner

Farah made tomato soup and baked cheese bread. Kit cut the bread. Nell put bowls on the round table.

The flat felt warm and lived in. There were plants on the windows. Colourful cloth lay over one chair. A small radio stood by the sink.

"My last flatmate was my cousin," Farah said. "She got a job in Northcross. She left six plants and took all the good spoons."

"I have no plants," Nell said. "I have four spoons."

"Then you may be the right person."

They ate soup. Kit told them about a door he had repaired that morning. It was the front door of a sweet shop. The owner wanted it to close slowly, but a small dog pushed it open all day.

Farah told Nell about Harbour Kitchen. It was a cafe near the fish market. Farah started work at five every morning.

"And I thought six was early," Nell said.

"At six, I have already made forty rolls."

After dinner, Farah showed Nell the spare room. It was larger than the attic room. It had a high window and an empty bookcase.

"Rent is four hundred and ninety pounds a month," Farah said. "That includes water. We share electricity, heat, and internet."

"When do you need an answer?"

"Not tonight. First, find out about your job."

Nell liked the room. She also liked that Farah did not push her.

They returned to the kitchen. Kit had washed the bowls.

"Do you always wash dishes in other people's homes?" Nell asked.

"Only when I want more cheese bread."

Farah gave him the last piece.

At nine, Kit left for his workshop. Nell stayed for another cup of tea.

"How long have you known him?" she asked.

"Since school. His family moved here when he was twelve. He was quiet then."

"He is quiet now."

"You did not know him then."

Nell looked around the kitchen again. On the wall was a list.

TUESDAY
Buy rice
Call Mum
Finish blue dress
Water plants
Dinner with Nell

Her name was already part of Farah's week. It was a small thing, but it made Nell happy.

When Nell walked back to Lantern House, the streets were wet and clear. She passed the clock in the square. It was ten past nine.

A person was standing under it, but Nell could not see the face. A long coat moved in the wind. Then a bus came between them. When it passed, the person was gone.

Nell thought about it for a moment. Then she went home to her dry attic room.

Chapter 9: Saturday Market

On Saturday, Mae sent Nell to the market. Lantern House needed eggs, apples, flowers, and two jars of orange jam.

"Take the green basket," Mae said. "And check the eggs before you leave the stall."

The market filled Market Square. Red and white covers kept off the light rain. People sold fruit, bread, cheese, clothes, and old records. A man played a violin near the clock.

Nell bought thirty eggs from a woman called Grace. She opened both boxes and checked each egg. Then she bought apples from the next stall.

At the flower stall, the man in the green apron knew her.

"You found Beacon Lane," he said.

"I did. You were right about the rain too."

His name was Tom. He chose yellow flowers for the dining-room tables.

"Mae always buys these," he said. "Her husband liked them."

Nell did not know that Mae had a husband. There were no family pictures in the kitchen.

At the jam stall, only one jar of orange jam was left.

"More will come at one," the seller said.

Nell looked at the time. She had to return by twelve. She bought one orange jar and one lemon jar.

On her way back, she heard someone call her name. Farah was behind a cake stall. She wore a white apron.

"Try this," Farah said. She gave Nell a small piece of apple cake.

"Is this breakfast?"

"It has fruit in it."

Nell ate the cake. It was warm and sweet.

"Mae needs orange jam," Nell said. "Do you know another place?"

Farah pointed across the square. "The food shop on Bell Street. It closes at twelve thirty."

Nell went to Bell Street. The orange jam there cost one pound more. She bought it and kept the receipt.

Back at Lantern House, Mae checked the basket.

"Two orange jams, no broken eggs, and yellow flowers," she said. "A successful trip."

"One jar cost more. Here is the receipt."

Mae put it in a small metal box. "Thank you. Clear records make quiet evenings."

Nell placed the flowers on the tables. In the last jar, she found an old piece of paper under the water mark. It was not part of the flower paper. It was a corner of a photograph.

The picture showed a young woman on a small stage. She held a guitar. Her hair was long, but Nell knew her face.

It was Mae.

Chapter 10: A Message from Home

Nell put the piece of photograph on Mae's desk.

"I found this in the flower box," she said.

Mae looked at it. For a second, she did not speak.

"That box has been in the back room for years," she said. "This must have fallen out of an old album."

"Were you in a band?"

"A very small one. A very long time ago."

Mae put the photograph in a drawer. Then the phone rang, and she went to answer it.

Nell wanted to know more, but Mae was busy. Nell carried clean towels upstairs.

Her own phone rang in Room Four. It was her father, Peter.

"Hello, Dad."

"Your mother says you never call."

"I called on Wednesday."

"You sent a message. That is not the same. How is the hotel?"

"It is a guesthouse. The work is good."

"For six weeks. What happens after that?"

Nell sat on the bed. Her father liked plans. He had worked in the same office for twenty-eight years. When Nell left her shop job in Dunley, he did not understand.

"I may stay," she said.

"May is not a plan. There is a full-time job at Martin's shop. Your old manager told me."

"I do not want to go back there."

"You need regular work."

Nell looked out of the window. Kit was crossing the street below with a piece of wood under his arm.

"I am at work now, Dad. I must go."

"Think about the shop. Please."

After the call, Nell stayed in the room for a minute. She knew her father wanted her to be safe. He did not know that the old job had made every day feel the same.

At lunch, Mae found her in the kitchen.

"Bad news?" she asked.

"No. An old job is free. My father wants me to take it."

"Do you want it?"

"No. But this job ends soon."

Mae cut an apple into four pieces. "We will talk about that next week. I need time to look at the summer bookings."

It was not an answer, but it was hope.

That afternoon, a postcard arrived for Lantern House. There was no stamp. Someone had put it through the door by hand.

The front showed a blue boat under a yellow moon. On the back, there were only eleven words.

MAE - FRIDAY, SIX O'CLOCK. UNDER THE CLOCK. I FOUND YOUR SONG.

There was no name.

Chapter 11: The Blue Postcard

Nell read the postcard twice. Then she took it to Mae.

Mae was adding numbers in the booking book. She saw the blue boat and put down her pen.

"Where did you get this?"

"It came through the door. There is no name."

Mae turned the card over. Her face was very still.

"Do you know who sent it?" Nell asked.

"Perhaps."

"What song did they find?"

Mae closed the booking book. "It is an old matter. Thank you for bringing the card to me."

She put it in her pocket. Then she went into her flat and closed the door.

At dinner, Mae did not eat much. She checked the wall clock several times. At five forty-five, she put on a dark coat.

"I am going out," she told Nell. "Robin is in the sitting room. Please listen for the front bell."

"Will you be long?"

"I do not know."

Mae left. Rain had started again. Nell watched her walk towards Market Square.

Robin Vale came into the hall. "Mae looks worried."

"She has a meeting."

Robin looked at Nell. "Under the clock?"

Nell was surprised. "How do you know?"

"I saw the postcard on the desk. I know the picture."

He explained that the blue boat was on an old record cover. The record was made by a local band called Low Tide. Mae had played guitar with them when she was nineteen.

"Were you in the band too?" Nell asked.

"No. I taught music at their school. Mae wrote one beautiful song. They played it once at the summer hall. Then the band ended."

"Why?"

Robin looked towards Mae's closed door. "That story belongs to Mae."

At half past six, the front bell rang. Nell ran to open the door, but Mae was not there. Kit stood outside. Water ran from his jacket.

"I brought the wood for the attic window," he said. "I can come back tomorrow."

Nell let him in. "Mae is out."

At seven, Mae still had not returned. Nell made tea for herself and Kit. They sat near the front desk.

At seven fifteen, the door opened. Mae came in alone. Her hair was wet.

"Did the person come?" Nell asked.

Mae took the blue postcard from her pocket. It was soft from the rain.

"No," she said. "No one came."

Chapter 12: Under the Clock

The next morning, Mae worked as usual. She made coffee, cooked eggs, and talked to the guests. She did not speak about the meeting.

Nell did not ask. She knew Mae would tell her when she was ready.

At ten, Kit came to repair the attic window. The wind was quiet, and the sky was clear. He put a ladder against the house. Mae stayed in the garden while he worked.

Nell cleaned Room Six. From its window, she could see Market Square. The clock stood above the people and market stalls.

She saw the man from the record shop. She saw two women with shopping bags. Then she saw Robin Vale under the clock.

Robin was speaking to a thin man in a long brown coat. The man gave him a white envelope. Robin put it inside his jacket.

Nell moved away from the window. She felt wrong for watching them.

At lunch, Robin returned to Lantern House. He went straight to his room. He did not stop at the desk.

Mae was in the garden with Kit. Nell made sandwiches in the kitchen. She heard slow steps behind her.

Robin stood in the doorway. He held the white envelope.

"I need to give this to Mae," he said.

"She is outside."

"I know. I am trying to find the right words first."

He sat at the kitchen table. The envelope had MAE written on it in blue ink.

"Was that man under the clock the person with her song?" Nell asked.

Robin nodded. "His name is Leon Price. His older brother was in Low Tide. Leon found a box after his brother died. There may be a tape in it."

"Why did he not meet Mae last night?"

"He became afraid. He thought she might be angry."

Mae came in through the garden door. She saw the envelope.

"Robin," she said quietly. "What have you done?"

"Only what Leon asked. He wanted me to bring this."

Mae opened the envelope. Inside was a small key and a note.

The box is at Seabird Storage, unit 18. It belongs to you now. I am sorry I did not wait last night. Leon.

Mae sat down. She held the key in her hand.

"Do you want me to come with you?" Robin asked.

"Not today."

Nell put the sandwiches on the table. No one ate them.

After a long silence, Mae looked at Nell.

"Low Tide made a tape before the band ended," she said. "My song was on it. I thought the tape was lost."

"What happened to the band?"

Mae closed her hand around the key. "We stopped trusting one another. That is a longer story."

Chapter 13: Busy Friday

Friday began with a broken coffee pot. At seven ten, the handle came off in Nell's hand. Hot coffee stayed in the pot, but Nell could not carry it.

"Use the small pots," Mae said. "We have three."

The small pots made more work. Nell filled them many times. One guest wanted coffee in the garden. Another wanted breakfast in Room Eight because her baby was asleep.

At eight, a group of six walkers arrived. They were not guests yet. They wanted to leave their bags before their rooms were ready.

Nell wrote a number on each bag. She gave every person a card with the same number.

"Please keep the card," she said. "Your bags will be in the back room until two."

One man put his card in his hat. Another took a picture of his number with his phone.

At nine, the doorbell rang again. A delivery driver had four boxes for Lantern House. Mae was cooking, so Nell checked the name and address.

The boxes were for Lantern Court, not Lantern House. Lantern Court was a block of flats across town.

"These are not ours," Nell said.

The driver looked at his screen. "Number 14, Beacon Lane."

"That is our address, but the name is wrong."

He checked again and found a second address on the order. He took the boxes back to his van.

At ten, breakfast ended. Nell had not eaten. She made toast and stood by the kitchen sink.

Mae came in. "You are very quiet."

"If I speak, someone may ask for a room."

Mae laughed. "The house cannot hear you."

The phone rang.

Nell pointed at it. "It can."

The call was from a woman called Iris Chen. She was Mae's daughter. She planned to arrive on Sunday.

"Please do not tell Mum," Iris said. "It is a surprise."

Nell looked through the kitchen door. Mae was only a few steps away.

"Of course," Nell said. "How long will you stay?"

"Only one night. I have work in Redwater on Monday."

Nell wrote IRIS - SUNDAY in her own notebook and closed it.

The day stayed busy. When the six walkers returned, every bag was in the right room. The coffee pot was still broken, but all three small pots were clean.

At nine that evening, Nell went to her attic. Kit had finished the window. The new wood was pale and smooth. On the chair was a short note.

No more indoor rain. The paint will be dry by Saturday. - Kit

Nell put the note beside her blue lamp. It was the best message of the day.

Chapter 14: The Wrong Room

On Saturday afternoon, Nell made her first big mistake.

A guest called Clara Dean arrived early. Her room was not ready, so Nell asked her to wait in the garden. Clara gave Nell a small red case.

"Please put it in my room," she said. "There is a glass gift inside."

Nell wrote ROOM 3 on the luggage label. Then the phone rang. While she answered it, a cleaner moved the case behind the desk.

At two, Room Three was ready. Nell carried a red case upstairs. She put it beside the bed.

At three, Clara came back. Nell gave her the key. Ten minutes later, she came down again with the case.

"This is not mine," she said.

Nell looked at the label. It said ROOM 3, but the case was darker than Clara's. It belonged to a guest in Room Eight.

Nell ran upstairs. Room Eight was open. A young woman was looking inside Clara's case.

"I did not take anything," the woman said. "I opened it because I thought it was mine."

Inside, a glass bird lay in white paper. It was not broken.

Nell took both cases to the desk. She checked the small name cards. The cases were almost the same colour and size.

"I am very sorry," she told both guests. "I looked only at the room label. I did not check the name."

Clara held the glass bird near the window. Light moved through its blue wings.

"It is a gift for my mother," she said. "I am glad it is safe."

The other woman checked her clothes and closed her case. She was not pleased, but she accepted Nell's apology.

Mae came back from the bank after the guests went upstairs. Nell told her everything.

"Did you hide the mistake?" Mae asked.

"No."

"Did you check that nothing was lost or broken?"

"Yes."

"Then we can change the system."

Mae found bright labels in a cupboard. From then on, each bag had a guest name and a room number. A second person checked the label before any bag went upstairs.

Nell still felt bad.

"I was too fast," she said.

"You were pulled away in the middle of a job," Mae replied. "Finish one job or leave a clear sign before you start another."

That evening, Clara left a small paper bag at the desk. Inside was a blue glass bead on a string.

"The artist gave me two," she said. "This one is not for your mother."

Nell hung it by her round window. It turned slowly above the new frame.

Chapter 15: Kit's Workshop

Nell went to Kit's workshop on Monday. Mae wanted him to look at an old dining-room chair. One leg was loose.

The workshop stood behind the old cinema. A black sign hung over the door.

MORGAN WOODWORK
REPAIRS AND SMALL ORDERS
OPEN MONDAY-FRIDAY, 8:00-5:00

The door was open. Nell heard a machine, but it stopped before she went inside.

Kit wore clear glasses and a grey shirt. Fine dust covered his shoulders.

"The window woman," he said.

"The important lamp man."

Nell showed him a picture of the chair. Kit looked at it closely.

"I can collect it tomorrow morning. It may only need new glue."

He wrote the details in a large book. Nell saw many simple drawings of tables, doors, and shelves.

"Did you draw these?"

"Yes. A picture helps when a customer and I use different words."

At the back of the workshop stood a small wooden boat. It was not large enough for a person. One side was painted blue.

"What is that?" Nell asked.

"A window display for Tom's flower shop. He wants it before the summer festival."

The boat looked like the boat on Mae's postcard.

"Do all boats in Morrowbay look like that?"

"No. This one is from an old record cover. Tom chose it because people here know the picture."

Kit found the record on a shelf. The cover showed a blue boat under a yellow moon. Four young people stood at the edge of the water. Mae was one of them.

The band name, Low Tide, was at the top.

"Have you heard it?" Nell asked.

"The record has only two songs. Mae does not sing on them."

"She wrote another song."

Kit looked at her. "Did she tell you that?"

"Yes. Someone found an old tape."

Nell wished she had not spoken. The story was Mae's, not hers.

"I should not talk about it," she said.

Kit put the record back on the shelf. "Then I will not ask."

He made tea in a tiny room beside the workshop. They drank it outside in the sun. People walked past with shopping bags. A bus stopped at the corner.

"How long will you stay in Morrowbay?" Kit asked.

"My job has three more weeks. After that, I do not know."

"Farah hopes you will take the room."

"What do you hope?"

Kit looked into his cup. "I hope the dining chair only needs glue."

Nell laughed, but his ears became red.

Chapter 16: Farah's Birthday

Farah's birthday was on Thursday. She did not want a large party.

"Six people, soup, and cake," she told Nell. "No balloons. I do not trust balloons."

Nell asked Mae if she could finish early. Mae said yes. She also gave Nell a bottle of apple drink for the table.

At five, Nell went to Harbour Kitchen. Farah was still at work. The cafe was closed, but she had flour on both hands.

"The cake is not ready," Farah said. "This is bad for a person who makes cakes."

"Can I help?"

"You can carry these boxes to the flat. Keep the small one flat."

Nell carried two food boxes along Gull Street. At the flat, Kit was hanging paper lights above the round table. Tom from the flower shop brought a bunch of red flowers. A woman called Rosa came with three bowls of soup. She was Kit's older sister.

"Farah says no balloons," Rosa said. "She said nothing about twelve paper birds."

They put paper birds in the kitchen and hall.

Farah arrived at seven with the cake. It was round and white, with small red stars on top.

"You made your own birthday cake," Nell said.

"I know what I like."

They ate soup and bread. Farah opened her gifts. Rosa gave her a scarf. Tom gave her a plant in a blue pot. Kit gave her a box for sewing thread. Nell gave her four old spoons from Second Morning.

Farah held them up. "Now you cannot move in. You have no spoons."

"That was my plan. I will visit for dinner forever."

After cake, they played music. Farah chose songs from her phone. Kit moved the chairs, and Rosa danced with Tom.

Nell stood by the window. She had known these people for less than a month. They did not feel like strangers now.

Farah came to stand beside her.

"Mae will keep you," she said.

"She has not decided."

"Mae decides slowly. Then she moves quickly."

At ten, the others left. Nell helped Farah clean the table.

"The room is yours if you want it," Farah said. "I do not need an answer tonight, but I want you to know."

Nell touched the back of one chair. She thought of her father's offer in Dunley. It was a safe job in a place she knew. This room was only a possible home in a new town.

"Thank you," she said. "I want it. I only need the work to go with it."

Farah put the four spoons in a drawer.

"They can wait for you," she said.

Chapter 17: The Late Ferry

The last ferry from Bell Island usually arrived at eight fifteen. On Friday night, strong wind made it late.

Four Lantern House guests were on it. Mae watched the harbour website on her computer.

TRAVEL UPDATE - 8:05 P.M.
Bell Island ferry delayed.
New arrival time: 10:10 p.m.
Passengers should remain in the indoor waiting area.

"They will be cold and hungry," Mae said.

The kitchen was closed. Nell found bread, cheese, tomatoes, and a pot of soup. Mae called the ferry office and left a message for the guests.

At nine, the wind hit the front windows. Rain ran down the glass. Robin sat in the hall with a book, but he read the same page for a long time.

"Were you expecting someone?" Nell asked.

"One of the guests is Leon Price."

Nell put down a pile of towels. "The man with the tape?"

Robin nodded. "He booked a room this morning. He wants to speak to Mae."

Mae came out of the kitchen. She had heard him.

"Why did no one tell me?"

"He asked me not to," Robin said. "He was afraid you would tell him not to come."

Mae looked angry. "You both make choices for me. I do not like it."

Robin stood up. "You are right. I am sorry."

At ten twenty, a taxi stopped outside. Nell opened the door. Four wet people came in with bags. The last one was the thin man in the brown coat.

Leon Price looked at Mae.

"Hello," he said.

"Hello, Leon. Your room is ready."

No one spoke about the tape.

Nell served hot soup in the dining room. The other guests talked about the rough journey. Leon sat at a table near the door. Mae brought him tea.

"I should have waited under the clock," he said.

"Yes," Mae replied.

"I did not know what was in the box when I sent the card. I only knew your name was on it."

"Did you open it?"

"No."

Mae sat across from him. "My name was on many things in those days. It did not always mean they came back to me."

Leon looked down at his hands. "My brother was not an easy man. I loved him, but I know that."

The wind made the old house shake. Mae and Leon stayed at the table. Nell went to the kitchen and closed the door. Their talk was private.

At midnight, Leon went upstairs. Mae found Nell washing the last soup bowls.

"Thank you for waiting," Mae said.

"Are you all right?"

Mae thought for a moment. "Not yet. But we are speaking now."

Chapter 18: Dinner by Candlelight

The wind continued on Saturday. At four in the afternoon, all the lights went out.

The computer screen became black. The kitchen fan stopped. Somewhere upstairs, a guest called, "Hello?"

Mae found two battery lamps in a cupboard. Nell checked the hall and stairs. Light came in through the windows, but the upper hall was dark.

Mae put a notice by the front desk.

POWER CUT IN BEACON LANE
The electricity company is working nearby.
Please ask at the desk for a lamp.
Dinner will be served at 6:30 as planned.

"Can we make dinner?" Nell asked.

"The cooker uses gas. We can make soup and warm bread. The fridge must stay closed."

Leon offered to help move tables. Robin found a box of candles, but Mae used them only in the dining room, away from the curtains. Each table had one candle in a deep glass jar.

At six thirty, thirteen people sat down. The room was gold and dark. Outside, the rain hit the windows.

Farah arrived with two large cakes from Harbour Kitchen.

"The cafe closed early," she said. "These cannot stay there tonight."

Kit came five minutes later. He had checked on his sister and workshop. Both were fine.

"I saw the lights here," he said.

"We have no lights," Nell replied.

"I saw the candles."

Mae gave them bowls of soup. Farah and Kit sat at the end of Robin's table.

After dinner, Robin saw an old piano in the corner. It was usually covered with books.

"Does it work?" he asked.

"Some of it," Mae said.

Robin played a simple tune with one hand. Several keys made no sound, but the tune was clear. Leon listened from across the room.

"That is Mae's song," he said.

Robin stopped.

Mae stood by the window. "You remember it?"

"My brother played it at home for years," Leon said. "He never played it for other people."

Mae sat at the piano. She pressed four notes. Then she played the tune from the beginning. Robin joined with his left hand.

There were no words. The music rose and fell like small waves. The guests became quiet.

Nell watched Mae's face. She did not look nineteen. She looked like herself, in her own dining room, playing a song that had returned.

When the music ended, the electric lights came on. Everyone blinked. Then they began to clap.

Mae laughed and covered her eyes with one hand.

"The candles were better," Robin said.

Chapter 19: The Sunday Visitor

Iris Chen arrived on Sunday at eleven. She had Mae's eyes and a fast way of walking. She carried a black case and a bunch of yellow flowers.

Nell was ready for her. She stopped Mae in the kitchen.

"There is a guest at the door for you."

"At this time? Rooms are not ready."

Mae went into the hall. Iris stepped out from behind the desk.

"Surprise."

Mae put both hands over her mouth. Then she hugged her daughter.

"You said you had work all weekend," she said.

"The work starts tomorrow. I came early."

Iris was a stage manager. She worked with theatres in different towns. Her black case held a laptop, papers, tape, and many pens.

At lunch, Mae told her about the power cut and Leon's visit. Iris knew about Low Tide, but she had never heard the lost song.

"Will you get the box today?" she asked.

Mae shook her head. "Tomorrow. Leon is coming with me."

"I can stay another day."

"No. You have work. I will be fine."

Iris did not look happy, but she did not argue.

In the afternoon, she helped Nell at the desk. Three people arrived together for two rooms. Iris checked their forms quickly.

"Mum says your job is temporary," she said when the guests went upstairs.

"It is."

"Do you want to stay?"

"Yes."

Iris looked around the hall. "This house needs another person. Mum tries to do everything."

"She said she will decide next week."

"That means she has decided. She is only looking for a reason to say no."

Nell smiled. "Farah said almost the same thing."

"Farah knows her well."

Before dinner, Iris opened her laptop at the kitchen table. She showed Mae a room list for a theatre company.

SEA GLASS THEATRE - AUTUMN COMPANY
Arrival: 8 September
Departure: 3 November
Rooms needed: 7
Breakfast: 14 people

"We need a place for actors and crew," Iris said. "Lantern House is near the theatre."

Mae read the list. "Fourteen breakfasts for eight weeks?"

"Not every day. They travel on Mondays."

Mae looked at Nell. The autumn booking could bring regular work after summer.

"Send me the full plan," she told Iris. "No promises yet."

Iris smiled. "That also means she has decided."

Chapter 20: The Small Key

On Monday morning, Leon waited in the hall. Mae wore her coat and held the small key from his envelope.

"Seabird Storage opens at nine," she said. "We will walk."

Robin wanted to go, but Mae asked him to stay. This time, the choice was hers.

Before she left, she gave Nell the desk keys.

"I will be back before lunch. Call me if there is a real problem."

"What is a real problem?"

"Water where water should not be. Fire. A missing wall. For small problems, use your good sense."

The morning was quiet. Nell checked out two guests. She took a booking for July. She showed a couple the ground-floor room.

At eleven, a man came to repair the coffee pot. At eleven fifteen, he asked for payment. Nell opened the desk drawer to find the invoice book.

A small brass key lay under it.

It looked like the key Mae had taken to the storage unit.

Nell picked it up. A paper label was tied to it. The writing was old.

LOW TIDE - MUSIC CUPBOARD

The old piano stood beside a narrow cupboard in the dining room. Nell had never seen it open. She took the key to the kitchen, but she did not try it.

The key and cupboard were part of Mae's story. Nell put the key in a clean envelope. She wrote FOUND IN DESK DRAWER on the front.

Mae and Leon returned at twelve. They carried a dusty green box between them.

"We found more than one tape," Mae said. "There are letters too."

Nell gave her the envelope.

Mae read the label. "I forgot this cupboard had a key."

They went into the dining room. Mae opened the narrow door. Inside were music books, a small tape player, and a yellow scarf. There was also an empty space large enough for the green box.

Leon touched the scarf. "My brother wore this at the summer hall."

"He left it after our last show," Mae said.

They put the box in the cupboard, but Mae did not close the door.

"Nell," she said, "thank you for not opening this alone."

"It was not mine to open."

Mae looked at the desk key in Nell's hand. "You understand this house. That matters more than knowing every answer on your first day."

She took a folded paper from her pocket and gave it to Nell.

LANTERN HOUSE
Permanent assistant, 32 hours each week
Start date: 1 May
Room available until 31 May

Nell read it twice.

"Is this a job offer?"

"Yes," Mae said. "If you still want it."

Nell thought of Farah's round table and the four spoons in the drawer.

"I do," she said.

Chapter 21: A Room Above the Bank

Nell took the job paper to Farah's flat that evening. Farah read it at the round table.

"Thirty-two hours," she said. "That is a real job."

"It was a real job before. It only had an end date."

"You know what I mean. Now you can take the room."

The spare room still had an empty bookcase. Farah had put a clean green cover on the bed. A small plant stood on the window ledge.

"I thought you left all the plants to me," Nell said.

"This one is easy. It needs water when the soil is dry. If it becomes sad, put it in my room."

They sat down with paper and a pen. Farah had written the costs clearly.

MONTHLY RENT: GBP 490
WATER: included
INTERNET: GBP 15 each
HEAT AND ELECTRICITY: shared bill
DEPOSIT: GBP 300
MOVE-IN DATE: 10 May

"We should both sign a simple agreement," Farah said. "My aunt owns the flat. She will send it tomorrow."

"Can I pay the deposit on Friday?"

"Yes. Keep a copy of the payment. My aunt will give you a receipt."

Nell looked around. The kitchen was full of Farah's things, but there was space for hers. There was an empty shelf above the sink. Two hooks by the door had nothing on them.

"I do not own much," Nell said.

"Good. I own too much. Together, we own the right amount."

Kit arrived with a wooden box. It was the same size as the empty shelf beside Nell's future bed.

"I had some spare wood," he said. "Farah said you needed a box for books."

Nell ran her hand over the smooth top. "I have six books."

"It can hold other things until you buy more."

Farah put the red cup inside it. "The collection has begun."

They ate rice and vegetables for dinner. Kit had to leave early because he was making new shelves for the bookshop.

At the door, he turned to Nell.

"So you are staying?"

"Yes."

His smile was quiet but wide. "Good."

After he left, Farah looked at Nell.

"He made that box on Sunday," she said. "Mae did not offer you the job until today."

"Perhaps he has a lot of spare wood."

"Perhaps."

Nell put her copy of the room costs in her bag. She had a job and a place to live. The two things were only words on paper, but they made a new shape for her life.

Chapter 22: Peter Comes to Town

Nell's father arrived on Saturday morning. He did not tell her he was coming until his train was near Morrowbay.

His message said, ARRIVE 10:42. I CAN STAY UNTIL SIX.

Nell met him at the station. Peter Carter wore a dark coat and carried no bag.

"You came for one day?" Nell asked.

"I want to see this place."

They walked down the hill. Peter looked at the small shops and the harbour.

"It is busy with visitors now," he said. "What is it like in winter?"

"I do not know yet."

"That is my point."

At Lantern House, Mae welcomed him. She showed him the dining room and kitchen. Peter asked many questions about Nell's hours and pay.

Mae answered each one. "Nell will work four mornings and two afternoons. Summer is busy. Autumn will bring a theatre company. Winter is quieter, but we have regular guests."

"Can you promise work for a year?" Peter asked.

"I have offered a permanent job," Mae said. "No small business can promise that nothing will change."

Peter pressed his lips together. Nell knew that look. He wanted a stronger answer.

At lunch, Robin joined them. He told Peter that he had stayed at Lantern House for twelve springs.

"Mae runs a careful house," Robin said. "Your daughter makes it better."

Peter looked at Nell. "She has been here for one month."

"A month can show a great deal," Robin replied.

After lunch, Nell took Peter to Farah's flat. Farah was at work, but she had left the key with Nell. The rooms were clean. The street outside was quiet.

Peter checked the window lock and the smoke alarm. Then he sat at the round table.

"You have decided," he said.

"Yes."

"Before you called me."

"It is my decision, Dad."

He looked hurt. Nell sat across from him.

"I know you worry," she said. "The old shop was safe, but I was not happy there. I want to try this."

"Your mother and I made safe choices for you."

"I know. I am thankful. But I am twenty-six now."

Peter looked through the window at the roofs. "Your mother thinks I push too hard."

"She is right."

He almost smiled. "She often is."

Before his train, they walked by the harbour. Peter bought two paper cups of tea. At the station, he gave Nell a small envelope. Inside was an old photograph of Nell and Jamie as children at the sea.

"Your first visit to Morrowbay," he said. "You were five."

Nell did not remember the day, but she knew the clock behind them in the picture.

"Perhaps this town is not as new as I thought," she said.

Peter hugged her. "Call your mother. A real call."

Chapter 23: The Green Box

The green box stayed in the music cupboard for one week. Mae opened the cupboard each day, but she did not touch the tapes.

On Sunday evening, there were no new guests. The dining room was empty. Mae put the box on one table.

"I want to look now," she said.

Robin and Leon sat with her. Iris joined them on a video call from Redwater. Nell was in the kitchen, but Mae asked her to come in.

"You found the key," Mae said. "You can stay."

The box held seven music tapes, a book of song words, photographs, and letters. Each tape had a date. Some had names.

LOW TIDE - PRACTICE - JUNE
SUMMER HALL - 14 AUGUST
MAE'S NEW SONG - FIRST TRY

Mae picked up the last one. "This is it."

The old tape player made a hard click. For a few seconds, there was only a low sound. Then a guitar began.

The sound was not clear. A chair moved in the room on the tape. Someone laughed. A young man's voice counted, "One, two, three, four."

Mae's younger voice began to sing.

Nell did not know the words. She heard the sea, a late bus, and a light in a window. The song was about a person coming home. It was simple and warm.

After one minute, the tape stopped.

"That cannot be all," Robin said.

Leon turned the tape over. The other side was empty. They tried again, but the same short part played.

Mae looked in the box. "There was a full recording. We made it at the summer hall."

Leon opened the song book. Several pages were missing from the middle.

"My brother may have kept them somewhere else," he said. "I found only this box."

Mae touched the tape label. "This is enough for tonight."

She did not sound angry. She sounded tired.

Iris spoke from the laptop. "Mum, the part we heard was beautiful."

"It was unfinished."

"Many beautiful things are unfinished."

Mae smiled at the screen. "That sounds like something a theatre person says when the set is late."

They put the tapes back in the box. Mae kept the one-minute recording outside. Robin offered to take it to a music shop that could make a digital copy.

"Only with Mae's name on it," Leon said. "That is important."

Mae looked at him. "Yes. It is."

Nell closed the music cupboard at the end of the night. The small key turned easily. This time, everyone knew what was behind the door.

Chapter 24: The Quiet Guest

A woman called Ruth Bell came to Lantern House in May. She booked Room Six for five nights. She was writing a book and wanted a quiet place.

"I work in the morning," she told Nell. "Please do not clean my room before two."

Nell wrote the time in large letters on the room sheet.

Ruth came down for breakfast at seven every day. She drank tea and ate one piece of toast. She did not speak much. Then she worked at the desk in her room.

On Wednesday, a new family arrived in Room Five. They had two small children. The children were happy and loud. They ran in the hall and laughed on the stairs.

At ten, Ruth came to the desk.

"I asked for quiet," she said.

"I am sorry. The family arrived today. I can speak to them."

"Children do not become silent because you speak to them."

Nell knew this was true. She checked the room list. Room Nine was empty. It was at the back of the house, away from the family.

"Would you like to move to Room Nine?" she asked. "It is smaller, but it looks over the garden. There is a desk."

Ruth wanted to see it. Nell took her upstairs. The room was clean and bright. A tree stood outside the window.

"I will move," Ruth said. "Can I have help with my case at two?"

"Yes. I will move it with you."

Nell also spoke to the family. She did not ask the children to be silent all day. She asked the parents to keep the upper hall quiet before eight in the morning and after nine at night. The parents agreed.

That afternoon, Nell helped Ruth move. Ruth's case was full of papers. A smaller bag held three books and a little clock.

"What kind of book are you writing?" Nell asked.

"A story about two sisters and a closed railway station."

"Does it end well?"

Ruth looked at her. "I have not decided."

On Friday, Ruth worked in the garden. The children from Room Five played near the tree. They built a house from small sticks. Ruth watched them for a while.

At dinner, she gave Nell a note.

Room Nine is exactly right. Thank you for finding a quiet answer without making the house quiet for everyone.

Nell put the note in the desk drawer. Mae kept kind notes there. On difficult mornings, she read one.

Chapter 25: Moving Day

Nell moved out of the attic on 10 May. She packed her clothes in the blue bag. Her six books went into a fruit box from the kitchen. The blue lamp, red cup, and glass bead went into another box.

Mae stood at the bottom of the attic stairs.

"You can leave things here until this evening," she said.

"Farah is coming with a car at eleven."

Farah did not own a car. The car belonged to Rosa, Kit's sister. It was small and bright orange.

At eleven ten, the orange car stopped outside. Farah got out of the front. Kit climbed out of the back.

"Why are you in the back?" Nell asked.

"The front seat is full of paper birds," he said.

Farah had forgotten to take the birthday birds out of the car. They moved them onto the back seat and made space for Nell's boxes.

Everything fit in one journey. Nell carried the blue lamp up to the flat. Kit carried the wooden book box. Farah carried the plant.

Mae came with them. She brought a long, thin package.

"This was in the attic before you arrived," she said. "It belongs in a home, not a store room."

Inside was a picture of Morrowbay harbour at night. A small gold light shone in every window.

They hung it above Nell's bed. Kit measured the place, but Farah told him the wall did not need a meeting first.

"Pictures can move," she said.

"Holes do not," he replied.

At one, they ate sandwiches at the round table. Nell's red cup stood beside Farah's yellow one. Mae looked around the flat.

"You will be happy here," she said.

"How do you know?"

"The kitchen has good light."

After lunch, Mae returned to Lantern House. Rosa needed her car, so Farah left too. Kit stayed to fix a loose handle on Nell's door.

When he finished, they sat on the floor in Nell's room. The chairs were still in the kitchen.

"Do you miss the attic view?" he asked.

"A little. But I do not miss three flights of stairs at six in the morning."

"You can see the clock from this window."

Nell looked. Between two roofs, she could see the top of the Market Square clock.

"That is enough of the town for one room," she said.

Kit stood to leave. At the door, he gave her a small wooden hook.

"For one of the empty places by the front door," he said.

Nell hung her coat on it after he left. Farah's coat was on the next hook. The flat no longer looked like a place she might live. It looked like home.

Chapter 26: A Day Without Mae

Mae took a full day off in the middle of May. It was the first time since Nell arrived.

"Iris is coming to Bell Island," Mae said. "We are having lunch. I will be back tonight."

Nell and Rosa would run Lantern House. Rosa often helped Mae in summer. She knew the rooms and the kitchen.

"Call only if you need me," Mae said.

"We will not call," Rosa replied. "Go."

The morning began well. Breakfast was quiet. Three guests left, and one new guest arrived before lunch.

At twelve, a pipe under the kitchen sink began to drip. Nell put a bowl under it and called the plumber Mae used. The plumber could come at four.

At one, the card machine stopped working. Nell put a sign at the desk.

CARD MACHINE TEMPORARILY UNAVAILABLE
Cash and bank transfer accepted.
Receipts provided.

At two, a guest said his key did not open Room Four. Nell tried it. The key was for Room Five. She had given him the wrong one at check-in.

"I am sorry," she said. "Here is the correct key."

She checked the key board. No one had entered Room Five, and its guest had not arrived yet.

At three, the smoke alarm in the hall made one short sound every minute. Rosa changed its battery. The sound stopped.

"Do you still think we will not call Mae?" Nell asked.

"Nothing here needs Mae," Rosa said. "It only needs time."

At four, the plumber fixed the pipe. At four thirty, the card machine began to work again. At five, the Room Five guest arrived and got the right key.

By six, the house was calm.

Mae returned at eight with a box of sweets from Bell Island.

"How was the day?" she asked.

Nell and Rosa looked at each other.

"Quiet," Rosa said.

Nell told Mae about the pipe, machine, key, and alarm. She showed her the plumber's bill and the card payments from later in the day.

Mae checked each paper.

"You could have called me," she said.

"We did not need you," Nell replied.

Mae smiled. "That is a very good thing to hear."

She gave Nell and Rosa the sweets. Then she went to her flat and closed the door. A day away had made her look younger.

Chapter 27: The Wedding List

In June, a wedding party booked all nine rooms. The wedding was at Morrowbay Hall on Saturday. Twenty people would stay at Lantern House for two nights.

The bride, Sophie Bell, sent a long room list. Nell printed it and read every line.

ROOM 1: Mrs Bell
ROOM 2: Sophie Bell and Anna Brook
ROOM 3: Mr and Mrs Cole
ROOM 4: Dan Bell
ROOM 5: Lily Ames and baby
GROUND-FLOOR ROOM: Nora Ames

There were more names below. Some guests wanted early breakfast. Some needed a taxi. One child needed a small bed.

Nell made a clear page for each room. Mae checked it. Then Nell called Sophie.

"I have one question," she said. "Your list puts Nora Ames in the ground-floor room. Is that because she needs a room without stairs?"

"Yes. She walks with a stick. Thank you for checking."

On Friday, flowers, bags, hats, and suit covers filled the hall. Farah brought the wedding cake to Morrowbay Hall, not Lantern House. She stopped at the guesthouse on her way back.

"The cake is safe," she told Sophie. "The top flowers will go on tomorrow morning."

Sophie looked close to tears. "Thank you. I have asked everyone about that cake five times."

"It is a good cake. It does not mind."

At six, Sophie could not find a small cloth bag. It held two silver hair clips from her grandmother.

Nell and Mae checked the hall and Room Two. Farah looked in her empty cake boxes. The bag was not there.

"I had it in the taxi," Sophie said. "I think I put it beside me."

Nell called the taxi office. The driver checked the back seat. He found a small cloth bag under the front seat and brought it back.

Sophie opened it at the desk. Both silver clips were inside.

"I thought I had lost them," she said.

"Now they can stay at the desk until morning," Mae said. She put the bag in a locked drawer and gave Sophie a receipt.

The next day, everyone ate breakfast at different times. There were seven kinds of clothing in the hall and no empty chairs. Nell helped call taxis in groups.

At ten thirty, Sophie came down in a simple blue dress. The silver clips shone in her hair.

"You look wonderful," Mrs Bell said.

Sophie looked at Nell, Mae, and Farah. "I also feel ready."

After the last taxi left, Lantern House was silent. Twenty cups stood in the kitchen.

"Tea first," Mae said. "Then the cups."

Chapter 28: The Glass Bird

Clara Dean returned to Lantern House in June. She had stayed there when Nell put her red case in the wrong room.

This time, Clara carried a strong black box.

"No glass gifts in soft cases," she said.

She was in Morrowbay for an art fair. She made small birds, fish, and flowers from coloured glass. Her stand was in the summer hall.

Nell visited the fair on Sunday with Farah and Kit. Sun came through the high windows. It made bright shapes on the floor.

Clara's table held twenty glass birds. No two were the same. One was blue and green, like deep water. One was white, with a red beak.

"Your bead is from this blue glass," Clara told Nell.

Kit picked up a small bird with gold wings. "How does it stand on such thin legs?"

"Very carefully," Clara said.

At the next table, Tom sold plants in painted pots. Farah bought a small herb plant for the kitchen. Kit did not buy anything. He said he had no safe shelf for glass.

Near the door, a poster showed the summer events.

MORROWBAY SUMMER HALL
June: Art Fair
July: Food Weekend
August: Night of Music
Tickets from the hall desk

Mae's old band had played in this building. Nell imagined her on the stage at nineteen.

Robin was sitting near the stage. He came to the fair every year. Mae had said she was too busy, but Nell saw her come through the door at two.

Mae walked slowly to the stage. Robin joined her. They looked at the empty piano.

"The room feels smaller," Mae said.

"We were smaller," Robin replied.

Leon Price came in behind them. He held an old photograph in a clear cover. It showed Low Tide on the same stage.

"I found this in a different box," he said. "I thought you should have it."

Mae accepted the picture. "Thank you."

They stood together without anger. They were not close friends, but they were no longer people on two sides of a closed door.

Before Nell left, Clara gave her a paper package.

"This bird has one wing lower than the other," she said. "I cannot sell it, but it still catches light."

Inside was the blue and green bird. Nell tried to pay, but Clara said no.

At the flat, Kit put up a narrow wooden shelf. He fixed it well above the round table. Nell placed the glass bird there.

"Now you have a safe shelf," she said.

"It is Farah's shelf."

Farah looked at him. "You made it this morning."

Kit's ears became red again.

Chapter 29: A Job in Redwater

Kit came to Lantern House on a hot Tuesday. He needed to speak to Mae about new shelves, but he waited until Nell finished at the desk.

They walked to the harbour. People sat outside cafes. Small boats moved in the bright water.

Kit bought two lemon drinks. He did not drink his.

"I have been offered a job in Redwater," he said.

Nell looked at him. "A new job?"

"A six-month order. An old theatre needs new doors and wall panels. I would work there from Monday to Friday."

Redwater was two hours away by train. Iris worked there often. It was a much larger town.

"Will you take it?" Nell asked.

"I have not decided. It is good work. It could lead to other theatre work."

"It sounds important."

"It is also far away."

Nell waited. Kit looked at the water.

"Farah thinks I should go," he said.

"Farah is often right."

"What do you think?"

Nell wanted to say, Stay here. She wanted Tuesday dinners and walks by the harbour. But she remembered how she felt when her father told her to return to Dunley.

"I think you want to take it," she said. "Being far away does not make it wrong."

Kit nodded. "I can come home at weekends. My workshop will stay here. Rosa can check it."

"Then it is not goodbye."

"No."

They walked to the end of the harbour wall. A red boat passed them. Wind moved Nell's hair across her face.

"There is another thing," Kit said.

"Another job?"

"No. I wanted to ask you to have dinner with me. Just you and me. Before I go."

Nell smiled. "We have dinner together every Tuesday."

"Farah is always there. Sometimes Rosa and Tom are there. Once there were twelve paper birds."

"Do the birds stop it from being dinner?"

Kit finally drank some of his lemon drink. "You know what I mean."

Nell did know.

"Yes," she said. "I will have dinner with you."

Kit smiled, but he still looked worried.

"Take the Redwater job," Nell added. "We can have dinner before you go and after you come back."

Chapter 30: Jamie's Weekend

Nell's brother Jamie came to Morrowbay in July. He arrived on Friday with a large sports bag and oil on one hand.

"You came straight from work," Nell said.

"The bus left ten minutes after my shift."

Jamie was twenty-three. He repaired cars at a garage in Dunley. He liked old engines and loud music. He did not like planning trips.

Farah's sofa became his bed. On Friday night, they ate at Harbour Kitchen. On Saturday, Nell showed him the guesthouse.

Jamie met Mae in the kitchen. A food mixer was making a strange sound. He listened to it for five seconds.

"The bowl is not sitting straight," he said.

Mae moved the bowl. The sound stopped.

"You can visit again," she told him.

Jamie looked at the key board and old desk. "Nell talks about this place all the time."

"Do I?" Nell asked.

"Every call. Mae said this. Robin did that. Kit fixed a window. Farah made bread."

Farah came through the back door with a basket of rolls.

"Now you know all of us," she said.

Kit took Jamie to see the workshop in the afternoon. Jamie liked the machines and old tools. Kit showed him the wooden boat for Tom's window.

"I could make a small motor for it," Jamie said.

"Tom wants flowers in it, not a race."

"Flowers can move."

They spent an hour making a slow turning base for the boat. Kit would finish it after work.

That evening, Jamie and Nell sat on the beach. The stones were warm from the day.

"Dad says you will come home before winter," Jamie said.

"Dad says many things."

"He misses you. He does not say that part."

Nell picked up a flat stone. "I miss all of you too. I can miss home and stay here."

Jamie nodded. "I know."

He told her that the garage might close. The owner wanted to sell the land. No final choice had been made.

"What will you do?" Nell asked.

"I do not know. Maybe find another garage. Maybe learn something new."

Nell saw the same fear that she had felt six months before.

"You can come here if you need a bed," she said. "Farah may ask for rent after three nights."

"I can fix the mixer again."

"It is not broken now."

"I will find something."

On Sunday morning, Jamie came to Lantern House for breakfast. He sat at the end of the kitchen table while Nell cut fruit. Mae gave him an extra piece of toast.

"A guest in Room Seven has a car problem," Mae said. "I told him you work with cars, but you are here for a holiday. You do not have to look."

Jamie went to the front of the house with the guest. He did not touch the car. He listened while the man explained the noise. Then Jamie looked at the paper from the car service company.

"They are already sending someone," he said. "It is better to wait for them."

The guest thanked him and went back inside. Jamie washed his hands, although they were still clean.

"You wanted to open the car," Nell said.

"Very much. But it is not my garage, and I do not have my tools."

After breakfast, they walked to the market. Jamie bought a small model bus for their father. Peter collected buses from towns he visited. He did not have one from Morrowbay.

"He will say it is the wrong colour," Jamie said.

"Then he will put it on his shelf."

They chose a postcard for their mother. The picture showed the harbour on a sunny day. Real clouds covered the harbour behind them.

Nell wrote, JAMIE AND I ARE BOTH HERE. WE ATE TOO MUCH BREAKFAST. LOVE FROM MORROWBAY.

Jamie added, NELL ATE MORE.

"That is not true," Nell said.

"It is on a postcard now. It must be true."

At noon, Farah finished work and met them by the clock. She brought three warm rolls in a paper bag. Kit joined them from the workshop. The four of them sat on a low wall in the square.

Jamie watched Kit and Nell pass the paper bag between them.

"Dad did not tell me about this," he said.

"About bread?" Nell asked.

"About Kit."

Kit looked at the town clock. "My workshop opens in five minutes."

"It is Sunday," Farah said. "Your workshop is closed."

"I may open it."

Nell gave Jamie the last roll. "There is nothing to tell Dad. Kit and I are friends."

"Friends who both become red," Jamie said.

Kit stood up. "I should check the turning boat."

Farah pulled him back down by his jacket. "The boat can turn without you."

Jamie liked Farah at once. Before his bus, he gave her money for the food and for sleeping on the sofa. She accepted the food money but not the rest.

"Next time, you can repair something," she said.

"I will bring tools."

At the bus stop, Jamie hugged Nell. "You are different here."

"Different in a bad way?"

"No. You talk to everyone. At home, you went to work and came back."

The bus doors opened. Jamie climbed on and found a seat by the window. He held up the model bus for Nell to see.

When the real bus left, Nell walked back through town. She thought about her old days in Dunley. Jamie was right. She had made her life very small there. Morrowbay was not a large town, but her days in it were wide.

Chapter 31: The Missing Name

Mae asked Nell to check the August bookings. Every room was full for the Night of Music at the summer hall.

Nell read the names and payment notes. On Saturday, Room Eight had a payment but no guest name. The line said only TWO PEOPLE - THREE NIGHTS.

"Do you remember this booking?" Nell asked.

Mae did not. The payment had come by bank transfer. Its note was NIGHT MUSIC 8.

Nell checked emails from the same date. She found a message from an address she did not know.

Hello Mae,
As agreed, I have sent the first payment for Room Eight. We will arrive on Friday after six. Please keep our visit private for now.
L

"The first letter may be Leon," Nell said.

Mae shook her head. "Leon lives in town. He does not need a room."

"Could it be someone from Low Tide?"

Mae looked at the old record photograph. Low Tide had four people: Mae, Leon's brother Simon, a drummer called June Bell, and a singer called Ellis Hart.

June Bell lived abroad. Ellis had become a well-known radio presenter under another name. Mae had not spoken to him for more than thirty years.

"Do you have his email?" Nell asked.

"No. Iris may. She met him at a theatre event."

Iris checked the address and called Mae back.

"It is his assistant's address," she said. "Ellis is coming to Morrowbay for the music night. He may want a quiet visit."

Mae became pale. "He could have used his name."

"Perhaps he thought you would say no," Iris replied.

"That is not a good reason."

Mae wrote to the email address.

Hello,
Lantern House can keep guest details private, but we need the full names of all guests before arrival. Please also confirm that both guests know about the booking. Without this information, we cannot hold Room Eight.
Mae Chen

The answer came an hour later. Ellis Hart would come with his wife, Mara. They both knew about the booking. Ellis wanted to speak to Mae about the old music before the public event.

Mae added both names to the book.

"Will you let him stay?" Nell asked.

"Yes. A clear name changes things."

She looked at the August date. "But I will choose when we speak. He will not arrive with a surprise meeting under a clock."

Chapter 32: The Boat in the Window

Tom's flower shop had a new window for the summer festival. Kit's blue wooden boat stood in the middle. Jamie's slow motor made it turn. Yellow flowers filled the boat.

People stopped to look. Some knew the Low Tide record cover. Others only liked the moving boat.

Tom put a small card in the window.

BLUE BOAT MADE BY KIT MORGAN
TURNING BASE MADE BY JAMIE CARTER
FLOWERS GROWN AT LARK FARM

Jamie saw a picture of the window online. He sent it to Nell with five happy faces. Nell showed Mae.

"Your brother has his first Morrowbay job," Mae said.

At noon, Kit came to Lantern House. It was his last day before the Redwater work began.

"My train leaves at six tomorrow morning," he told Nell. "I found a room near the theatre."

"Will you be back on Friday?"

"Late Friday. We can have dinner on Saturday if you are free."

Nell checked the work list. She was free after four.

"Yes."

They walked to the flower shop after work. The blue boat turned behind the glass. Mae's younger face was on a copy of the record beside it.

"Does she like the window?" Kit asked.

"She says it is too blue. That means yes."

They bought ice cream and sat in Market Square. The clock showed five fifteen. Buses came and went. No one waited with a secret postcard.

Kit gave Nell a folded sheet of paper. It was his Redwater address, train times, and work phone number.

"Farah already has this," he said. "Rosa does too."

"Do you think you will become lost?"

"No. I do not want people to wonder where I am."

Nell understood. The paper was not only travel information. It was a promise that he would stay part of their days.

"I will send you pictures of bad doors," she said.

"Only doors?"

"Windows too."

Kit laughed. Then the town clock rang the half hour.

At the station the next morning, Farah, Rosa, and Nell stood with Kit. Rosa gave him food for the train. Farah gave him a paper bird from her birthday.

Nell gave him the blue glass bead from her window. It was on a short string.

"For your new room," she said.

"I thought this was important."

"It is. Bring it back."

The train left at six. Nell watched until its red lights were gone.

Chapter 33: A Letter for Robin

Robin Vale went home at the end of July. He usually stayed until June, but this year he had remained longer because of the tapes.

His home was in Eastmere, three hours away. His sister lived near him. He promised to return for the Night of Music.

On his last morning, Nell made strong coffee. Mae put yellow flowers on his table.

"Room Three will miss your newspaper," Nell said.

"Room Three will enjoy the quiet."

Robin left one book in the sitting room. His name was inside it. Nell found it after his taxi had gone.

She sent him a message, but he did not answer. Mae put the book behind the desk.

Two days later, a letter arrived for Robin at Lantern House. The writing was small and careful. There was no return address.

Nell called him. This time, he answered.

"A letter came for you," she said. "Would you like us to send it to Eastmere?"

Robin was silent.

"What does the writing look like?" he asked.

Nell described it. She did not open the letter.

"Please put it inside my book," Robin said. "Keep both at the desk. I will collect them in August."

"Are you sure?"

"Yes. It can wait."

Mae heard the call. When it ended, she looked at the letter.

"I know that writing," she said. "It belongs to June Bell."

June was the old drummer from Low Tide. She had not answered Mae's messages about the tape.

"Why did she write to Robin?" Nell asked.

"He was our teacher. We all trusted him."

Mae wanted to know what the letter said, but she did not ask Nell to open it. They put it inside Robin's book and closed the desk drawer.

In August, Robin arrived one day before the music event. He opened the letter alone in Room Three. Later, he came to the kitchen.

"June will not come," he said. "But she sent a copy of the missing song pages. She kept them all these years."

He placed four pages on the table. Mae's name was at the top. Below it were the words and music for the full song.

At the bottom, June had written one line.

This was always yours, Mae. I am sorry I stayed quiet.

Chapter 34: Night of Music

The summer hall was full on Saturday night. People came to hear local singers and bands. Farah helped behind the stage. Iris managed the event. Kit came back from Redwater on the late train.

Mae did not plan to perform. She sat with Nell, Robin, Leon, and Ellis Hart near the side of the hall.

Ellis was older now, with white hair and round glasses. His wife, Mara, held his hand. He had spoken to Mae that morning at Lantern House.

"I used your tune in another song years later," he had told her. "Only four notes, but I knew where they came from. I should have asked."

Mae had listened. She did not forgive him at once. She asked him to put the truth in writing. Ellis agreed.

Now he went onto the stage alone. The room became quiet.

"Many years ago, I played in a Morrowbay band called Low Tide," he said. "Our best writer was Mae Chen. Tonight, I want to give her name back to a song that always carried it."

He did not sing Mae's song. He only told the truth. Then he left the stage.

The next act was late. Iris came to Mae's seat and bent down.

"We have five empty minutes," she said. "The piano is ready."

Mae shook her head. "No."

Robin touched the music pages in his pocket. "You do not have to sing. We can play it together."

Mae looked at the stage. Then she looked at Nell.

"Will it sound old?" she asked.

"It is old," Nell said. "That is not the same as bad."

Mae stood up.

Robin sat at the piano. Mae took a guitar from a stand. She checked it with Farah's help. Then the two of them played.

The full song was longer than the tape. The tune moved past the late bus and the lighted window. It reached the person at the door. It ended with a quiet sound like a boat touching land.

Mae did not sing. A guitar carried the main line. Robin's piano held it gently.

When they finished, the hall was silent for one breath. Then everyone clapped.

Nell saw tears on Leon's face. Ellis stood with the other people. He did not try to join Mae on stage.

Afterwards, Mae came down the steps. She looked tired and bright.

"Five empty minutes," she said to Iris. "That is all you gave me."

"You used four."

Mae laughed and hugged her daughter.

Chapter 35: The Morning After

Lantern House went to bed late after the music night. Breakfast still began at seven.

Nell's alarm rang at six. She wanted to throw it across the room. Farah was asleep on the sofa because her own bed held two visiting cousins.

Nell dressed quietly and walked through the empty streets. Paper cups and event cards lay near the summer hall. Workers were already cleaning the square.

Mae was in the kitchen when Nell arrived. She had made coffee but not turned on the radio.

"How do you feel?" Nell asked.

"Like a person who played music after midnight and now has to cook eggs."

Robin came down first. He wore his yellow scarf.

"Strong coffee," he said. "Strong enough to wake the whole coast."

"The coast was awake all night," Nell replied.

Ellis and Mara sat at a table near the garden. Leon chose a different table. No one wanted a difficult talk before coffee.

At eight thirty, a woman came to the front door. She worked for the Morrowbay News. She wanted to speak to Mae about Low Tide.

"Not during breakfast," Nell said. "You can leave your name and work number. Mae will decide if she wants to call."

The woman gave her a card. She did not enter the house.

Mae thanked Nell later. "I may speak to her, but not today."

At ten, the last event guests checked out. Ellis gave Mae a signed letter. It said that she had written the tune and that he had used part of it without asking. It also promised that future information about his later song would name her.

Mae read the letter and put it in the green box.

"Is it finished now?" Nell asked.

Mae looked at the box. "The argument is finished. Memory is never finished."

Leon left a little later. He and Mae shook hands at the door.

Robin stayed one more night. He slept in a chair in the garden for most of the afternoon.

At three, Mae closed the front desk for one hour. She and Nell sat on the back steps with tea.

"Last night was not how I dreamed it when I was nineteen," Mae said.

"Was it worse?"

"No. It was mine. That made it better."

They listened to the town become quiet again.

Chapter 36: An Autumn Plan

Iris returned to Lantern House with the theatre plan. The Sea Glass Theatre would begin work in September. Actors and crew would stay for eight weeks.

She spread room lists across the dining-room table.

"The main company has twelve people," she said. "Seven will stay here. Five have flats. Two more people will visit for one week."

Mae checked the dates. "We already have guests in Rooms Two and Six on the first weekend."

"The theatre company can start with five rooms."

Nell read the breakfast notes. Some people left early. One actor did not eat dairy food. The stage carpenter needed a place for one large case.

"The case can go in the back room," Nell said. "Not in the hall."

Iris nodded. "Good."

The company was bringing a new play called The Clockmaker's Daughter. They would practise at the theatre for six weeks, then give twelve shows.

"I need someone to help with travel and room changes," Iris said. "Only a few hours each week. Nell knows the house and the town."

Mae looked at Nell. "Do you want more work?"

"What would I do?"

"Meet late trains, carry messages between the house and theatre, and keep the room list correct," Iris said. "There may be small surprises."

"Theatre people use the word small in a strange way," Mae said.

Nell wanted to try. The work was new, but it used things she had learned at Lantern House.

"I can work two afternoons," she said. "Not during breakfast."

Mae and Iris agreed. Iris wrote Nell's name on the company sheet.

NELL CARTER - HOUSE CONTACT AND LOCAL ASSISTANT

Nell read the line. Six months ago, she had put boxes on shelves in a Dunley shop. Now a theatre company was putting her name in its plan.

That evening, she told Kit on the phone. He was in his room in Redwater. The blue glass bead hung near his window.

"I may see your theatre before you do," he said.

"I have seen the outside."

"The inside is where all the difficult doors are."

"Then I will send pictures."

They talked until the last train from Redwater reached Morrowbay. Kit was not on it that night, but his voice made the distance feel smaller.

Chapter 37: The Empty Chair

In late August, the dining-room chair came back from Kit's workshop. He had fixed the loose leg before going to Redwater. Rosa brought it in her orange car.

"Kit forgot to send the bill," she said.

Mae looked at the chair. "That is not new."

The chair was strong again. Its wood shone. Nell put it at Table Four.

That evening, Mae found an old mark under the seat. Four names were written there in faded ink: Mae, Simon, June, Ellis.

"This chair came from the summer hall," she said. "We took it after the hall bought new ones. Low Tide wrote our names under it."

Nell touched the old letters. "Did Kit see them?"

"He must have. He cleaned around them."

Mae called him. "You kept the writing," she said.

Nell could hear Kit's voice through the phone. "It was part of the chair."

Mae paid the bill the next day. Then she placed the chair near the piano instead of at a table.

"Why is it here?" Robin asked when he saw it.

"For people who listen," Mae said.

Robin sat in it and played the first line of her song. The chair did not move.

In September, Robin went home. Leon stopped visiting every day. Ellis and Mara sent a card from their own town. The green box stayed in the locked cupboard.

Lantern House became quiet for one week before the theatre company arrived. Some rooms were empty. The dining room had only four guests at breakfast.

Nell sat in the old chair after work. Mae was making a shopping list.

"Do you miss everyone?" Nell asked.

"I miss some people. I miss the noise less."

"The theatre company will bring noise."

"That is why I am enjoying today."

They heard a car stop outside. Iris came in with two boxes of company papers and a broken umbrella.

"The first actor arrives tomorrow, not Monday," she said. "His flat has no water."

Mae looked at Nell. "The quiet week is over."

Nell stood up from the old chair. It stayed by the piano, ready for the next person who needed to listen.

Chapter 38: Kit Comes Home

Kit came home every second weekend. The Redwater theatre work was going well, but the days were long.

On his first weekend back, he and Nell had dinner alone. They chose a small place near the harbour. It had six tables and a menu on a black wall.

Kit arrived early. He wore a clean blue shirt. Nell had never seen it before.

"Is that a Redwater shirt?" she asked.

"Rosa bought it. She said my grey shirts all look like work."

They ordered fish pie and peas. For a few minutes, both talked too much. Nell told him about every guest. Kit described three theatre doors.

Then they became quiet.

"This feels different from Tuesday dinner," Nell said.

"There are no paper birds."

"That must be it."

Kit smiled. "I missed you."

Nell put down her fork. "I missed you too."

After dinner, they walked along the harbour. The night was warm. Music came from an open cafe door.

Kit stopped near a row of small boats.

"I may get more work in Redwater after this order," he said. "I do not know yet."

"Do you want it?"

"Some of it. I want to work on theatres. I also want my own workshop here."

"Can you do both?"

"Perhaps. Not every week forever."

Nell understood the problem. A person could want two lives at the same time.

"We do not need to decide tonight," she said.

"No."

They reached the end of the harbour wall. Kit took her hand. His fingers were rough from work.

Nell looked at him. "Is this also different because there are no paper birds?"

"Farah told me not to make jokes at this part."

"Farah knows about this part?"

"Farah knows everything."

Nell laughed. Then Kit kissed her. It was a short, careful kiss. A sea bird made a loud sound above them.

"That bird does not like me," Kit said.

"It wants your fish pie."

They walked back slowly, still holding hands. At the flat, Farah did not ask any questions. She only looked at Nell's face and put another cup on the table.

Chapter 39: Seven New Guests

The theatre company arrived on a wet Monday. Seven people came to Lantern House with eleven cases, three boxes, and one long bag.

Iris stood in the hall with her room list. Nell checked each name.

Sami Hale, an actor, had Room Two. Lydia Frost had Room Three. Owen Marsh had Room Four. The director, Casper Wynn, had Room Seven.

The long bag belonged to the stage carpenter, Dev Cole. It held pieces of a wooden clock. Nell put it in the back room as planned.

One name was not on the list. A young man called Benji Rowe stood by the door with a green case.

"I joined the company on Friday," he said. "I am the new sound assistant."

Iris checked her phone. The company office had sent a message at midnight, but she had not seen it.

Lantern House had no free room.

"Benji can use my room tonight," Iris said. "I will sleep in Mum's flat."

"Your room is in Redwater," Mae said.

"I booked Room Nine for myself."

Mae looked at the new list. "You did not tell me."

"It was in the Friday message."

For a moment, mother and daughter stared at each other. Then Nell took a blank room card.

"Benji can have Room Nine," she said. "Iris can use my old attic room. The bed is ready for late guests."

Mae nodded. "That works for two nights. Then we have another booking."

Iris called a nearby flat owner. A room would be free on Wednesday. Benji could move there.

The guests carried their cases upstairs. Casper left his hat on the desk. Lydia asked for hot water. Dev checked the long clock bag twice.

At seven, the company ate soup in the dining room. They talked about the first practice at Sea Glass Theatre. Nell listened to names she would soon know well.

Sami held up his room key. "Does the front door stay open late?"

"No," Nell said. "Your room key also opens the front door. Please close it behind you."

Owen looked at the old piano. "Does anyone play?"

Mae was carrying bread. "Sometimes."

After dinner, Iris took a folder from her case. She gave Nell a copy of the week's plan.

Tuesday: first full practice
Wednesday: costume delivery
Thursday: company photographs
Friday: clock test

"It should be a calm first week," Iris said.

Mae and Nell looked at each other. Neither of them believed her.

Chapter 40: The Light in the Window

Nell woke before her alarm on Tuesday. Rain touched the flat window. Farah was already at Harbour Kitchen, and the rooms were quiet.

Nell made tea in her red cup. She looked at the picture above her bed. Every painted window in Morrowbay had a small gold light.

Six months had passed since her train arrived. She knew the way from the station now. She knew which market stall sold the best apples. She knew the sound of Lantern House at six in the morning.

She also knew that knowing a place did not make every day easy.

At Lantern House, Lydia wanted breakfast early. Sami had lost his room key in the theatre. Benji needed to move to his new room after work. The wooden clock had to go to Sea Glass Theatre before noon.

Nell wrote each job on a page. Then she began with coffee.

At ten, she and Dev carried the long clock bag to the theatre. They used a small company van. Iris met them at the stage door.

Inside, the theatre was dark and cool. Rows of red seats faced an empty stage. Work lights hung high above it.

"Put the clock by the left wall," Iris said. "Casper wants it for the first scene."

Nell helped Dev open the bag. The pieces made a tall wooden clock with a round face. Its hands pointed to six.

"It does not move yet," Dev said. "Kit Morgan is making the last part in Redwater."

Nell smiled. Their work had met before they did.

Iris gave her another sheet. "The costume delivery is late. It will arrive at the station at five. Can you meet it?"

"Yes."

"Thank you. I knew you would be useful."

Nell walked back to Lantern House for the lunch shift. Mae was at the desk. A copy of the Low Tide music lay beside her.

"Iris has given you more work," Mae said.

"Only a station collection."

"Nothing is only a station collection in theatre."

Nell laughed. "I will remember that."

At five, the train arrived with two costume boxes and a tired driver. Nell checked both labels before she signed for them. She took the boxes to the theatre.

At seven, Kit called from Redwater. At eight, Farah came home with warm bread. At nine, Nell stood at her bedroom window.

Across the roofs, she could see the top of the town clock. Farther away, Lantern House stood near the dark sea. Its brass lamp shone above the blue door.

Someone opened the attic curtain. A new guest turned on the light in Nell's old room.

Down in Beacon Lane, the blue front door opened. Sami and Owen came out with their theatre papers. They stopped under the brass lamp and tried to read the next day's plan. Mae came to the door and pointed them towards the square. Even from far away, Nell knew the shape of her hand in the air.

A bus moved past the end of the street. The light from its windows ran over the wet road. Nell remembered her first walk from the station, with one bad case wheel and rain in her face. She had needed a flower seller to show her the way. Now she could walk from the station to Lantern House in the dark. She knew every turn.

Farah called from the kitchen. "Tea is ready."

Nell opened her bedroom door. The blue and green glass bird shone on its shelf. Her coat hung beside Farah's coat in the hall. Two plates waited on the round table, and a message from Kit lay beside her red cup.

She looked through the window once more. The guest in the attic closed the curtain, but the lamp above the front door stayed on.

The window looked like one small gold square in the night. Nell thought of Mae's song. A late traveller saw a light and knew where to go.

Nell was not a traveller now. She was part of the light.
